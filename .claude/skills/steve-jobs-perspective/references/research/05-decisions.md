# Steve Jobs — Major Decisions Catalog

Research Agent 5 (Decisions). Tagging convention: **[PRIMARY]** = Jobs's own words (interviews, keynotes, internal memos quoted in primary sources), **[SECONDARY]** = biographer/journalist reconstruction (Isaacson 2011, Sculley 1987, Young/Simon 2005, Kahney, Schlender/Tetzeli 2015, Hertzfeld), **[INFERENCE]** = analyst reading by this agent.

---

## 1976 — Co-founding Apple, the $666.66 Apple I, and the Atari/HP detour

**Context.** Jobs (21) was a part-time Atari technician with a phone-phreaking past and a recent India trip; Wozniak (25) worked at HP and had designed a one-board computer for the Homebrew Computer Club. Wozniak had already offered the design to HP five times; HP declined because it was a hobbyist machine with no obvious enterprise market. [SECONDARY: Wozniak, *iWoz*, 2006]

**Options.** (a) Let Woz publish the schematics for free, as Homebrew norms demanded. (b) Sell finished boards to hobbyists. (c) Sell only as a kit. (d) Jobs's choice: form a partnership, sell *assembled* boards through The Byte Shop (Paul Terrell), and treat it as a real company.

**What he chose.** Incorporated Apple Computer with Wozniak and Ron Wayne on April 1, 1976. Sold Apple I assembled at **$666.66** to Terrell's Byte Shop (50 units, ~$500 BOM). [PRIMARY: Apple I price list]

**Stated rationale.** Jobs reportedly liked repeating digits; Woz said it was just $500 cost + 1/3 markup that rounded oddly. [SECONDARY: Wozniak] Jobs's deeper rationale: "You've got to *sell* them. Otherwise it's a hobby." [SECONDARY: Isaacson, paraphrase]

**Post-hoc reflection.** Jobs later framed Apple's founding as the moment he turned Woz's engineering gift into a business: "Woz designed a great computer, but it would be sitting in hobby stores today if it weren't for me." [SECONDARY: Isaacson, ch. 5]

**Gap between words and actions.** Jobs publicly praised the Homebrew sharing ethic while privately closing the hardware. The proprietary instinct was present from day one — contested by some Homebrew veterans who recall Jobs taking schematics but giving little back.

## 1979–80 — Xerox PARC: the GUI bet (and what he ignored)

**Context.** Apple II was a hit; Jobs was scouting the next platform. Xerox's venture arm wanted to invest pre-IPO. Jobs traded ~$1M of pre-IPO Apple stock (Xerox bought 100,000 shares at $10.50) for two demos at PARC. [SECONDARY: Hiltzik, *Dealers of Lightning*]

**Options.** (a) License PARC's full Smalltalk/Alto stack including networking (Ethernet), object-oriented programming, and laser printing. (b) Take only what was demoed and reverse-engineer. (c) Walk away — Xerox already had the Star coming.

**What he chose.** Jobs latched onto the **bitmapped GUI and mouse**. He ignored (or didn't grasp at the time) Ethernet and Smalltalk's OOP. He hired key PARC researchers (Larry Tesler, Bruce Horn) and redirected the Lisa and Mac teams to copy what he saw. [SECONDARY: Hertzfeld, *Revolution in the Valley*]

**Stated rationale.** "Within ten minutes, it was obvious to me that all computers would work like this someday." [PRIMARY: *Triumph of the Nerds*, 1996] On the ethics: quoting Picasso, "Good artists copy, great artists steal." [PRIMARY: same interview]

**Post-hoc reflection.** Jobs publicly maintained Xerox "fumbled the future." Privately he conceded he missed the networking story — Apple was late to TCP/IP and to OOP (which only landed inside Apple via NeXTSTEP a decade later). [INFERENCE]

**Gap.** He framed it as theft justified by superior execution. Tesler and others insist Apple *did* pay (in stock) and *did* hire — not pure theft. The mythology was useful to Jobs; the record is more complicated. [Contested.]

## 1985 — The Sculley showdown and the Mac-vs-Apple-II civil war

**Context.** Mac launched Jan 1984 with the "1984" ad; sales cratered by Christmas (no hard drive, 128K RAM, no software). The Apple II group resented the Mac team's "pirates" arrogance and consumed most of revenue. Jobs (chairman, Mac division GM) tried to slash Apple II prices and divert resources; Sculley (CEO, recruited by Jobs in 1983) sided with the board.

**Options.** (a) Accept demotion to head of "AppleLabs," a research role with no operating authority. (b) Try to oust Sculley by lobbying the board during Sculley's China trip. (c) Resign and start something new.

**What he chose.** Attempted the coup in May 1985. The board voted unanimously for Sculley. Jobs was stripped of operating duties, kept the chairman title until September, then resigned and announced NeXT.

**Stated rationale.** "What can I say? I hired the wrong guy." [PRIMARY: Stanford 2005] At the time, to the board: he claimed Sculley was destroying Apple's spirit. [SECONDARY: Sculley, *Odyssey*]

**Post-hoc reflection.** Stanford 2005: "Getting fired from Apple was the best thing that could have ever happened to me. The heaviness of being successful was replaced by the lightness of being a beginner again." [PRIMARY]

**Gap.** Jobs publicly forgave the firing as a gift; biographer interviews show he carried resentment for decades and made Sculley a non-person in Apple's official history. [SECONDARY: Isaacson, ch. 16] Words said "gift"; actions said "grudge."

## 1985 — Founding NeXT: workstation, cube, magnesium, $10K

**Context.** Jobs took five Apple senior staff with him (triggering an Apple lawsuit, later dropped). He had ~$7M from selling Apple stock and put in $12M of his own money over time. Target market: higher education research, framed as "the next Mac for the next generation of students."

**Options.** Build a consumer machine and compete with Apple/IBM directly. Build a software company. Build a high-end workstation against Sun/SGI.

**What he chose.** A **workstation** with a **magnesium cube** form factor (1-foot perfect cube), optical magneto-optical drive (no floppy), Mach/UNIX OS, $6,500 base / ~$10,000 configured. Manufactured in a fully automated Fremont factory.

**Stated rationale.** "A computer for the 1990s designed in the 1980s." [PRIMARY: NeXT launch, Oct 1988] On the cube: it had to be a perfect cube because the geometry was honest. Paul Rand was paid $100K for the logo.

**Post-hoc reflection.** Conceded the hardware was a mistake: "We tried to do too much. The cube was beautiful, but it cost too much to make and there weren't enough customers." [SECONDARY: paraphrase, Schlender/Tetzeli] He pivoted to selling NeXTSTEP as software in 1993 and shut hardware down.

**Gap.** Said he wanted to serve students; priced it out of student reach. Said he learned humility from being fired; the cube factory was as grandiose as anything at Apple. The form-factor obsession that worked for iMac/iPod actively hurt NeXT. [INFERENCE]

## 1986 — Buying Pixar from Lucasfilm for $5M (then funding ~$50M more)

**Context.** George Lucas was divorcing and needed cash; his computer graphics division (Ed Catmull, Alvy Ray Smith, John Lasseter) was for sale. Asking price had been $30M; no buyer for years. Ross Perot had bid and walked.

**Options.** Don't buy — Jobs was already bleeding cash on NeXT. Buy and run it as a hardware company (the Pixar Image Computer). Buy and pivot to animation.

**What he chose.** Paid **$5M to Lucas + $5M into the company** = $10M total in Feb 1986. Initially ran Pixar as a hardware/software firm; Lasseter's animated shorts were marketing for the imaging hardware. Killed hardware in 1990; sold imaging division in 1990; kept funding the animation team. Total personal contribution: ~$50–60M by 1995. [SECONDARY: Price, *The Pixar Touch*, 2008]

**Stated rationale.** At purchase: graphics hardware was the future of medical imaging. Later: "If it weren't for Pixar, I would have folded years ago." [PRIMARY: 1996 interview]

**Post-hoc reflection.** Catmull and Lasseter, not Jobs, drove the creative pivot. Jobs's contribution was *patience and capital* — atypical for him. He later said: "Lucky to have been associated with something that good." [SECONDARY]

**Gap.** Jobs publicly took credit for "betting on animation"; the record shows he resisted the animation pivot until the hardware business was clearly dead. [Contested: Catmull's *Creativity, Inc.* is gentler on Jobs than Alvy Ray Smith's accounts.]

## 1995–96 — Pixar IPO (Toy Story) and using the cash for leverage

**Context.** *Toy Story* released Nov 22, 1995; grossed $361M worldwide. Pixar IPO'd one week later (Nov 29, 1995) at $22, closed at $39. Jobs owned 80%; his stake was suddenly worth ~$1.2B.

**Options.** Cash out. Hold and let Disney keep the cut (Pixar got only ~10% of *Toy Story* profits under the 1991 deal). Renegotiate from strength.

**What he chose.** Held, then renegotiated the Disney deal in 1997 to 50/50 on co-financed films. Used the credibility (and the wealth) to negotiate the NeXT sale to Apple later that year from a position of *not needing the money*.

**Stated rationale.** "Pixar was the most important thing in my life for the second half of the 1990s." [PRIMARY: Isaacson interview]

**Post-hoc reflection.** The wealth liberated him to be ruthless at Apple in 1997 — he could take a $1 salary and one share, because Pixar made him a billionaire. [INFERENCE, widely held by biographers]

**Gap.** None notable. This is the cleanest decision in the catalog — he held, he renegotiated, he won.

## 1996 — Selling NeXT to Apple for $429M + stock

**Context.** Apple under Amelio was hunting for a modern OS; Copland was a disaster; finalists were Be (Gassée) and NeXT. Gassée demanded ~$300M; Jobs initially asked less and won. Final deal: $429M cash + 1.5M Apple shares.

**Options.** Sell NeXT to Sun. Sell to SGI. Take Apple's deal. Stay independent and continue dying.

**What he chose.** Apple, December 1996. Returned as "advisor" January 1997. By July 1997, Amelio was out; by September, Jobs was interim CEO ("iCEO").

**Stated rationale.** Publicly: NeXT's tech would save Apple. Privately to Larry Ellison: "How do I take it over?" Ellison: "Buy it." Jobs: "It's not for sale." Ellison: "Then how do you take it over?" [SECONDARY: Isaacson, ch. 22 — Ellison's recollection]

**Post-hoc reflection.** "I didn't go back to Apple to make a fortune. I went back because I love Apple." [PRIMARY: 1998] The succession-by-acquisition was, on his own later admission, the plan from early on. [Contested: he denied it in public for years.]

**Gap.** Major. He said he didn't want the CEO job; he engineered Amelio's removal within six months. Words said "advisor"; actions said "acquirer-via-reverse-merger." [INFERENCE supported by Ellison testimony.]

## 1997 — The Great Killing: Newton, clones, OpenDoc, Cyberdog; the 4-quadrant grid; Microsoft $150M

**Context.** Apple lost $1.04B in fiscal 1997. ~350 products. Mac clone licensees (Power Computing, UMAX) were eating Apple's high-margin sales. Newton was a beloved-by-fans, money-losing PDA division.

**Options.** Cut 20%. Cut 70%. Cut everything that didn't fit a single matrix.

**What he chose.** Drew a **2x2 grid**: Consumer / Pro × Desktop / Portable. Killed everything else. Killed Newton (May 1998, after his return), killed OpenDoc, killed Cyberdog, killed the printer line, killed Mac clones by buying out Power Computing for $100M and refusing to license OS 8.

**The Microsoft deal.** Aug 6, 1997 at Macworld Boston: Bill Gates on the giant screen. Microsoft invested $150M in non-voting Apple shares, committed to 5 more years of Office for Mac, and IE became default browser. In return, Apple dropped a long-running patent suit (QuickTime code in Video for Windows).

**Stated rationale.** "Focusing is about saying no." [PRIMARY: WWDC 1997] "Deciding what *not* to do is as important as deciding what to do." [PRIMARY]

**Post-hoc reflection.** "If we succeed, they have to succeed. But there's been too much of a sense of, for Apple to win, Microsoft has to lose." [PRIMARY: Macworld 1997, on Gates deal]

**Gap.** The Microsoft deal contradicted decades of Jobs's "Microsoft has no taste" rhetoric; he ate the contradiction in public to save the company. Killing Newton was personal — it was Sculley's baby, and several insiders read the kill as score-settling as much as strategy. [Contested.]

## 1998 — iMac: gumdrop, no floppy, USB-only, translucent

**Context.** First product fully on Jobs's watch since return. Apple needed a hit consumer machine. CRT all-in-one was the simple form; Jony Ive's team proposed translucent Bondi Blue plastic.

**Options.** Conventional beige box. Beige all-in-one. Radical color/transparency play.

**What he chose.** **Bondi Blue translucent gumdrop**, no floppy disk, USB only (no ADB, no SCSI), $1,299, launched Aug 15, 1998. Internal engineering revolted over the floppy decision — even Phil Schiller pushed back.

**Stated rationale.** On the floppy: "It's dead. We're not going to lead the industry by following." [PRIMARY: paraphrase] On color: "Most people make beige boxes. The fruit colors gave us permission to be different." [PRIMARY: Isaacson]

**Post-hoc reflection.** iMac sold 800K units in first 5 months; saved Apple. Jobs later said it taught him the lesson: "Skating to where the puck is going." [PRIMARY: borrowed from Gretzky, used repeatedly]

**Gap.** None notable. Both rhetoric and action aligned.

## 2001 — iPod: 1.8" Toshiba drive, FireWire-only, SoundJam/iTunes

**Context.** MP3 players in 2001 were either flash-tiny (32–64MB, ~10 songs) or hard-drive-clunky (Creative Nomad). Jobs had identified music as Apple's "digital hub" anchor in 2000. Toshiba had shopped a 1.8" / 5GB drive that nobody wanted.

**Options.** Build a flash player. License an existing player. Build the hard-drive player at $300+ price.

**What he chose.** **1.8" Toshiba HDD**, 5GB ("1,000 songs in your pocket"), FireWire-only sync, scroll wheel (Phil Schiller's contribution), Mac-only initially. Acquired SoundJam MP from Casady & Greene (early 2000) and turned it into iTunes 1.0 (Jan 2001). iPod shipped Oct 23, 2001 at $399.

**Stated rationale.** "1,000 songs in your pocket" [PRIMARY: launch keynote]. FireWire because USB 1.1 was too slow for a full library. Mac-only because it was an anchor for Mac.

**Post-hoc reflection.** Opening to Windows in 2003 (after internal fights — Jobs initially resisted) was the decision that turned iPod from hit to phenomenon. He told Isaacson he was wrong to resist Windows compatibility for as long as he did. [SECONDARY: Isaacson, ch. 30]

**Gap.** Said "digital hub for Mac"; the strategy only worked once he abandoned Mac exclusivity. He admitted the error privately.

## 2003 — iTunes Music Store: 99¢ and breaking the labels

**Context.** Napster had collapsed (2001); the labels were suing customers. CD sales were dropping; the labels wanted subscription, not à la carte. Jobs had ~3% PC market share — too small to be a threat.

**Options.** Ad-supported model. Subscription. Per-track DRM-locked sales at price labels chose.

**What he chose.** **99¢ per track**, album $9.99, DRM (FairPlay) that allowed 5 machines + unlimited iPods, Mac-only at launch. Sold the labels by promising the Mac's 3% share contained the risk. Universal first, then the rest. Launched April 28, 2003.

**Stated rationale.** "People want to own their music." [PRIMARY: launch keynote] "If you treat them like criminals, they'll act like criminals." [PRIMARY, on DRM-light approach]

**Post-hoc reflection.** 2007 "Thoughts on Music" open letter: called for ending DRM entirely. Labels reluctantly went DRM-free on iTunes by 2009.

**Gap.** Public rhetoric ("anti-piracy") and private negotiating leverage (his small market share, which he sold as a feature) were both true at once. Once iTunes dominated, he reversed on DRM publicly to keep labels from defecting to Amazon MP3.

## 2005 — Intel transition: abandoning PowerPC

**Context.** IBM/Motorola PowerPC roadmap was stalled: no G5 laptop, thermal problems, performance behind Intel's Pentium M / Core. Apple had run a parallel Intel-compatible build of OS X (codenamed "Marklar") in secret since 2001.

**Options.** Stay with PowerPC and hope IBM delivers. Build custom silicon (a decade too early). Switch to Intel and risk a third CPU transition (68K→PPC was the first, Classic→OSX the second).

**What he chose.** Announced at WWDC June 6, 2005. Universal Binaries (fat binaries) and Rosetta translator for back-compat. Completed transition in 14 months (vs. predicted 24).

**Stated rationale.** "We can envision some amazing products we want to build for you, and we don't know how to build them with the future PowerPC roadmap." [PRIMARY: WWDC 2005]

**Post-hoc reflection.** Set the template for the 2020 Apple Silicon transition under Cook — same Universal Binary playbook, same Rosetta name. [INFERENCE]

**Gap.** None notable. Words and actions matched. The secret parallel build for years contradicted earlier public denials of an Intel switch, but Jobs treated denial-then-reveal as standard practice.

## 2007 — iPhone: multi-touch, no keyboard, no Flash, AT&T exclusive, no third-party apps (then yes)

**Context.** Two parallel internal projects: P1 (an iPod-with-phone, scroll wheel) and P2 (a touchscreen tablet shrunk to phone size). Jobs killed P1 in late 2005. Carriers controlled handsets; Jobs hated this and called them "orifices." [PRIMARY]

**Options.** Hardware keyboard like BlackBerry. Resistive touchscreen with stylus. Capacitive multi-touch — never shipped at this scale.

**What he chose.** Multi-touch capacitive screen, virtual keyboard, no Flash, **AT&T (Cingular) US exclusive** for 5 years in exchange for unprecedented carrier concessions (Apple controlled the OS, the icon layout, the revenue share on data). Launched Jan 9, 2007, shipped June 29.

**The App Store reversal.** At launch: web apps only. "The full Safari engine is inside the iPhone. You can write amazing Web 2.0 and AJAX apps." [PRIMARY: WWDC 2007] Within months, internal pressure (Schiller, Forstall) and developer pushback flipped him. App Store launched July 10, 2008.

**Stated rationale at launch.** "We don't want our phones to be like PCs. The last thing you want is to have loaded three apps and your phone doesn't work." [PRIMARY: 2007] On Flash: would come later in 2010 letter.

**Post-hoc reflection.** Jobs admitted to Isaacson he had been "really worried" about third-party apps but Schiller and Forstall convinced him there was a secure way. [SECONDARY: Isaacson, ch. 38] On the AT&T exclusive, no public regret; internally, Schiller and others felt it cost share to Android.

**Gap.** "Closed only" → "Open SDK" reversal in 18 months is the largest visible product-strategy U-turn of his career. He never publicly acknowledged it as a reversal; he framed App Store as the plan all along. [Contested by internal sources.]

## 2008 — MacBook Air: the unibody bet

**Context.** Sony's TZ-series ultraportables were the benchmark. Apple's PowerBook line was thick. Ive's team had been prototyping milled-aluminum unibody construction.

**Options.** Plastic and weight savings. Sandwich aluminum stamping. CNC-milled unibody from solid billet (expensive; needed factory retooling).

**What he chose.** **CNC unibody** in aluminum, no optical drive, no Ethernet port, single USB, custom 1.8" SSD option, pulled out of a manila envelope on Jan 15, 2008 at Macworld.

**Stated rationale.** "The world's thinnest notebook." [PRIMARY] On removing the optical drive: the future is wireless. Re-disc-installation app called Remote Disc.

**Post-hoc reflection.** Initial sales modest; iteration in 2010 (cheaper, smaller 11" model) made it the de facto template for the entire industry's "ultrabook" category.

**Gap.** None notable. The form-factor-first approach was on-brand.

## 2010 — iPad: below netbook price, killing the category

**Context.** Netbooks (Asus Eee, Acer) had hit ~20% of laptop sales by 2009 at $300–500. Microsoft was pushing Tablet PC (stylus, Windows). Apple's tablet project predated iPhone — Jobs had shelved it in 2004 to do iPhone first.

**Options.** Price tablet at premium $1,000+ (in line with MacBook). Match netbook at $399. Undercut.

**What he chose.** **$499 base** (10" Wi-Fi 16GB), announced Jan 27, 2010, shipped April 3. Killed netbook category within 18 months — netbook share collapsed from ~20% to ~2%.

**Stated rationale.** "If there's going to be a third category of device, it's going to have to be better at certain key tasks than a laptop or smartphone." [PRIMARY: launch] On netbooks: "Netbooks aren't better than anything. They're just cheap laptops." [PRIMARY]

**Post-hoc reflection.** Sold 15M in first 9 months; defined a new category. Jobs called it the most important product he'd worked on, more so even than iPhone. [SECONDARY: Isaacson]

**Gap.** None notable. He'd named the threat (netbooks) and priced to kill it — words and actions aligned.

## 2010 — "Thoughts on Flash": public takedown of Adobe

**Context.** Adobe had been pressuring Apple for Flash on iPhone/iPad since 2007. Flash was a battery hog, security-bug factory, and crucially: a cross-platform runtime that would let developers bypass App Store APIs. Adobe was also late shipping Mac products (Photoshop), a personal Jobs grievance going back years.

**Options.** Quiet refusal (status quo). License Flash and ship a degraded version. Public letter.

**What he chose.** Posted **"Thoughts on Flash"** under his own name on apple.com, April 29, 2010. Six points: not "open," not the "full web," reliability/security/performance, battery, touch-unfriendliness, and the killer: "the most important reason" — letting a third party between Apple and developers would slow Apple's platform.

**Stated rationale.** Public-facing was a battery/UX argument. Real argument was point 6: platform control. [PRIMARY: the letter says this explicitly, sixth point — "we cannot accept being at the mercy of a third party"]

**Post-hoc reflection.** Adobe announced end of mobile Flash in Nov 2011. HTML5 became the de facto standard. Jobs won decisively.

**Gap.** He framed it as principle ("open standards"); Apple was simultaneously running the most closed app distribution channel in computing. The contradiction was conscious. [INFERENCE]

## 2003–2011 — Cancer: nine-month delay, alternative medicine, liver transplant, succession

**Context.** Oct 2003: routine kidney scan accidentally revealed a pancreatic neuroendocrine tumor — a *rare, slow-growing, often-curable* type, unlike the more common pancreatic adenocarcinoma. Standard of care: surgery (Whipple procedure) as soon as possible.

**Options.** Surgery immediately. Watch and wait. Alternative therapies.

**What he chose.** Delayed surgery **nine months** while pursuing veganism, juice fasts, acupuncture, herbal remedies, and a psychic he'd known from India days. [SECONDARY: Isaacson, ch. 35] Finally had modified Whipple July 2004. Disclosed publicly only after surgery.

**Liver transplant.** By 2009, cancer had metastasized to liver. Got on transplant lists in multiple states (legal "multi-listing"); transplant in Memphis, Tennessee, April 2009 (shorter wait list than California). Announced as "hormonal imbalance" earlier that year — disclosure was minimal and arguably misleading.

**Stated rationale.** Privately to Isaacson, regretting the delay: "I really didn't want them to open up my body, so I tried to see if a few other things would work." [PRIMARY via Isaacson] On disclosure: investors had no right to his medical record; he treated it as a privacy issue.

**Post-hoc reflection.** "I wish I had done it sooner." [PRIMARY via Isaacson] Most oncologists who've reviewed the case publicly believe the nine-month delay shortened his life — though contested, since neuroendocrine tumors are genuinely variable.

**Succession.** Cook ran Apple during Jobs's 2004, 2009, and 2011 medical leaves — three live trials. Jobs formally resigned Aug 24, 2011, naming Cook CEO. Died Oct 5, 2011.

**Gap.** Massive. The man who preached "trust the experts" on chip design and manufacturing distrusted the experts on his own body. The man who demanded radical disclosure from suppliers stonewalled Apple's own shareholders on his health. [Contested but documented.]

## 2011 — Tim Cook as successor; the Foster + Partners campus

**Context.** Cook had been COO since 2005, had run Apple through three medical leaves, was an operations master (collapsed inventory from months to days), and notably had a *different* personality — quiet, methodical, non-charismatic. Other internal candidates: Forstall (iOS), Mansfield (hardware), Ive (design).

**Options.** Co-CEOs. Forstall (most Jobs-like). Ive (creative heir but didn't want operating role). Cook.

**What he chose.** **Cook, alone, with the explicit instruction: "Don't ask what I would do. Do what's right."** [PRIMARY via Isaacson, ch. 41] Told the board not to recreate him.

**Apple Park.** Jobs spent his last summer (2011) personally walking the Cupertino City Council through the campus design — circular, 2.8M sq ft, the "spaceship," Foster + Partners architect. His final public appearance was the June 7, 2011 Cupertino council meeting.

**Stated rationale.** On Cook: he was the operator who could let Ive be Ive. On the campus: "I think we have a shot at building the best office building in the world." [PRIMARY: Cupertino council, June 2011]

**Post-hoc reflection.** Cook has talked about the "do what's right" instruction as the most important thing Jobs gave him — license to diverge.

**Gap.** Jobs spent his life ensuring nothing happened at Apple without his fingerprints; his succession decision deliberately broke the pattern. Possibly his most self-aware decision. [INFERENCE]

## Patterns Across Decisions

Eight recurring patterns extracted from the catalog above:

### 1. Kill products to clarify focus
1997's matrix (350 → 4 products), killing Newton, killing clones, killing OpenDoc, killing iPod mini for nano, killing Xserve later under Cook (foreshadowed). Subtraction was a strategic tool, not a budget concession. Quote: "Deciding what *not* to do is as important as deciding what to do."

### 2. Pick a hard form-factor or price constraint and let it cascade
Apple I at $666.66 (had to be assembled to justify it). NeXT cube (perfect cube → magnesium → factory automation → $10K → failure). iMac (no floppy → USB-only → translucent). iPod ("1,000 songs in your pocket" → 1.8" HDD → FireWire). MacBook Air (manila envelope thin → unibody → no optical). iPad ($499 → kill netbooks). The constraint upstream forced engineering and supply-chain courage downstream.

### 3. Public denial → secret build → dramatic reveal
Intel transition (denied PowerPC switch for years while Marklar built in secret). iPhone (denied phone ambitions while two parallel teams competed). Tablet (shelved 2004, revived 2009). The reveal was choreographed; the denial was strategy, not honesty.

### 4. Reversals he wouldn't acknowledge as reversals
No-Windows-iPod → iPod-for-Windows. No-third-party-apps → App Store. DRM-required → DRM-free. Each pivot was presented as continuation of the plan. He never said "I was wrong" in public; biographer interviews show he could say it in private. The asymmetry was deliberate brand management.

### 5. Use leverage you have, sell the constraints as features
Microsoft deal (sold dependence as truce). Music labels (sold 3% Mac share as "contained risk"). AT&T (sold exclusivity as carrier concession). NeXT-to-Apple (sold reverse-acquisition as advisory). He could reframe weakness as strategy faster than negotiators could process.

### 6. Form follows feeling, not just function
Bondi Blue, magnesium cube, manila envelope MacBook Air, the iPod's white earbuds as social signal, the iPhone's home-button-as-anchor. Industrial design wasn't decoration; it was the thesis. Engineers who tried to cost-engineer the surface were overruled, sometimes against margin.

### 7. Personalize institutional fights
Sculley (the firing he never forgot). Gassée (Be lost the NeXT-vs-Be bake-off partly because Jobs ran a personal campaign). Adobe (Flash letter as proxy for old Mac grievance). Eric Schmidt/Android ("I'll spend my last breath… I'm going to destroy Android"). Strategic fights were also personal ones; the energy came from grievance.

### 8. Delay disclosure until inevitable — for products and for his own body
Apple-Intel kept secret for 4 years. iPhone hidden until polished. Pancreatic cancer delayed for 9 months (privately) and disguised as a "hormonal imbalance" (publicly) until impossible. The same control-the-narrative reflex that worked for product launches arguably cost him personally. The most dangerous version of his greatest strength.
