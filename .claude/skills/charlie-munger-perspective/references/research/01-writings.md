# Charlie Munger — Beat 1: Writings

Research distilled from Munger's **published speeches, essays, parody letters, Wesco/Daily Journal letters, and book forewords**. Primary anchor: the eleven talks collected in *Poor Charlie's Almanack* (Kaufman, ed., 2005; Stripe Press abridged ed., 2023). Verbatim quotes are in quotation marks with source tags. Paraphrased summaries are unquoted.

---

## 1. Core Arguments That Recur ≥3 Times

### 1.1 "Invert, always invert" (Jacobi's rule)
Munger's single most-repeated heuristic. Treat every hard problem backward: ask not "how do I succeed?" but "how do I fail?", then avoid that.

- Harvard School 1986 commencement: *"The great algebraist, Jacobi, had exactly the same approach as Carson and was known for his constant repetition of one phrase: 'Invert, always invert.' It is in the nature of things, as Jacobi knew, that many hard problems are best solved only when they are addressed backward."* (Talk 1, *Poor Charlie's Almanack*)
- Repeated in "Practical Thought About Practical Thought" (1996): *"It is not enough to think problems through forward. You must also think in reverse… that is why the great algebraist, Carl Jacobi, so often said: 'Invert, always invert.'"* (Talk 4)
- Repeated in "Academic Economics" (UCSB 2003) as one of the antidotes to "man-with-a-hammer syndrome." (Talk 9)
- Repeated throughout *Psychology of Human Misjudgment* (1995/2005 expanded).

### 1.2 Latticework of mental models
The foundational claim of Munger's epistemology: isolated facts are useless; you need a multidisciplinary lattice.

- USC Business School 1994, "A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom": *"You can't really know anything if you just remember isolated facts and try and bang 'em back. If the facts don't hang together on a latticework of theory, you don't have them in a usable form. You've got to have models in your head. And you've got to array your experience — both vicarious and direct — on this latticework of models."* (Talk 2)
- Reiterated in Stanford Law 1996 (Talk 3) and the Harvard Law 1948 50th Reunion address (Talk 5: "The Need for More Multidisciplinary Skills from Professionals").
- Munger usually estimates "you need about 80 or 90 important models" — see USC 1994 and "Practical Thought" 1996.

### 1.3 Man-with-a-hammer syndrome
The pathology that follows from a non-latticework mind.

- USC 1994: *"To the man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail."* (Talk 2)
- "Academic Economics" UCSB 2003: lists *"Fatal Unconnectedness Leading to 'Man-with-a-Hammer Syndrome'"* as Defect #1 of academic economics. (Talk 9)
- *Psychology of Human Misjudgment* — discussed under Twaddle, Authority-Misinfluence, and as a meta-bias driving most of the other 25.

### 1.4 Lollapalooza tendency
Munger's coinage for compound effects: when several psychological tendencies converge on the same outcome, the result is non-linear and extreme.

- *Psychology of Human Misjudgment* (1995, expanded 2005), Tendency #25: *"The tendency to get extreme consequences from confluences of psychological tendencies acting in favor of a particular outcome."* (Talk 11)
- USC 1994 cites Tupperware parties, open-outcry auctions, and Milgram experiments as lollapaloozas. (Talk 2)
- "Academic Economics" 2003: identifies failure to grasp lollapaloozas as a core defect of economics. (Talk 9)

### 1.5 Circle of competence
- USC 1994: *"Every person is going to have a circle of competence. And it's going to be very hard to advance that circle… You have to figure out what your own aptitudes are. If you play games where other people have the aptitudes and you don't, you're going to lose."* (Talk 2)
- Recurs in Wesco shareholder letters (1990s–2000s) and Daily Journal annual meetings.

### 1.6 "Sit on your ass" investing
- *Outstanding Investor Digest* / Worldly Wisdom 1995–1998 compilation: *"There are huge advantages for an individual to get into a position where you make a few great investments and just sit on your ass: You are paying less to brokers. You are listening to less nonsense. And if it works, the governmental tax system gives you an extra 1, 2 or 3 percentage points per annum compounded."*
- Reaffirmed in CNBC interview Nov 14, 2023 and across Berkshire/Wesco meetings — Munger explicitly calls it "sit-on-your-ass investing."
- Connected to "Bet big when you have the odds" — USC 1994: *"The wise ones bet heavily when the world offers them that opportunity. They bet big when they have the odds. And the rest of the time, they don't. It's just that simple."* (Talk 2)

### 1.7 "Deserve what you want" — the iron prescription
- USC Law 2007 commencement: *"The safest way to try and get what you want is to try and deserve what you want."* The talk frames this as Munger's master rule for the conduct of a life. (Talk 10)
- He repeated it at the Daily Journal meetings (2014, 2017, 2019).

### 1.8 Take a simple idea and take it seriously
- Back cover, *Poor Charlie's Almanack* (Stripe Press 2023): *"There is an old two-part rule that often works wonders in business, science, and elsewhere: 1. Take a simple, basic idea and 2. Take it very seriously."*
- Restated at countless Wesco / Daily Journal meetings.

### 1.9 Avoid stupidity rather than seek brilliance
- Wesco shareholder meeting remarks, multiple years: *"It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent."* (Frequently attributed to the 1994 USC talk and Berkshire 2002; recurs throughout Wesco letters.)

### 1.10 The power of incentives
- *Psychology of Human Misjudgment* (1995/2005), Tendency #1, Reward and Punishment Superresponse Tendency: *"Never, ever, think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives."* (Talk 11)
- The famous condensation: *"Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome"* — Munger has used this line in the 1995 Harvard speech and recurrently at Wesco/Daily Journal meetings.
- Munger's FedEx night-shift story (workers paid by shift not by piece, problem solved overnight) appears in the same speech as the textbook illustration.

### 1.11 Iron prescriptions for misery / iron prescription for life
- Harvard School 1986: the speech *is* the iron prescription — invert, then avoid:
  - *"Be unreliable. Do not faithfully do what you have engaged to do."*
  - *"Learn everything you possibly can from your own personal experience, minimizing what you learn vicariously from the good and bad experience of others."*
  - *"Go down and stay down when you get your first, second, third severe reverse in the battle of life."*
  - Borrowing from Carson: ingest chemicals; envy; resentment.
  - Closing toast: *"Gentlemen, may each of you rise high by spending each day of a long life aiming low."* (Talk 1)

### 1.12 Two-track analysis
Always analyze (a) the rational factors governing interests *and* (b) the subconscious psychological factors that distort. Appears explicitly in *Psychology of Human Misjudgment* (1995/2005) and the 1996 Stanford talk.

### 1.13 Checklist routine
Munger insists you must run a literal mental checklist of the models so you don't forget any. USC 1994: *"You should have all this elementary [worldly] wisdom and then you should go through a mental checklist in order to use it. There is no other procedure in the world that will work as well."* (Talk 2)

### 1.14 "I'm not entitled to have an opinion…" (steelman rule)
- USC Law 2007: *"I'm not entitled to have an opinion on this subject unless I can state the arguments against my position better than the people do who are supporting it. I think only when I reach that stage am I qualified to speak."* (Talk 10)

### 1.15 Assiduity
- USC Law 2007: *"Assiduity… I like that word because it means: sit down on your ass until you do it."* (Talk 10)

### 1.16 "Seamless web of deserved trust"
- USC Law 2007: civilization's highest form, in Munger's language, is *"a seamless web of deserved trust."* (Talk 10)

## 2. Coined Terms / Proprietary Jargon

| Term | One-line definition | Primary source |
|---|---|---|
| **Lollapalooza tendency / effect** | Non-linear blow-up when multiple psychological biases converge on the same outcome. | *Psychology of Human Misjudgment*, 1995/2005 (Talk 11) |
| **Man-with-a-hammer syndrome** | If your only tool is one discipline, every problem looks like a nail. | USC 1994 (Talk 2); UCSB 2003 (Talk 9) |
| **Latticework of mental models** | A multidisciplinary mesh of ~80–90 big ideas onto which you hang experience. | USC 1994 (Talk 2) |
| **Sit-on-your-ass investing** | Find a few great businesses, hold forever, save on tax/brokerage/nonsense. | Worldly Wisdom 1995–98; CNBC Nov 2023 |
| **Iron prescription** | An inversion-derived rule: identify what guarantees failure, then refuse it. | Harvard School 1986 (Talk 1) |
| **Two-track analysis** | Analyze the rational interests *and* the subconscious distortions in parallel. | *Psychology of Human Misjudgment* (Talk 11) |
| **Chauffeur knowledge** (vs **Planck knowledge**) | Glib parroting of an expert's lines vs. real, paid-for understanding. | USC Law 2007 (Talk 10) |
| **Deserved trust / seamless web of deserved trust** | The highest civilizational good; the system Berkshire is built on. | USC Law 2007 (Talk 10) |
| **Physics envy** | Economists copying physics' precision where life is messy. *"I call physics envy. And of course, that term has been borrowed from penis envy."* | UCSB 2003 (Talk 9) |
| **Twaddle tendency** | Producing meaningless verbiage that crowds out real thought. | *Psychology of Human Misjudgment* (Talk 11) |
| **Febezzle / functional bezzlement** | Munger's extension of Galbraith's "bezzle" — undiscovered embezzlement that *feels* like wealth while it lasts. | UCSB 2003 (Talk 9) |
| **Deprival-superreaction tendency** | Loss aversion stated in Munger's idiom — strong reaction to taking-away (or near-miss of getting). | *Psychology of Human Misjudgment* (Talk 11) |
| **Kantian fairness tendency** | Humans' built-in expectation of reciprocity/fair dealing. | *Psychology of Human Misjudgment* (Talk 11) |
| **Influence-from-mere-association tendency** | Pavlovian transfer of feeling from one stimulus to a co-occurring one. | *Psychology of Human Misjudgment* (Talk 11) |
| **"Bullshit earnings"** | What you should mentally substitute every time you read "EBITDA." | Attributed in *Poor Charlie's Almanack*; frequently quoted at Wesco and Berkshire meetings: *"Every time you see the word EBITDA, you should substitute the words 'bullshit earnings.'"* |
| **Cocoa-Cola problem / Glotz problem** | Munger's "build Coca-Cola from scratch to $2T" thought experiment. | "Practical Thought About Practical Thought," 1996 (Talk 4) |
| **One-legged man in an ass-kicking contest** | What you are without numerical fluency. | "Practical Thought" 1996 (Talk 4) |

## 3. Books Munger Repeatedly Recommended

Pulled from *Poor Charlie's Almanack* (recommended-reading list, ed. Kaufman) and his speeches. Most-cited first:

- **Robert Cialdini — *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion*** (Munger reportedly gave Cialdini a Class A Berkshire share; *Influence* is cited in nearly every Munger talk on psychology after 1995).
- **Benjamin Franklin — *Autobiography*** (Franklin is Munger's personal hero; cited in Harvard 1986, USC 1994, USC Law 2007).
- **Charles Darwin** — Munger praises Darwin's method of relentlessly seeking disconfirming evidence; cited in *Psychology of Human Misjudgment* and the Harvard Law 1948 reunion speech.
- **Richard Dawkins — *The Selfish Gene* and *The Blind Watchmaker*** (both in PCA recommended list).
- **Jared Diamond — *Guns, Germs, and Steel* and *The Third Chimpanzee***. Munger reread *Guns, Germs, and Steel* — rare for him.
- **Garrett Hardin — *Living within Limits*** (source of "tragedy of the commons" model in Munger's lattice).
- **Ron Chernow — *Titan: The Life of John D. Rockefeller, Sr.***
- **David S. Landes — *The Wealth and Poverty of Nations***.
- **Andrew Carnegie — *Autobiography*** / **Peter Krass — *Andrew Carnegie***.
- **Matt Ridley — *Genome***.
- **Herbert Simon — *Models of My Life***.
- **John Gribbin — *Deep Simplicity***.
- **Roger Fisher / William Ury — *Getting to Yes***.
- **Andrew Grove — *Only the Paranoid Survive***.
- **Frank Partnoy — *F.I.A.S.C.O.***.
- **William Thorndike — *The Outsiders***.
- **Viktor Frankl — *Man's Search for Meaning***.
- **Walter Isaacson — *Einstein: His Life and Universe*** (Munger said he reads "every Einstein biography").

Authors Munger cites by name across multiple speeches: **Cicero, Franklin, Darwin, Galbraith, Skinner, Pavlov, Cialdini, Keynes, Einstein, Planck, Carl Jacobi, Carl Braun, Adam Smith.**

## 4. Recurring Analogies & Rhetorical Moves

- **Jacobi / "Invert, always invert"** — opening or pivot move in at least four talks (1986, 1994, 1996, 2003).
- **Carson's three prescriptions for misery** (chemicals, envy, resentment) — opens Harvard 1986; Munger calls Carson "a brilliant comedian" who *"failed amply"* before figuring it out. (Talk 1)
- **The man with a hammer / every problem looks like a nail** — recurs across USC 1994, UCSB 2003, *Psychology of Human Misjudgment*.
- **Max Planck and his chauffeur** — USC Law 2007: the chauffeur memorized Planck's lecture, gave it once in Munich, fielded a hard question with: *"I'm surprised that in an advanced city like Munich I get such an elementary question. I'm going to ask my chauffeur to reply."* Munger's punchline: *"I think I've just described practically every politician in the United States."* (Talk 10)
- **Galbraith's "bezzle"** — extended to Munger's "febezzle"/functional bezzlement. UCSB 2003 (Talk 9).
- **The Tupperware party / open-outcry auction / Milgram** — canonical lollapalooza examples. USC 1994 (Talk 2); *Psychology of Human Misjudgment*.
- **Coca-Cola / Glotz thought experiment** — extended hypothetical that runs the length of "Practical Thought About Practical Thought," 1996 (Talk 4).
- **"One-legged man in an ass-kicking contest"** — used in "Practical Thought" 1996 to dramatize numerical illiteracy. (Talk 4)
- **Carl Braun's communications rules** (who/what/where/when/why) — invoked in 1994 USC and the 1996 Stanford talk as a model of clear thinking.
- **Ben Franklin's "if you would persuade, appeal to interest and not to reason"** — quoted in USC Law 2007 and used as the launchpad for tendency #1 in *Psychology of Human Misjudgment*.
- **Cicero — *"A man who does not know what happened before he was born goes through life like a child"*** — USC Law 2007 (Talk 10).
- **"Aiming low" closing** — "may each of you rise high by spending each day of a long life aiming low" (Harvard 1986, Talk 1) and the parallel "deserve what you want" close in USC 2007 (Talk 10) function as Munger's signature anti-grandiosity sign-offs.

## 5. Direct Attacks (Recurring Targets)

- **EBITDA**: *"Every time you see the word EBITDA, you should substitute the words 'bullshit earnings.'"* (attributed in *Poor Charlie's Almanack*; quoted at Berkshire and Daily Journal meetings repeatedly).
- **Efficient-Market Hypothesis / Modern Finance Theory**: USC 1994 — *"the efficient market theory is obviously roughly right… [but] partly efficient and partly inefficient. And believe me, if you make it perfectly efficient, you'll go broke."* (Talk 2). UCSB 2003 lists EMH/"hard-form" rational-actor economics among the central defects of academic economics. (Talk 9)
- **Academic economics**: UCSB 2003 lays out ~9 defects — fatal unconnectedness, failure to attribute when borrowing from other disciplines, physics envy, too much macro, failure to synthesize, psychological ignorance, neglect of second-order effects, underweighting of "febezzlement," and underemphasis on virtue/vice effects. (Talk 9)
- **Stock-option accounting / phony earnings**: the entire premise of "The Great Financial Scandal of 2003" (written summer 2000, Talk 8). Quant Tech's hypothetical founder considered stock-option accounting *"weak, corrupt, and contemptible."* The parody predicts (correctly, three years out) the option-backdating wave and the WorldCom/Enron-style implosions.
- **Cryptocurrency**: Daily Journal annual meeting, Feb 2023: *"Sometimes I call it crypto crappo, sometimes I call it crypto sh-t. It's just ridiculous that anybody would buy this stuff."* *"It's asinine, it's not slightly stupid, it's massively stupid, and it's very dangerous."* WSJ op-ed, Feb 2023: ban it, as China did. ("rat poison" earlier in 2013.)
- **Banking compensation / financial-services excess** — recurs in Wesco letters (post-2008) and Daily Journal meetings. He repeatedly attacks bonus structures that reward short-term earnings while loading tail risk on shareholders and taxpayers.
- **BlackRock / ESG**: Daily Journal 2023 — Munger criticized large index houses (BlackRock, State Street, Vanguard) for accumulating proxy power and pushing political agendas they don't fund the consequences of. [unverified exact wording — paraphrase consistent with multiple meeting transcripts].
- **Gold**: characteristic Munger dismissal — civilized people don't buy gold; he repeats this at multiple Berkshire meetings and once linked it to "Jews fleeing Hitler" as the only justifiable use.
- **Politicians / "chauffeur knowledge"**: USC Law 2007 (Talk 10).
