# The Mom-Test Audit

Named for Rob Fitzpatrick's *The Mom Test*: ask questions so concrete that even your mom couldn't lie to
make you feel good. The Playbook's instruction — *"surface what people actually do rather than what they
think they would do"* — is this. Run this audit on **every** interview question: the ones the workers
generate *and* the ones the founder drafts.

## The four red flags

For each question, check all four. If any fires, rewrite.

| Flag | Looks like | Why it produces noise | Rewrite toward |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Future-facing** | "Would you use a tool that…?" "How likely are you to pay for…?" | People predict their future selves optimistically and to be polite. | A concrete past event: "Tell me about the last time you…" |
| **Leading** | "Don't you find it frustrating that…?" "Wouldn't it be better if…?" | Telegraphs the answer you want; they agree to be agreeable. | A neutral opening: "How do you handle … today?" |
| **Too broad** | "How do you keep up with AI?" | Invites a tidy summary of the imagined-typical case, not a real instance. | A bounded, recent instance: "What did you do in the last 7 days to…?" |
| **Socially-desirable** | "How important is staying current to you?" | Everyone says "very" — it's identity-affirming and costless. | A revealed-preference proxy: time spent, money paid, last action taken. |

Worked rewrites:

- ❌ "Would you pay for a personalized AI digest?" → ✅ "What's the last thing you paid for to keep up with AI, and what made you pull out a card?"
- ❌ "Is it annoying to track 20 creators?" → ✅ "Walk me through the last time you sat down to catch up on AI — what did that actually look like?"
- ❌ "How do you stay on top of model releases?" → ✅ "When did you last change how you work because of something a model shipped? How did you hear about it?"

## Past behaviour over stated intention

The single highest-leverage move. Anything answerable by imagining the future is weak; anything answerable
only by recalling a specific past event is strong. When you catch a future-tense or hypothetical verb
("would", "might", "could you see yourself"), rewrite it to interrogate a dated, concrete past instance.

## Deflection probes

Pick the 2–3 moments in each guide most likely to draw a dodge, and pre-write the follow-up that drills
past it. Common dodges and their probes:

- **Generic enthusiasm** ("I'd love that!") → "When did you last go looking for something like it? What did you do instead when you couldn't find it?"
- **Hypothetical willingness** ("I'd pay for the right one") → "What did you actually pay for, most recently, in this area?"
- **The tidy summary** ("I usually just check Twitter") → "Take me to the most recent specific time — what day, what did you open first?"
- **Compliment** ("That's a great idea") → "What's the hardest part about this for you today?" (move off the idea, onto their problem)

## Multi-persona

If the hypothesis names several personas, each gets its own guide — a finance manager and a CFO relate
to the same problem differently, and one flattened guide loses that. The anchoring (`kill-criteria-anchoring.md`)
stays constant across personas; the *framing* and the *vocabulary* shift per persona.

## Auditing the founder's own drafts (the Playbook exercise)

When the founder hands you questions they drafted, run the same four-flag audit, name each flagged
question and *why* it's flagged, propose the rewrite, and add a deflection probe for the two or three
spots most likely to generate a dodge. Don't silently replace their questions — show the flag and the
fix so they build the judgment (they'll reuse it on every future idea).
