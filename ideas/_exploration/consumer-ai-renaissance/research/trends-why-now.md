# Trends / Why-Now — Findings

## Key findings

- **Token costs fell ~98% in under 3 years** — GPT-4-class output tokens dropped from ~$60/M (March 2023) to ~$0.40–$1/M by 2026; DeepSeek's December 2024 pricing ($0.27/$1.10/M) forced OpenAI and Google to respond, pushing mid-tier capable models to commodity pricing. A 10-minute AI-assisted session that cost $1–2 in inference two years ago now costs fractions of a cent, making the $200/hr professional vs. pennies-per-interaction margin structure arithmetically real today rather than theoretical.

- **Real-time voice AI crossed the demo-to-deployable threshold in 2025** — OpenAI's GPT Realtime API (launched August 2025), co-developed with LiveKit, delivers sub-250ms speech-to-speech latency in a single multimodal model (no STT+LLM+TTS pipeline). Open-source alternatives (Qwen3-Omni, Pipecat, LiveKit agents) let indie builders deploy sub-200ms voice agents on modest infrastructure. This enables the conversational, emotionally-present experience consumer services require — therapy, coaching, concierge — not just chatbots.

- **Stripe's Agentic Commerce Suite (2025) closes the last friction gap for AI-completed purchases** — Shared Payment Tokens (SPTs) let AI agents execute purchases using a user's saved payment method without exposing credentials, scoped per-seller with time/amount bounds. ChatGPT's Instant Checkout (powering Etsy/Shopify transactions) went live in 2025. This means an AI concierge can now research, book, and pay end-to-end — the agentic payment rail didn't exist at scale 18 months ago.

- **Consumer AI monetization remains a 3% conversion problem — but that's the opportunity signal** — Menlo Ventures' 2025 State of Consumer AI report puts the market at $12B across 1.8B users, with only 3% converting to paid. At $20/month potential, the addressable monetization gap is ~$432B annually. Only 9% of consumers pay for more than one AI subscription (a16z). The implication: consumers will pay, but only for focused, outcome-specific experiences — not general chatbots. Life-event services (wedding, relocation, job search) are inherently outcome-specific and time-bounded, structurally better suited to capture willingness to pay than subscription chat tools.

- **80% of AI non-adopters prefer a human touch — the human-in-the-loop bridge is a real wedge** — Menlo Ventures data: 80% of consumers who haven't adopted AI tools say they prefer interacting with people over machines. Named company using this explicitly: Jobbatical (immigration/relocation) runs AI analytics + human expert review and reports a 95% visa success rate across 30+ countries. Duckbill (personal assistant) is explicitly described as "human-in-the-loop" and appears on Forerunner's curated consumer AI list. The pattern is: AI handles research/drafting/logistics, a human expert reviews or is on-call — consumers pay the premium for quality assurance they can't yet fully trust to the AI alone.

- **Wedding is the #1 life-event vertical by spend + stress signal** — 2025 data: average US wedding costs $34,200; 86% of couples report stress symptoms during planning; 84% are stressed about costs; 43% say planning strains their relationship. A professional wedding planner costs ~$2,400 (just 4% of budget) yet the average couple still finds the coordination overwhelming. The $62.7B US wedding services market (growing ~6% CAGR to 2033) represents dense, time-bounded, high-willingness-to-pay demand. Critically, couples already pay hundreds of dollars to multiple vendors — an AI coordinator bundling vendor research, timeline management, and communication is a clear value-capture point.

- **Immigration/relocation is an undercrowded, high-anxiety vertical with existing willingness to pay $5,000+** — Immigration lawyers charge $3,000–$10,000+ per case; Jenova.ai explicitly markets against "$5,000+ immigration consultant" fees. Startups getting traction here: NextCountry (legal-grade AI matching + concierge setup), Jobbatical (visa + relocation AI+human hybrid), Visas.AI, OpenSphere (O-1A/EB-1A). Immigration tech funding picked up in 2025 — described as "the year AI got funded" in the space. The regulatory complexity + emotional stakes naturally preserve a human-in-the-loop requirement, which blocks full commoditization.

- **Synthesis ($30M a16z Series A, 1.2M subscribers) validates AI tutoring as a consumer category a16z will write checks for** — Synthesis, originally SpaceX-employee children's tutoring, raised a Series A led by a16z and reached 1.2M active subscribers. Khanmigo grew from 68K (2023–24) to 1.4M users by mid-2025. The AI 1-on-1 tutoring segment commanded $5.8B of a $15B AI tutoring market in 2025. This is proof that highly focused, outcome-specific AI learning products (not generic chat) convert consumers to paid. Subject-specific or exam-specific (SAT, IELTS, coding interviews) niches remain open.

- **AI mental health hit a ceiling from the B2C side — Woebot's consumer pivot failure is an anti-signal** — Woebot raised $124M and had 1.5M users but shut down its consumer app and pivoted to B2B health systems. The global AI mental health app market is projected at $2.1B in 2025 but adoption is only 9–20% in consumer surveys despite high need. Lesson: mental health / therapy verticals face trust, liability, and reimbursement barriers that make the pure consumer path hard without clinical credentialing infrastructure. Adjacent emotional support (coaching, life coaching, breakup support) is more viable for solo builders.

- **a16z identifies dedicated consumer experiences as the primary white space vs. general assistants** — a16z's State of Consumer AI 2025 explicitly states model companies "don't have the intuition or attention and resources to be innovating outside their areas of core competency" — citing Replit, Gamma, Suno, Character AI, Eleven Labs as breakout cases. The smart money is not funding new general assistants; it is backing vertically focused, opinionated products. Marissa Mayer's Dazzle AI (personal assistant) raised $8M seed at $35M post from Forerunner + Kleiner Perkins in December 2025 — signal that consumer AI personal assistance is still early-stage enough for seed, not yet crowded at the product layer.
