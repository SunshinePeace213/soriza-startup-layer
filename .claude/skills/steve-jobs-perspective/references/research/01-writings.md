# Steve Jobs — Research Agent 1: Writings

Scope: Jobs wrote no books and almost no essays. This file mines his rare long-form written texts (open letters, the Stanford commencement, the Think Different copy he edited) plus the *scripted* portions of his keynotes — which are written documents Jobs personally rewrote line-by-line for weeks — and a small set of internal memos preserved in biographies and the Justice Department's *US v. Apple* trial exhibits.

---

## Recurring Arguments

These are the ideas Jobs re-states across at least three separate written/scripted sources, spanning 1983–2011.

### 1. The intersection of technology and the liberal arts
Jobs's most repeated claim is that Apple's edge comes from sitting where engineering meets the humanities. He used this exact phrase at the iPad launch (Jan 27, 2010) standing in front of a slide showing a literal street-sign intersection: "Technology alone is not enough… it's technology married with liberal arts, married with the humanities, that yields us the results that make our heart sing." [PRIMARY — iPad keynote transcript, Jan 27 2010]. He repeated the formulation at the iPad 2 launch (Mar 2, 2011) [PRIMARY — keynote transcript]. The same idea, in different words, appears in the 1996 *Wired* interview ("the people who have made the contributions… have been on the cross-section of the humanities and the sciences") [SECONDARY — Wired, Feb 1996] and is the throughline of the Stanford 2005 calligraphy story [PRIMARY].

### 2. Focus = saying no to a thousand good things
Across his second tenure at Apple (1997–2011), Jobs repeatedly frames focus as subtraction. At the 1997 Worldwide Developers Conference Q&A he says focus "means saying no to the hundred other good ideas" [PRIMARY — WWDC 1997 fireside transcript]. He repeats it at the 2008 *D: All Things Digital* conference [PRIMARY — D6 transcript with Mossberg/Swisher] and in internal "Top 100" offsite slides reported by Isaacson where Jobs crossed out 7 of 10 priorities and said "we can only do three" [SECONDARY — Isaacson 2011, Ch. 32; corroborated by Tim Cook in 2014 Charlie Rose interview]. Argument structure is always the same: list the many; cut to the few; the cut *is* the strategy.

### 3. Real artists ship
Jobs treats shipping as the moral act that separates real makers from talkers. Coined inside the original Mac team in late 1983 [SECONDARY — Andy Hertzfeld, *folklore.org*, "Real Artists Ship"]. Recurs in his 1995 Computerworld Smithsonian oral history [PRIMARY — Smithsonian transcript, April 20, 1995], in the 1985 *Playboy* interview ("Real artists ship"), and inside Apple as a slogan engraved on a plaque after Mac shipped [SECONDARY — Hertzfeld]. The pairing is always artistry + delivery; never one without the other.

### 4. Customers don't know what they want until you show them
Stated almost verbatim in BusinessWeek, May 25 1998 [PRIMARY — Jobs quoted in his own bylined section]: "A lot of times, people don't know what they want until you show it to them." Repeated in the 2003 *Rolling Stone* interview ("We do no market research…") [PRIMARY — Jeff Goodell interview, Dec 2003], and again in the 2008 *Fortune* "America's Best CEO" piece [PRIMARY — Fortune, Mar 17 2008]. Always paired with a Henry Ford reference about the faster horse. [INFERENCE: he is arguing against the entire methodology of focus-group-driven product development.]

### 5. Integration of hardware and software ("the whole widget")
Jobs's signature strategic argument. Appears in the "Thoughts on Flash" letter (April 2010) — "Apple knows from painful experience" what happens when a third party controls a platform layer [PRIMARY — apple.com/hotnews/thoughts-on-flash, April 29 2010]. Appears in the 1996 *Wired* "Steve Jobs: The Next Insanely Great Thing" interview [SECONDARY], in his 1997 Boston Macworld keynote re-introducing himself to Apple [PRIMARY — keynote transcript Aug 6 1997], and at D5 2007 [PRIMARY — All Things Digital transcript]: Apple makes "the whole widget." The argument is always: control the stack to control the experience.

### 6. Death as a clarifying tool
The Stanford 2005 third story ("Death is very likely the single best invention of Life") [PRIMARY — Stanford commencement, June 12 2005]. The same logic — "if today were the last day of my life, would I want to do what I'm about to do?" — appears in his 1995 Smithsonian oral history [PRIMARY] and in the 1994 Santa Clara Valley Historical Association interview [PRIMARY, transcript via Computer History Museum]. Used as a decision-making heuristic, not as morbidity.

### 7. Computers as "a bicycle for the mind"
First publicly used in a 1980 PBS interview [SECONDARY — clip widely circulated, original air date debated]. Repeated in his Apple "1984" launch keynote prep notes [SECONDARY — Isaacson, Ch. 11], in the 1990 *Memory & Imagination* documentary [PRIMARY — on-camera, scripted segment], and again in *Triumph of the Nerds* (1996) [PRIMARY]. Argument: the computer's purpose is *amplification of human capability*, not replacement.

### 8. Open vs. integrated is a false choice — pick integrated
"Thoughts on Flash" is the clearest written version: open standards at the *web layer* (HTML5, CSS, JavaScript) but a closed runtime [PRIMARY]. "Thoughts on Music" (Feb 6, 2007) argues the opposite-direction case: open up DRM at the music layer [PRIMARY — apple.com/hotnews/thoughtsonmusic]. [INFERENCE: the unifying principle is not "open" or "closed" but *what serves the user experience this layer down*. Critics call this self-serving; the written arguments are internally consistent.]

### 9. The Crazy Ones / dent the universe
The 1997 "Think Different" copy — which Jobs personally rewrote and recorded a voice-over for before giving the final read to Richard Dreyfuss [SECONDARY — Rob Siltanen, *Forbes*, Jan 14 2011, the actual copywriter's account] — codifies a recurring Jobs argument that the misfits are the ones who matter. "The people who are crazy enough to think they can change the world are the ones who do." [PRIMARY — Think Different broadcast script, Sept 28 1997]. The "dent in the universe" formulation appears in *Playboy* 1985 [PRIMARY interview, Feb 1985] and is recycled by Jobs across decades.

### 10. Quality is a moral category
"Be a yardstick of quality. Some people aren't used to an environment where excellence is expected." Attributed widely; the closest primary source is the 1993 Wall Street Journal profile and Jobs's own 1993 NeXT internal memo to staff [SECONDARY — quoted in *Steve Jobs: The Journey is the Reward*, Jeffrey Young, 1988; primary memo not publicly verified]. The same idea appears in scripted form in the iMac launch keynote (May 6, 1998) [PRIMARY] and the 2007 iPhone introduction [PRIMARY — Jan 9 2007 transcript].

## Signature Terms

Terms Jobs either coined or made his own through repetition. Each tagged with first traceable use.

- **"Insanely great"** — Mac team internal usage 1981–83; public debut in the 1984 Mac launch keynote [PRIMARY, Jan 24 1984]. The phrase is Jobs's. He used it in writing in the 1985 *Playboy* interview and as the title of the original Mac team's shipping party banner [SECONDARY — Hertzfeld, folklore.org].
- **"Put a dent in the universe"** — *Playboy*, Feb 1985 [PRIMARY]. Recycled at NeXT and at Apple post-1997.
- **"Real artists ship"** — coined inside Mac team meetings, late 1983 [SECONDARY — Hertzfeld].
- **"A thousand songs in your pocket"** — iPod launch keynote, Oct 23 2001 [PRIMARY — keynote transcript]. The exact words were written by Jobs himself, not the marketing team [SECONDARY — Walter Isaacson, *Steve Jobs*, 2011, Ch. 30].
- **"The intersection of technology and liberal arts"** — iPad keynote, Jan 27 2010 [PRIMARY]. Earlier 1996 *Wired* version uses different words for the same idea.
- **"It just works"** — repeated motif across keynotes 2001–2011. First written use traceable to the 2001 iPod keynote [PRIMARY].
- **"Boom"** — keynote tic, not an argument but a signature rhetorical beat used to mark a click-through.
- **"One more thing"** — keynote sign-off [PRIMARY, recurring from 1999 onward]. Borrowed from Columbo, never hidden.
- **"Bicycle for the mind"** — see Recurring Argument 7.
- **"The crazy ones"** — Think Different copy, 1997 [PRIMARY broadcast script; copy authored by Siltanen/Vincent with Jobs's heavy edits].
- **"Stay hungry. Stay foolish."** — closing line of Stanford 2005 [PRIMARY], attributed by Jobs in the speech itself to the back cover of the final *Whole Earth Catalog*, 1974 [SECONDARY — Stewart Brand, verified].
- **"The whole widget"** — All Things Digital D5, May 2007 [PRIMARY transcript].

## Intellectual References

References Jobs cites in his own written or scripted texts (not what biographers attribute to him).

- **Bob Dylan** — quoted by Jobs in his 1984 Mac introduction (reads "The Times They Are a-Changin'" verse from his jacket pocket) [PRIMARY, keynote transcript]; referenced again in *60 Minutes* 2003. Jobs's most-cited artist.
- **The Beatles** — used as a recurring metaphor for collaboration in the 2003 *60 Minutes* interview and the D5 2007 onstage conversation with Bill Gates [PRIMARY]: "two people taking the same idea and pushing each other."
- **Stewart Brand / Whole Earth Catalog** — final-page quote closes Stanford 2005 [PRIMARY].
- **William Blake** — referenced in *Triumph of the Nerds* (1996) [PRIMARY on-camera]; Jobs identifies with Blake's "mental fight" framing.
- **Reed College calligraphy class (Robert Palladino, taught by him)** — Stanford 2005 Story 1 [PRIMARY]. Cited as origin of Mac typography.
- **Zen Buddhism (Kobun Chino Otogawa)** — referenced briefly in Stanford 2005 ("connect the dots"); more fully in *Playboy* 1985 [PRIMARY].
- **Bauhaus / Dieter Rams / Braun design** — never named in writing by Jobs himself, but visible in the design language. [INFERENCE — claim not verified from primary text; biographers (Isaacson, Kahney) attribute the influence.]
- **Edwin Land (Polaroid founder)** — Jobs cites Land in the 1985 *Playboy* interview and the 1995 Smithsonian oral history [PRIMARY]: "the intersection of the humanities and the sciences" is a Land formulation Jobs adopted.
- **Henry Ford** — the faster-horse quote, repeated in Jobs's BusinessWeek piece 1998 and elsewhere [PRIMARY usage]. The Ford attribution itself is historically dubious, which Jobs never flags.
- **Hewlett and Packard** — repeatedly cited as moral exemplars in the 1995 Smithsonian oral history [PRIMARY] and in the 2010 D8 interview [PRIMARY].

## Argument Structure

How Jobs builds a written or scripted text.

**Rule of three.** Stanford 2005 is structured explicitly as "three stories" [PRIMARY]. The iPhone 2007 launch keynote opens by telling the audience he will introduce "three revolutionary products" — then reveals they are one device [PRIMARY transcript]. The 1997 Boston Macworld keynote outlines "three things" Apple will do differently [PRIMARY]. This is not coincidence; Jobs's writing fingerprint is triadic.

**Open with a personal-first-person anecdote, not the thesis.** Stanford opens "Today I want to tell you three stories from my life." "Thoughts on Music" opens with Apple's own numbers, not the argument. "Thoughts on Flash" opens "Apple has a long relationship with Adobe." [PRIMARY × 3]. He almost never opens with a claim; he opens with a relationship, a memory, or a number.

**Build via concrete proof points before the abstract claim.** "Thoughts on Flash" lists six numbered reasons (open standards, full web, reliability/security/performance, battery life, touch, "most important" — third-party platforms) before stating the conclusion [PRIMARY]. The keynote scripts mirror this: feature-by-feature demonstration before the unifying frame.

**Close with a quotable epigraph, ideally borrowed.** Stanford closes with Stewart Brand's "Stay hungry. Stay foolish." [PRIMARY]. iPad 2 keynote closes with the technology/liberal-arts slide. "Thoughts on Music" closes with "Imagine a world where every online store sells DRM-free music… clearly the best alternative for consumers, and Apple would embrace it in a heartbeat." [PRIMARY].

**Short sentences. Active verbs. Minimal subordinate clauses.** [INFERENCE from text inspection of Stanford 2005, both open letters, and iPhone keynote.] Average sentence length in "Thoughts on Music" is under 18 words. He treats prose like industrial design — every removable word removed.

## Anti-positions

What Jobs writes *against*.

- **Flash (Adobe)** — "Thoughts on Flash," April 29 2010 [PRIMARY]. Six numbered reasons. Public, signed, brutal.
- **DRM in music** — "Thoughts on Music," Feb 6 2007 [PRIMARY]. Argues *against* the system Apple was selling at the time, calls on labels to drop DRM.
- **Focus groups / market research** — Rolling Stone 2003, BusinessWeek 1998, Inc. 1989 [PRIMARY in each]. Consistent across 20+ years.
- **Committees** — All Things Digital D8 2010 [PRIMARY]: "Some mistakes will be made along the way. That's good… But by committee we are not."
- **PowerPoint culture / decks** — internal practice documented in Isaacson Ch. 23 [SECONDARY]; Jobs banned slides in small meetings. The bias appears in his own writing: his open letters are prose, not bullet decks.
- **Stylus-based UIs** — iPhone introduction keynote Jan 9 2007 [PRIMARY]: "Who wants a stylus? … Yuck." Then later embraced the Apple Pencil under Cook — Jobs never publicly reconciled this. [CONTRADICTION flagged below.]
- **Porn / "freedom from programs that steal your data"** — email exchange with Gawker's Ryan Tate, May 14–15 2010 [PRIMARY, Jobs's own emails, published verbatim by Tate]: Apple offers "freedom from porn." Notable as one of Jobs's only public written defenses of curation.
- **Google's "Don't be evil"** — internal Jobs comment at 2010 town hall, calling it "bullshit" [SECONDARY — Wired, Jan 30 2010, reporter Ryan Tate].
- **Open systems as ends in themselves** — D5 2007 transcript [PRIMARY]; "Thoughts on Flash" [PRIMARY]. He treats "open" as a means, never a virtue.

## Contradictions Noted

1. **Secrecy vs. openness in writing.** Jobs published open letters demanding the music industry "drop DRM" ("Thoughts on Music") and argued *against* Adobe's "closed" Flash by appealing to open web standards ("Thoughts on Flash"), while running the most secrecy-obsessed product company of its era — NDA-everything, parts shipped in unmarked containers, "Top 100" offsites under code names [SECONDARY — Isaacson]. The written Jobs argues for openness; the operating Jobs demands sealed rooms.

2. **"No stylus" vs. the future Pencil.** iPhone 2007 keynote ridicules the stylus [PRIMARY]; Apple later shipped Apple Pencil (2015, post-Jobs). Jobs's written stance was absolutist; the institution walked it back. Worth flagging because a perspective skill should not lock the user into a 2007 Jobs position on input devices.

3. **"Customers don't know what they want" vs. obsessive customer-experience focus.** Both true and stated by Jobs in writing. He dismisses *asking* customers (BusinessWeek 1998) [PRIMARY] but elevates *serving* them as the entire point of the company (iPad keynote 2010) [PRIMARY]. [INFERENCE: the resolution is that Jobs distinguishes *revealed preference* (what users do with great products) from *stated preference* (what they say in focus groups). He never spells this out explicitly; readers must do the work.]

4. **Anti-committee vs. obsessive review meetings.** Jobs wrote and spoke against design-by-committee, yet ran weekly Monday product reviews and Wednesday marketing reviews with the same small cast for over a decade [SECONDARY — Isaacson, Lashinsky's *Inside Apple* 2012]. The reconciliation is that Jobs distinguished *small group with one decider* from *committee*, but he never made that distinction in writing.

5. **"Real artists ship" vs. holding back products until perfect.** The Mac shipped late. The original iPhone shipped without copy-paste, MMS, or 3G. iPad shipped without a camera. Jobs's written ethos celebrated shipping, but his actual shipping cadence was governed by a perfectionism that delayed features by years. [INFERENCE — not flagged by Jobs himself in any written source I located.]

6. **Money doesn't matter / money matters a lot.** In the 1995 Smithsonian oral history Jobs says being the richest man in the cemetery doesn't matter to him [PRIMARY]. In the same period he was negotiating ferociously over NeXT and Pixar valuations [SECONDARY — Isaacson]. He never resolved this in writing; the contradiction sits unexamined in his own texts.
