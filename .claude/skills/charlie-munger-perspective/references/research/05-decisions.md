# Charlie Munger: The Record of What He Actually Did

Behavior beats words. This file maps Munger's pivotal decisions — what he chose, why he said he chose it, what happened, and what (if anything) he said about it afterward. Where stated reasoning at the time differs from later reflection, both are recorded.

---

## 1. See's Candies (January 1972) — the pivot from cigar-butts to quality

**Context.** Berkshire (via Blue Chip Stamps) had a chance to buy See's Candies, a California chocolate retailer with after-tax returns of ~25% on tangible assets of ~$8M. Sellers wanted $30M. Buffett, trained by Ben Graham, balked at paying more than book value.

**Decision.** Munger, joined by Ira Marshall, pushed Buffett past his price ceiling. Buffett walked the deal up to ~$25M and the seller accepted. Munger's argument: See's wasn't a chocolate company, it was a piece of California's identity with brand equity competitors couldn't replicate.

**Stated logic at the time.** Munger saw a business that didn't need new capital to grow, generated cash, and had pricing power latent in customer affection. Buffett described his own resistance later: he was still "a cigar-butt investor" looking for fair companies at wonderful prices, not the inverse.

**Outcome.** See's generated more than $2B in pre-tax cash on the ~$25M cost over the following decades. More importantly, the deal reprogrammed Buffett. He later said Munger "weaned" him off Graham. The lesson Munger explicitly drew: "Warren and I raised the prices of See's Candies a little faster than others might have. There are actually people out there who don't price everything as high as the market will easily stand. And once you figure that out, it's like finding money in the street."

**Post-hoc reflection.** Buffett: "Charlie shoved me in the direction of not just buying bargains, as Ben Graham had taught me. This was the real impact he had on me. It took a powerful force to move me on from Graham's limiting view. It was the power of Charlie's mind." See's became the template for Coke (1988), Gillette, and eventually Apple. (Sources: 25iq.com, Quartr, GuruFocus, theinvestmentsblog.)

## 2. Wheeler, Munger & Co. winddown (1973–1976) — the temperament crucible

**Context.** Munger ran Wheeler, Munger & Co. from 1962. Through 1972 the partnership had returned ~28% annualized.

**Decision.** Munger held concentrated, leveraged positions (notably Blue Chip Stamps) through the 1973–74 crash. The fund lost ~32% in 1973 and ~31% in 1974 — limited partners' accounts compounded to a roughly 53% drawdown. He recovered most of it in 1975 (+73%) but **chose to wind the partnership down in 1976** anyway.

**Stated logic.** Munger said the drawdown "didn't bother me with my own money, but it made me suffer the tortures of hell as I thought through the loss of morale of the limited partners who had trusted me." Running other people's money through that kind of pain was, in his view, a job he no longer wanted.

**Outcome.** Full-period performance was still ~19.8% annualized (vs ~5% Dow). He kept Blue Chip Stamps and Wesco in his own and Buffett's hands and stopped managing outside capital for fees.

**Post-hoc reflection.** This experience underwrote his lifelong refrain that the most important quality in an investor is the temperament to hold through 50% drawdowns. He returned to it for the rest of his life as a lesson about psychological capacity, not stock-picking skill. (Sources: GuruFocus partnership analysis, Value Investing World, Wikipedia.)

## 3. Wesco Financial (1974–2011) — the laboratory

**Context.** Blue Chip Stamps started buying Wesco Financial — a Pasadena savings & loan — in 1974. Munger ran it as chairman from the mid-1970s through the 2011 merger into Berkshire.

**Decision.** Rather than run Wesco as a thrift, Munger **deliberately diversified it out of S&L exposure** into insurance (Wesco-Financial Insurance), steel service centers (Precision Steel), and a securities portfolio. He persuaded the existing management to stay rather than gutting them.

**Stated logic.** Munger judged the S&L industry structurally broken long before the 1980s collapse and refused to compound capital in a business he didn't respect.

**Outcome.** Wesco survived the S&L crisis intact and became known as "Mini-Berkshire." Berkshire acquired the remaining 19.9% in 2011 at $385/share, closing a 37-year compound.

**Post-hoc reflection.** Munger used Wesco's annual meetings (1986–2011) as his personal pulpit — the transcripts later became "Poor Charlie's Almanack" raw material. (Sources: Wikipedia, granitefirm.com Wesco meetings recap, SEC 8-K filings.)

## 4. Costco board seat (1997 – November 2023) — the institution he loved most

**Context.** Buffett was offered a Costco directorship, declined, and pointed Jim Sinegal to Munger.

**Decision.** Munger took the seat in 1997 and **never sold a share**, including through 2008–09 and through Amazon's rise. He was a Costco board member for 26 years until his death.

**Stated logic.** "I love everything about Costco. I'm a total addict, and I'm never going to sell a share." He saw Costco as the world's best execution of a single idea: pass scale benefits to customers, hard-wired into a 90%+ membership renewal rate.

**Outcome.** Munger's personal Costco position was over 180,000 shares at his death — a position widely estimated above $100M. He literally ate $1.50 hot dogs at Costco in his last weeks.

**Post-hoc reflection.** In his final Morningstar/Acquired Podcast interviews (late 2023), Munger called Costco "the only thing I want to keep" and "a perfect damn company" — though he also acknowledged Costco's stock had grown expensive. (Sources: Costco investor relations governance page, Morningstar "Final Thoughts," Yahoo/Benzinga.)

## 5. BYD (September 2008) — the bet he was proudest of

**Context.** Li Lu, who managed Munger's family money, brought him a Chinese battery-and-EV company run by an ex-government chemist named Wang Chuanfu. Munger championed it to Buffett. Berkshire (via MidAmerican Energy) bought ~10% for $230M in September 2008.

**Decision.** Munger pushed Buffett past his standing reluctance to invest outside the US in operating businesses he couldn't directly visit.

**Stated logic.** Munger's pitch: "This guy is a combination of Thomas Edison and Jack Welch — something like Edison in solving technical problems, and something like Welch in getting done what he needs to do. I have never seen anything like it."

**Outcome.** At peak in mid-2022 the stake was worth ~$9.5B — roughly a 40x at peak. Berkshire began trimming in 2022. BYD overtook Tesla in EV unit sales in Q4 2023.

**Post-hoc reflection.** "I have never helped do anything at Berkshire that was as good as BYD." Munger called Wang's outclassing of Musk "almost ridiculous." Notably this decision **violates** several of Munger's own stated rules: it was outside his circle of competence (Chinese capital goods), it was an operating business he could not visit, and it was a bet on a single founder. He acknowledged all of this and made it anyway. (Sources: Fortune, CNBC, EVWorld, Yahoo Finance.)

## 6. Daily Journal — the late-career masterclass and the late-career stumble

### 6a. The 2009 bottom-tick (Q1 2009)

**Context.** The Daily Journal Corporation, a sleepy LA legal-newspaper publisher Munger chaired since 1977, sat on ~$20M of cash through the 2008 crash.

**Decision.** In Q1 2009, at the market's literal bottom, Munger deployed Daily Journal's corporate cash into Wells Fargo (around $8/share), Bank of America, US Bancorp, and Posco.

**Stated logic.** Munger believed the US banking system was solvent, that Wells Fargo's franchise was misunderstood, and that being on the Wells board he had unusual visibility into the loan book.

**Outcome.** By H1 2009 the portfolio had grown to $47.9M — a $32.4M pre-tax gain, ~209% in six months. By 2015 Wells was up 570%, BAC 431%, USB 374% from cost.

**Reflection.** Munger described the trade as "shooting fish in a barrel," and used it for years afterward as Exhibit A in the case for **sitting on cash for decades and then betting decisively when the opportunity is screamingly obvious**.

### 6b. The Alibaba mistake (2021–2022)

**Context.** Through 2021, Munger built a position in Alibaba at Daily Journal, taking it from 165,320 shares in Q1 2021 to 602,060 shares by Q4 2021. He **doubled it on margin** in Q1 2022 even as the Chinese regulatory crackdown intensified, then halved it in Q2 2022.

**Decision.** Buy, double on leverage, then partially capitulate.

**Stated logic at the time.** Munger believed Alibaba was Amazon-plus-AWS-plus-Visa in the world's largest market, run by a generational entrepreneur, trading at a low-teens P/E. He saw the regulatory pressure as overblown.

**Outcome.** The position was cut at losses. Daily Journal's Alibaba stake was a meaningful drag on the portfolio.

**Post-hoc reflection.** At the 2023 Daily Journal meeting: "I regard Alibaba as one of the worst mistakes I ever made. In thinking about Alibaba, I got charmed with the idea of their position on the Chinese internet. I didn't stop to realize it's still a goddamn retailer." This is striking on its own terms — Munger explicitly admitted the failure was an analytical category error (he misclassified the business) and that he had been "charmed" — letting a narrative override a business analysis. (Sources: Fortune, Dr Wealth, GuruFocus.)

### 6c. The chairmanship transition (March 2022)

At 98 — not 97 as commonly reported — Munger stepped down as Daily Journal chairman in March 2022 (announced after the Feb 2022 annual meeting), donating $1M in stock to fund an employee equity plan, but **stayed on the board**. He chaired Daily Journal for 45 years. The fact that he refused to fully exit even at 98 says something about how he viewed his own utility: a continuing voice on capital allocation was, in his own self-assessment, still worth more than retirement. (Sources: Bloomberg, CNBC, Dr. David Kass blog.)

## 7. Munger, Tolles & Olson (1962) — the institution he engineered

**Context.** In February 1962, Munger and two partners left Musick, Peeler & Garrett to found a new LA law firm.

**Decision.** He built MTO with two deliberately unusual structural choices:

1. **A bonus-pool meritocracy.** Each partner privately writes down what every other partner should make. The numbers must sum to net income. Seniority is a factor partners may weigh but is not a rule. There are no points, shares, or fixed formulas. The system survived for 60+ years.

2. **Integrated from the start.** The 1962 founding partnership included a woman, when essentially no Wall Street/LA firm did this. MTO subsequently produced multiple women, women-of-color, an LGBTQ man, and a Black man as managing partners.

**Stated logic.** Munger believed incentive systems shape behavior more than rules do, and that hiring on talent rather than pedigree would produce a long-run quality advantage no competitor would copy because competitors were optimizing for short-run prestige.

**Outcome.** MTO became one of the most profitable per-partner firms in the country and a feeder for Supreme Court clerks. Munger left active practice in 1965 to invest full-time but kept his name on the door. (Sources: Commoncog case library, Wikipedia, MTO.com.)

## 8. The architecture obsession — Stanford, Michigan, UCSB

**Context.** Munger had no architecture training but designed major buildings he funded.

**Decisions.** $43.5M for Stanford Munger Graduate Residence (2004), $110M+ to Michigan for a graduate residence, ~$40M in stock to the Huntington Library (2023), Harvard-Westlake trustee for 40+ years with a ~$9.2M gift in 2006, and the $200M Munger Hall pledge to UC Santa Barbara — with the condition that **his blueprints be followed exactly**, including ~94% of single-occupancy rooms having no exterior windows.

**Stated logic.** Munger argued small interior rooms force students into common areas where serendipitous intellectual collisions happen. He cited Disney cruise ship cabin design as the model.

**Outcome.** Dennis McFadden, an architect on UCSB's design review committee, resigned in October 2021 in protest. A petition gathered ~14,000 signatures. In August 2023 — three months before Munger's death — **UCSB cancelled the Munger Hall project**. His other named buildings were built.

**Reflection.** Munger never publicly recanted on Munger Hall. In his October 2021 Architectural Record interview he remained adamant: students would adapt; common-area design mattered more than windows. This is a case where Munger's stated humility ("acknowledge what you don't know") and his actual behavior (donate $200M conditional on amateur blueprints being followed against credentialed protest) diverge sharply. (Sources: Wikipedia Munger Hall, Architect Magazine, Santa Barbara Independent, Architectural Record, Stanford News.)

## 9. The acknowledged misses — Walmart and Google

At the 2017 Berkshire annual meeting, Munger flatly told shareholders, **"We failed you,"** on two stocks:

- **Walmart.** "We blew Wal-Mart too… It was a total cinch. We were smart enough to figure that." Berkshire only bought in Q2 2005, after the major compounding was done.
- **Google.** "I feel like a horse's ass for not identifying Google earlier… We screwed up." The kicker: GEICO, a Berkshire subsidiary, was a heavy Google advertiser, so Berkshire had direct evidence of Google's unit economics inside its own P&L.

Munger's diagnosis was self-critical: both were inside his circle of competence, both were screamingly obvious, and he simply didn't act. He treated these as failures of execution, not of analysis. (Sources: Yahoo Finance, Business Insider, OfficeChai.)

## 10. Personal-life decisions that shaped the operating system

- **WWII (Army Air Corps, ~1943–46):** Caltech meteorology training. Disciplined him in probabilistic forecasting under uncertainty.
- **Divorce from Nancy Huggins (1953):** Left him near-broke at 29 with three children.
- **Death of son Teddy from leukemia (1955), age 9:** Munger said later, "I cried all the time when my first child died." Leukemia survival in 1955 was effectively 0%. This was the event he later credited (implicitly via Marcus Aurelius) with teaching him that "every mischance in life is an opportunity to behave well and learn" and that "self-pity… gets close to paranoia."
- **Marriage to Nancy Barry Borthwick (1956–2010):** 54 years; she died in 2010. He raised a blended family of nine.
- **Botched cataract surgery (1980):** Lost his left eye, suffered such severe pain it had to be removed, faced potential sympathetic ophthalmia in the right eye, and **started taking Braille lessons** rather than complain. He did not sue the surgeon. The right eye condition receded; he read on one eye for the remaining 43 years.

These personal decisions form the backbone of his Stoicism: not as an aesthetic but as a tool that had been operationally tested under the worst events a person can have. (Sources: Yahoo Finance, Rational Walk, Safal Niveshak, Wikipedia.)

## Patterns across the record

1. **He acted on conviction even against his own rules.** BYD broke "circle of competence" geographically. Alibaba broke it categorically. Munger Hall broke "stay inside what you know how to do." He was willing to violate his own rules when he believed the upside justified it — and was willing to own the failures publicly when it didn't work (Alibaba) and refuse to recant when he still believed (Munger Hall).
2. **He sized down, not out, when he lost faith.** Wheeler-Munger wound down rather than continued at smaller scale. Alibaba was halved, not zeroed. Costco was never trimmed. The pattern: when he no longer trusted the structure (Wheeler partners' tolerance, Alibaba thesis), he reduced commitment; when he trusted it (Costco), he never moved.
3. **He treated institutions as compound-interest machines.** MTO (60+ years), Daily Journal (45 years as chairman, plus board to death), Costco (26 years), Berkshire (60 years), Harvard-Westlake (40+ years as trustee). His revealed preference: stay on the board, keep voting, refuse the gold watch.
4. **The Teddy/eye/divorce sequence is upstream of everything.** The Stoic operating system wasn't a hobby — it was forged in the worst events a person can survive, then deployed in markets.
