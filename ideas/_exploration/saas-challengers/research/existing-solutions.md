# Existing Solutions — Findings

## Key findings

- **EDA incumbents (Synopsys ~31%, Cadence ~30%, Siemens ~13%) have a six-layer structural moat that is nearly impenetrable for a small team.** The hard stop: TSMC mandates "Calibre-clean" DRC/LVS at tape-out, meaning a challenger's tool literally cannot be used for production chips without foundry certification. PDK co-development happens 24 months before production with no open path for newcomers. 95%+ customer retention; no competitive losses on Cadence Palladium hardware in decades. Verdict: wrong beachhead for a bootstrap solo founder — regulatory/certification moat, not just incumbency. The only wedge the market has accepted is a point-tool that sits beside (not replacing) existing EDA — e.g. Quilter ($25M Series B, Oct 2025) automates PCB layout in minutes while teams keep Altium/Cadence Allegro for the rest of the flow. "Feature, not replacement" is the only viable EDA entry for a small team.

- **PCB layout is the one EDA sub-workflow an AI-native product can wedge without foundry certification.** Quilter (physics-driven RL, $40M total raised, Index/Benchmark/Coatue) targets hardware startups whose PCB layout sits on the critical path. It does not replace the EDA suite; it accelerates placement/routing from weeks to minutes. The wedge is speed-as-a-service for hardware teams that can't afford or wait for a specialist PCB engineer. This sub-workflow is now occupied by a funded player — avoid direct replication, but adjacent variants (e.g. schematic capture assistance, BOM optimization, DFM checks for SMB electronics shops) are not yet crowded.

- **ERP lock-in is real but concentrated in large enterprises; mid-market ($2M–$100M revenue) is structurally underserved and the crowded-but-fragmented wave of AI challengers has not produced a clear winner yet.** SAP/Oracle lock-in mechanisms: custom code in vendor-specific formats, data gravity in proprietary schemas, multi-year ELAs, and implementation costs that routinely exceed license fees (one U.S. manufacturer spent $2.5M just switching vendors in 2023). But AI-native challengers are swarming the $5M–$200M revenue band: Rillet ($70M Series B, a16z/ICONIQ, Aug 2025), DualEntry ($90M Series A, $415M valuation), Campfire ($100M, ~$500M valuation), Doss (~$20M, manufacturing/CPG/wholesale). Over $500M VC went into these seven platforms in 18 months. Finance/GL workflows are now crowded. The open gap: operations-layer ERP (inventory, procurement, order routing) for manufacturing/CPG SMBs in non-English markets — Doss is the closest but US-only and lightly funded.

- **China/HK ERP market is a structurally distinct opportunity: SAP/Oracle hold ~40% revenue share at the high end, but Kingdee and Yonyou dominate SME/government tiers, and Huawei MetaERP is now displacing foreign vendors at SOEs.** China ERP market projected to exceed 45B yuan (~$6.3B) by 2025, growing 19.7% YoY. The push to replace foreign ERP (SAP/Oracle) at SOEs due to data sovereignty concerns is accelerating — Huawei MetaERP passed SOE production tests in May 2025. For Ringo: the gap is not at Kingdee/Yonyou's turf (SME accounting) but at the mid-tier manufacturer that has outgrown Kingdee but can't afford SAP and has China-specific compliance needs (Golden Tax, VAT fapiao, multi-entity HK+mainland structures). No well-funded AI-native player targeting this specific segment.

- **ICS/SCADA/MES incumbents (Siemens, Rockwell, Schneider, Honeywell) are locked in by safety certification (IEC 61511, ISA/IEC 62443), OT protocol dependencies, and 40-year hardware depreciation cycles — not just software stickiness.** Replacing a DCS or safety-instrumented system requires re-certification of the entire control loop, which takes years and requires regulatory sign-off. However, the MES layer (above SCADA, below ERP) has softer lock-in: MES deals with production scheduling, quality, and traceability rather than safety-critical control. Tulip (frontline operations platform, $291M total raised, $1.3B valuation) wedged in here by letting operators build apps that connect to existing SCADA/ERP via APIs — a "System of Engagement" on top of the System of Record. This sub-workflow is now well-funded and occupied. The open gap: predictive-maintenance and OEE dashboards for sub-200-employee Asian manufacturers who cannot afford Tulip's enterprise pricing and have brownfield OPC UA/MQTT-capable equipment.

- **The "intelligence layer above ERP/TMS/WMS" is the newest and least-crowded wedge in supply chain as of June 2026.** Logistics Viewpoints (June 3, 2026) identifies an emerging category of AI orchestration sitting above existing ERP, TMS, and WMS without replacing them — focused on exception management, carrier selection, and demand signal aggregation. This is distinct from o9 (enterprise planning, $3.7B valuation, $533M raised) and Altana ($200M Series C, $1B valuation, supply chain transparency/trade data). The open territory is SMB-facing: mid-market companies whose WMS "exports to spreadsheets" and whose supplier lead times "live in email threads" — a segment most AI supply chain platforms explicitly exclude because their models need clean structured data.

- **Blue Yonder (Panasonic-owned) and Kinaxis do not publish pricing and route all deals through enterprise sales; their implementations run 12–24 months.** This is a structural channel conflict that prevents them from serving sub-$500M-revenue companies effectively. The SMB/mid-market gap in supply chain planning is acknowledged but sparsely addressed: Logility (inventory optimization), GoComet (logistics), MercuryGate (TMS for SMBs) exist but none are AI-native and none dominate. For a bootstrap founder, a narrow AI-native TMS or demand-planning tool targeting Asian SMB importers/exporters (HK/Greater China trade flows) has no obvious funded competitor in that geography.

- **"Feature, not a company" acquisitions are accelerating in AI-adjacent SaaS: Moveworks acquired by ServiceNow for $2.85B (March 2025); Vanti AI (manufacturing predictive maintenance) acquired by Tray (March 2025).** The pattern: a single-workflow AI tool gets absorbed into a platform before it can expand. The risk is highest when the wedge is a UI/copilot layer with no proprietary data flywheel. Mitigation observed in surviving challengers: own the data (Cadence JedAI trains on customer design data; Altana owns the 2.8B-shipment graph). For any idea in this space, the question is what data asset compounds with usage that an acquirer cannot simply replicate — without that, the exit is acqui-hire, not independence.

- **Synopsys-Ansys closed ($35B, July 2025) and Cadence-Hexagon announced ($3.16B, late 2025) signal that EDA incumbents are buying their way into system-level simulation — a domain that had independent vendors.** This consolidation leaves fewer independent point-tool targets for acqui-hire but also means the incumbents are distracted by integration work, creating a 12–24 month window where their product roadmaps slow in adjacent areas (AI-assisted analog, mature-node tools, cloud-native EDA for China fabless). China fabless companies are now explicitly building domestic EDA (export controls forcing localization) — a geo-specific opening that Ringo's trilingual/Greater China reach could access, though it requires deep semiconductor domain knowledge he does not currently have.

- **The ERP AI-native wave has a clear "feature trap" already emerging: augmentation layers like Nominal (~$30M) sit atop existing SAP/NetSuite as a "shadow general ledger" — this is the most acquirable, most replaceable position.** Full-stack replacements (Rillet, DualEntry, Campfire) that own the GL have more durable positioning but require winning the migration battle. The open high-value position that is not yet crowded: a full-stack AI-native ERP for a specific vertical (e.g. HK/China trading companies, cross-border e-commerce sellers with fapiao/VAT complexity) where SAP is too expensive, Kingdee lacks AI depth, and no Western AI-native ERP has localized.
