# Agent 4: External Views on Pareto

*What critics, biographers, and scholarly commentators say about Vilfredo Pareto. This is where blind spots are diagnosed and where the unresolved controversies live — especially the fascism question.*

---

## Canonical Scholarly Portraits

### Raymond Aron — *Main Currents in Sociological Thought*, vol. 2 (1967, French original *Les étapes de la pensée sociologique*)

Aron's chapter on Pareto, paired with Durkheim and Weber, is the canonical French portrait that introduced Pareto to a generation of Cold War-era sociologists. Aron treats Pareto as the Italian "neo-Machiavellian" who mocked traditional morality and humanitarian pretensions and emphasized "the oligarchic or elitist character of all societies." Aron is a sympathetic but distancing reader: he respects Pareto's logico-experimental ambition and his refusal of metaphysical consolation, but he openly recoils from what he calls Pareto's "exaggerated critique of humanitarianism." Aron tries to rescue Pareto from the fascism charge by reading him as a disabused liberal — a man whose contempt for democratic ideology was the contempt of a thinker who watched parliamentarism corrupt itself, not of one who wanted the boot. The chapter's lasting move is to treat Pareto's sociology as more useful than his politics: the theory of elites and residues survives even when Pareto's personal misanthropy is set aside.

### Joseph V. Femia — *Pareto and Political Theory* (Routledge, 2006)

The first English-language book-length political-theory study of Pareto, and the most aggressive recent rehabilitation. Femia's project is announced as "a detailed refutation of the familiar charge that Pareto was some sort of proto-fascist." Femia relocates Pareto in what he calls "the Machiavellian tradition of *sceptical liberalism*, which scorns metaphysical abstraction and assigns ontological primacy to the individual." Femia frames Pareto's importance through four dichotomies of political discourse: individualism vs. holism, science vs. hermeneutics, laissez-faire vs. social engineering, and value relativism vs. moral absolutism. The book is "critical but sympathetic," conceding Pareto's brittleness, his cynical excesses, and the unfalsifiability of his residue typology, while insisting his structural insights — that ideologies are derivations of pre-rational sentiment, that elites circulate, that democracy decays into demagogic plutocracy — anticipate postmodern epistemology and modern public choice.

### Talcott Parsons — *The Structure of Social Action* (1937)

Parsons made Pareto a member of the canonical triad (with Weber and Durkheim) at the founding moment of American sociology. The crucial scholarly point: this was a **selective edit.** Parsons drew on Pareto's concept of *non-logical action* and the equilibrium framework to build his "voluntaristic theory of action," but he stripped out the misanthropy, the contempt for democracy, the elite-circulation cynicism, and the political economy of plunder. What survived in Parsons's hands was a tame, methodologically respectable Pareto: the social-system theorist, useful for integrating the rational and the non-rational. Critics ever since have argued that "Parsons's Pareto" is barely Pareto — that the structural-functionalist reading domesticated a thinker who was, in Burnham's reading, far more politically combustible. The Pareto Parsons introduced to Harvard generated what was nicknamed "the Pareto vogue" of the late 1920s and 1930s.

### James Burnham — *The Machiavellians: Defenders of Freedom* (1943)

Burnham clustered Pareto with Gaetano Mosca, Robert Michels, and Georges Sorel as the "Machiavellians" — a "distinct tradition of political thought" interested in constructing a science of politics that begins from the assumption that "society is of its nature oligarchical, and the power of the oligarchy always rests upon force and fraud." Burnham's reading was politically incendiary: he used the Italian elite theorists to argue that the American liberal elite had grown "weak, inflexible and bound to American liberalism in ways that erode liberty." George Orwell, in his famous essay "Second Thoughts on James Burnham," treated this Pareto-via-Burnham construction as the dangerous ideological core of mid-century power-worship. For better or worse, Burnham is the reason Pareto is alive on the contemporary American right.

### Charles Powers — *Vilfredo Pareto* (Sage, "Masters of Sociological Theory," 1987)

The standard concise English-language introduction, 167 pages, written for graduate students. Powers presents Pareto as a "pioneering sociologist" whose theory of ruling elites and theory of essentially irrational political behaviour deserve serious modern engagement. Powers is largely defensive — he treats the fascism charge as anachronistic and the unfalsifiability charge as misunderstanding the heuristic function of residue/derivation typology. The book is useful precisely because it is uncontroversial; it represents the "rehabilitated, normal-science Pareto" of the late-20th-century sociology curriculum.

### Renato Cirillo — *The Economics of Vilfredo Pareto* (1979) and "Was Vilfredo Pareto Really a 'Precursor' of Fascism?" (*AJES*, 1983)

Cirillo is the most committed defender of Pareto-the-liberal. His 1983 essay's title is the punch: he argues that Pareto "remained a radical libertarian till the end, and continued to express serious reservations about fascism, and to voice opposition to its basic policies." Cirillo's evidence is private correspondence — he claims Pareto was critical of fascism in his letters even when his public statements appeared accommodating. Critics counter that selective letter-reading is exactly how exculpatory biographies are built.

### Vincent J. Tarascio

Tarascio is the methodologist who broke the dominant Schumpeter framing. Schumpeter had treated Pareto as essentially a Walrasian, a refiner of general equilibrium. Tarascio argued that during 1892–1900 Pareto was already tracing the outlines of an economic science methodologically different from Walras — one that took non-logical action and the experimental method seriously, and that produced the eventual rupture into sociology. This matters because it undermines the "Pareto as second-rate Walras" demotion that simmered in mid-century economics.

### Richard Bellamy and Norberto Bobbio

Bellamy's *Modern Italian Social Theory* and Bobbio's writings situate Pareto inside the Italian tradition of *realismo politico*: Machiavelli → Pareto → Mosca → Bobbio → Sartori. Bobbio is the most ambivalent: he was "strongly influenced by Hans Kelsen and Vilfredo Pareto" yet became postwar Italy's most prominent democratic liberal. The Bobbio reading inherits Pareto's diagnostic realism while explicitly rejecting his contempt-for-democracy conclusion.

## Major Criticisms (organized by charge)

### Charge 1: Proto-fascist, or at least fellow-traveler

**The case for:**
- He welcomed Mussolini's 1922 March on Rome.
- The fascist regime offered him a senatorship in the upper house. (He never formally responded — but didn't publicly refuse.)
- Mussolini falsely claimed to have attended his Lausanne lectures, presenting himself as a Paretian disciple.
- **Karl Popper** labeled Pareto "the theoretician of totalitarianism" — a phrase that stuck for decades.
- **Hannah Arendt** characterized him as "a preacher of violence," relying (per Adair-Toteff) on "vague, second-hand memories" and cherry-picked letter quotes.
- His relentless contempt for democracy, parliamentarism, humanitarianism, and the "demagogic plutocracy" of his time reads, in hindsight, as having softened the soil.

**The case against:**
- He died in August 1923, before fascism revealed itself — before the 1924 Matteotti assassination and the 1925 authoritarian consolidation. He cannot be held accountable for what he didn't live to see.
- In his late essays on constitutional reform, he insisted on "a broad freedom of the press" and on the necessity of opposition parties — positions fundamentally at odds with fascism in practice.
- Femia: he belongs to "sceptical liberalism," not authoritarianism.
- Cirillo's private letters show late-life reservations.
- His real targets across his career were anti-socialism, anti-statism, anti-colonialism, anti-militarism, anti-racism, and anti-anti-Semitism — none of which align with fascist ideology.
- Giandomenica Becchio: "his initial sympathy for early fascism has been overestimated and probably had been manipulated by the regime."
- S. E. Finer's 1968 defense argued vigorously that Pareto was no Carl Schmitt despite similar portraits.

**The unresolved residue:** even his most sympathetic readers concede he was a misanthrope who held democracy in contempt and admired strong men over weak parliaments. The fascism charge cannot be cleanly killed; it can only be downgraded from "fascist" to "contempt-of-democracy elder cynic who didn't live long enough to see what his contempt enabled." The skill should hold this open, not close it.

### Charge 2: The "logico-experimental" method does not deliver what it claims

Croce's 1900–1902 attack (in *Giornale degli Economisti*) is the original methodological critique: Pareto smuggled in **a hidden metaphysics** by treating human activity "as of the same nature as physical facts," and then disguised the move with mathematics. Croce: mathematicians "brought their disciplinary prejudices into the field, failing to recognize they were exchanging one bias for another." Pareto's response was pragmatic-flexible — methods should be judged by results — but he never fully answered the charge that "logico-experimental" is itself a philosophical commitment, not a neutral procedure.

Modern restatement: the logico-experimental method is a *positivist creed*, not a neutral epistemology. It rules out hermeneutics, value, and meaning by assumption, then claims to have *discovered* their unimportance.

### Charge 3: Residues and derivations are unfalsifiable

The structural complaint, found across Bogardus, modern sociologists, and political theorists: Pareto never clearly explained the nature of residues, the relationships between residue classes, or the rules for identifying when a given derivation is a rationalization of which residue. The classification is "vague" (Bogardus). It is "often not easy to uncover the residue common to different derivations." Result: the framework can absorb any observation — name an action and a residue can be backfilled. It functions as **post-hoc interpretation**, not testable theory.

**The defense:** residue/derivation is a *heuristic typology*, not a falsifiable theory in the Popperian sense. Powers and Femia both make this move — they treat it as a lens, like Weberian ideal types, not as physics.

### Charge 4: Reductive — everything explained by interests and sentiment

The charge from Croce, from Aron, and from later humanists: by reducing morality, religion, art, and political ideology to derivations of pre-rational residues, Pareto leaves no room for genuine value, genuine reason, or genuine choice. Everything is rationalization. The framework explains why people *say* they want freedom or justice, but not why freedom or justice might actually matter. The result is what Murray Rothbard called Pareto's "retreat into cynical critiques" after he had once been "a militant laissez-faire liberal."

### Charge 5: Inconsistency between the economist and the sociologist

The Schumpeter-tradition criticism: Pareto-the-economist gives us indifference curves, ordinal utility, Pareto-optimality, and equilibrium — a tightly mathematical, methodologically modest enterprise. Pareto-the-sociologist gives us residues, derivations, the circulation of elites, and a sprawling unfalsifiable typology that runs the other direction. How does the same mind produce both? Tarascio defended the unity (the seeds of the sociology were already in the early economic methodology); critics (including Schumpeter himself) treated the *Treatise* as the sad late work of a great economist gone sour.

### Charge 6: Pareto efficiency smuggles in the status quo

The democratic-theory and welfare-economics critique: Pareto efficiency is *not* the value-neutral concept it claims to be. By defining improvement as "no one is made worse off," it grants a veto to every existing rent-holder. A world where one person owns everything and everyone else owns nothing is Pareto efficient. The criterion "implicitly favors the status quo, biasing towards supposed stability even in the face of mounting inequality." Sen, Roemer, and a generation of welfare economists have argued that calling this neutrality is itself an ideological move.

**The defense:** Pareto efficiency was never meant to be a complete moral criterion; it is one diagnostic among many. Treating it as a status-quo bias is treating a thermometer as a thermostat. Critics retort that this is exactly how it gets *used* in policy.

### Charge 7: Feminist and democratic-theory critiques

Pareto's sociology has very little to say about women, sex, family structure, or the gendered division of labor; his "Class I" (combinations) and "Class II" (group persistence) residues are presented as universal psychology without ever interrogating whose psychology is being theorized. Feminist political theory (Pateman tradition) treats this as part of the broader silence of classical realist political thought on the gendered constitution of public/private. On democratic theory: Pareto's collapse of democratic legitimacy into "ideology = derivation = lie" leaves no conceptual room for the actually-existing improvements in welfare, rights, and inclusion that democratic politics has produced. The democratic theorists' charge is that Pareto's framework can explain *why* democracies disappoint but cannot explain *how* they sometimes work.

## Defenses Against the Major Charges

**"Not a fascist, just a misanthrope" (Femia, Cirillo, Becchio, Finer):** distinguish ideological commitment from rhetorical contempt. Pareto disliked the parliamentary regime he saw around him; that does not make him an advocate of the regime that replaced it. Death-before-1924 is load-bearing here.

**"Pareto efficiency is value-neutral, that's a feature not a bug" (welfare economists, public choice):** the criterion is meant to be modest and partial. Asking it to do distributional work is a category error. It is the floor of consensus, not the ceiling of justice.

**"Residues and derivations are heuristic typologies, not falsifiable theory" (Powers, Femia):** treat them as Weberian ideal types or analytical lenses. They are *useful* if they reveal recurring structures (the lion/fox cycle, the conservative/innovative oscillation). They are not *physics*.

**"The unity defense" (Tarascio):** the apparent inconsistency between economist and sociologist dissolves once you see the early methodology was already preparing the late typology.

**"Cited less, read more" (Mingardi):** the strongest defense is also the most damning — most of the things Pareto is blamed for, he didn't quite say, and most of the things he did say are forgotten. The recommendation: stop citing him as a totem and actually engage the texts.

## Peer Cluster — The Elite Theorists

The Italian school of elitism is a tight cluster, and external scholarship almost always reads Pareto through this triangle:

**Pareto — Mosca — Michels.** All three argue: (a) every society is divided into a ruling minority and a ruled majority, (b) ideology legitimates rule, (c) democratic forms do not change the underlying oligarchic substance. They differ on the mechanics:

- **Pareto:** cyclical circulation of elites; lions (force) and foxes (cunning) alternate; ideologies are *derivations* — ex-post rationalizations of pre-rational residues.
- **Mosca:** gradual ruling-class mobility; ideology *legitimizes* rule as a "political formula"; emphasis on the political class's organization.
- **Michels:** the iron law of oligarchy — even mass-democratic and socialist organizations develop oligarchic leaderships; **organizational logic** sustains oligarchy.

Mosca and Pareto bickered over priority, with Mosca insisting his *Elementi di Scienza Politica* (1896) preceded Pareto's elite work. The dispute survives in Italian scholarship.

**With Sorel:** Burnham added Georges Sorel (myth, violence, the role of irrational symbols in political mobilization) to the cluster. The four-name version — Pareto, Mosca, Michels, Sorel — is Burnham's signature framing.

**With Machiavelli:** the deep ancestor. The Italian *realismo politico* tradition reads from Machiavelli through Guicciardini to Pareto, Mosca, and into Bobbio and Sartori in the postwar period.

## Lineage Position

**Upstream of Pareto:**
- Machiavelli, Guicciardini (Italian political realism)
- Frédéric Bastiat (plunder theory of politics — Mingardi: "Pareto took Bastiat's theory of plunder and expanded it, making it a pillar of his own understanding of society")
- Léon Walras (general equilibrium, the Lausanne chair)
- Auguste Comte, Herbert Spencer (positivist sociology, though Pareto rejected their progressivism)

**Lateral / peers:**
- Mosca, Michels, Sorel (elite theorists)
- Croce (philosophical antagonist)
- Weber and Durkheim (yoked together with Pareto by Aron and Parsons, though Pareto interacted little with them)

**Downstream of Pareto:**
- **Joseph Schumpeter:** borrowed from Pareto's 1901 *Rise and Fall of Elites* "two themes to which Schumpeter would return repeatedly: the inevitability of elites and the importance of nonrational and nonlogical drives in explaining social action." Schumpeter's elite-democracy theory in *Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy* is unthinkable without Pareto. Schumpeter's critique of Pareto was that the equilibrium model is static, while real economies are dynamic — leading to creative destruction.
- **Talcott Parsons:** voluntaristic theory of action and structural functionalism.
- **Raymond Aron:** the disabused-liberal sociology of mid-century France.
- **James Burnham:** managerial revolution, Cold War elite theory, and a strange afterlife on the contemporary American right.
- **Public choice (Buchanan, Tullock):** the political economy of plunder, rent-seeking, the failure of democratic constitutionalism to discipline self-interested politicians.
- **Behavioral economics (Kahneman, Tversky, Thaler, et al.):** bounded rationality, two-system cognition — the modern descendants of Pareto's logical/non-logical action distinction, though with no acknowledgment.
- **Network science (Barabási, Watts):** Pareto distributions as the canonical form of power laws in social, biological, and technical networks. Pareto would have shrugged.

## Reputation Arc Across the 20th–21st Century

**1900–1916:** Continental economist of stature, occupant of the Lausanne chair, originator of the income distribution power law (1896).

**1916–1923:** *Trattato di Sociologia Generale* published; reception in Italy mixed. Croce's earlier methodological attack still hangs over him.

**1923–1935:** Mussolini's regime claims him as a hero. Pareto's actual relationship to fascism remains ambiguous because he dies before he can clarify it.

**1935–1945: "The Pareto vogue."** Lawrence Henderson at Harvard runs the famous Pareto Circle; Bernard DeVoto popularizes him in American intellectual journals; Parsons enthrones him in *The Structure of Social Action* (1937); Burnham makes him a Machiavellian (1943). This is the high-water mark in the English-speaking world.

**1945–1970:** Sharp decline. Anti-fascist intellectual climate makes association with him politically expensive. Popper labels him a totalitarian theorist. Sociology turns toward functionalism (Parsons absorbs the useful bits), then conflict theory (which prefers Marx), then ethnomethodology (which has no use for residues at all). Pareto becomes an embarrassment that sociology departments stop teaching.

**1970s–1980s:** Quiet rehabilitation. Cirillo (1979, 1983) defends him on fascism. Powers (1987) writes the standard introduction. Public choice economists (Buchanan, Tullock) revive his political economy of plunder under new vocabulary.

**1990s–2000s:** Femia's *Pareto and Political Theory* (2006). The 80/20 rule popularized through management consulting via Joseph Juran's *Quality Control Handbook* (1951) and Richard Koch's *The 80/20 Principle* (1997) — turning Pareto into a household name *for the wrong reason*, completely detached from his sociology. Pareto would have raised an eyebrow at Koch's self-help repackaging.

**2010s–2020s:** Network science discovers power laws everywhere (Barabási). Behavioral economics rediscovers non-logical action. Public choice and political realism both invoke him. The Italian-tradition revival (Bellamy, Bobbio's afterlife) treats him as foundational. *Jacobin* and the contemporary left rediscover him as a diagnostician of ruling-class decline. The contemporary right rediscovers him via Burnham.

The net: Pareto is once again *cited* widely, but — as Mingardi noted — "perhaps it would be better if he were cited less and read more." Most invocations of "Pareto efficient" or "Pareto principle" or "elite circulation" have no contact with the corpus.

## Blind Spots Others Identify

External commentators converge on a recurring list:

1. **Democracy can sometimes deliver.** Pareto's framework explains democratic disappointment elegantly but has no apparatus for democratic *success* — for the actual welfare, rights, and inclusion gains that democratic politics produced in the century after he died.

2. **Institutions, not just elites.** Mosca, Michels, and modern political science give weight to organizational form, formal rules, and constitutional design as autonomous causal forces. Pareto collapses too much into elite psychology and residue/derivation cycles.

3. **Value and meaning are not just rationalization.** The reductive sociology that treats every value as a derivation of a sentiment leaves no room for the possibility that humans sometimes actually mean what they say.

4. **Gender, family, intimate life.** Total silence on the gendered constitution of the social. The "circulating elite" is universalized as a male political class without acknowledgment.

5. **Race and empire.** Despite Pareto's personal opposition to colonialism, racism, and anti-Semitism, his sociology has no analytical apparatus for race, colonial extraction, or the role of imperial violence in shaping European prosperity.

6. **Methodological self-awareness.** The logico-experimental method is a philosophical commitment dressed as a procedure. Pareto never quite admits this; Croce's charge is never fully answered.

7. **Time horizons and dynamics.** Schumpeter's critique: the equilibrium model is static, the residue/derivation framework is cyclical, and neither captures genuine novelty or creative destruction.

8. **Inequality as a moral question.** Pareto efficiency cannot speak to distributional justice, and his sociology treats inequality as a natural fact rather than a contestable arrangement. This is now the most-cited blind spot.

## Top 3 Charges + Best Defenses (executive summary)

| Charge | Best Defense |
|---|---|
| **Proto-fascist** (Popper, Arendt) | He died in August 1923, before fascism revealed its authoritarian face; his late essays insist on press freedom and opposition parties; his Lebanon-list of "anti-"s (anti-socialism, anti-statism, anti-colonialism, anti-militarism, anti-racism, anti-anti-Semitism) is incompatible with fascist ideology (Femia, Cirillo, Becchio). |
| **Unfalsifiable residue/derivation framework** (Bogardus, Aron) | These are heuristic typologies in the Weberian ideal-type sense, not falsifiable theories — useful as lenses for recurring patterns of legitimation (Powers, Femia). |
| **Pareto efficiency smuggles in the status quo** (Sen, Roemer, welfare economics critics) | The criterion is meant as a partial diagnostic, not a complete moral framework; calling it value-neutral within its domain is correct, and the misuse is on the policymakers who treat it as moral. |

## Cleanest One-Sentence Statement of His Blind Spots

> Pareto's sociology can diagnose with brilliance why democracies, ideologies, and reform movements decay into rationalizations of elite self-interest, but it cannot see — and refuses to look for — the institutions, values, gendered structures, and genuine moral commitments through which human beings sometimes actually do better than their residues would predict.
