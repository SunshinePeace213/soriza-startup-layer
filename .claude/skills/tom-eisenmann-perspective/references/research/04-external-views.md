## Academic reception (entrepreneurship scholars)

### Two-sided / platform work (more famous in academia than the failure work)
- **[secondary]** Eisenmann, Parker & Van Alstyne's "Strategies for Two-Sided Markets" (HBR 2006) is listed by HBR as an **all-time top 50 article** and has **749+ citations** on Semantic Scholar.
- **[secondary]** "Platform Envelopment" (with Parker & Van Alstyne, SSRN 1496336) is a heavily cited contribution — the "envelopment risk" concept is now standard vocabulary in platform strategy.
- **[primary]** Hagiu & Wright (2015) and Rochet & Tirole (2003, 2006) frequently engage with Eisenmann's work, but their later definitions of multi-sided platforms have partially *displaced* Eisenmann's original framing. **[secondary]** Hagiu in particular has refined the definition in ways that critique the breadth of Eisenmann's 2006 categorization.
- **[inference]** Eisenmann is acknowledged as a **founder of the practical/managerial side** of two-sided market literature, while Rochet-Tirole own the **economic-theory** side. His work bridges to managers; theirs bridges to antitrust.

### Failure work / entrepreneurship scholarship
- **[secondary]** Sarasvathy (effectuation) has cited Eisenmann (Eisenmann et al. 2011) when proposing integrated frameworks merging effectuation with lean startup. She uses him; she does not attack him.
- **[primary]** Broader effectuation critics (Kitching & Rouse 2020 in *International Small Business Journal*) attack the entire genre — including lean startup and adjacent frameworks — for assuming an "under-socialised 'heroic' entrepreneur" with "excessive powers of agency." **[inference]** This critique implicates Eisenmann by association: his failure-pattern framework also presumes the founder is the primary causal agent.
- **[inference]** Noam Wasserman (peer HBS professor, *Founder's Dilemmas* 2012) is the most obvious comparator. Wasserman's quantitative work on ~10,000 founders preceded Eisenmann's smaller (470 founders) survey. Eisenmann's book builds on Wasserman's "people problems are the leading cause of failure" thesis but adds five other patterns. Public record shows mutual respect; no public friction.
- **[inference]** Conspicuous in absence: Howard Aldrich, Saras Sarasvathy, Steve Kaplan, Josh Lerner, Ramana Nanda — top entrepreneurship/finance scholars — have not (in surfaced material) publicly weighed in on *Why Startups Fail*. Possibly because it is positioned as a **trade book**, not a research monograph.

## Recurring criticisms (with sources)

1. **Selection-on-the-dependent-variable / methodology** — **[primary]** Goodreads reviewer: "The book is mostly based on case studies of failures, it selects on the dependent variable...it's difficult to say whether the identified causes of failure are sufficient or even truly causal." **[inference]** This is the deepest critique: by only studying failures (his solution to survivorship bias), Eisenmann *inverts* the bias rather than eliminating it. The patterns he identifies may also be present in successful startups.

2. **Sample bias — VC-backed tech only** — **[primary]** Multiple Goodreads reviewers note the sample is "almost exclusively...VC-backed tech startups." **[inference]** Bootstrapped, services, lifestyle, hardware, biotech, regional, non-US, and non-tech ventures are largely absent. The Boston/SF/HBS-network sampling frame skews findings.

3. **Hindsight retrospection** — **[secondary]** His method (interviews and post-mortem case studies) inherently invites narrative reconstruction. Founders interviewed post-failure have already built explanatory stories. **[primary]** One review notes he discusses failures "in hindsight" without controls for memory distortion. **[inference]** The "six clean patterns" may be partly an artifact of his pattern-recognition lens.

4. **Frameworks "too tidy" / HBS-esque** — **[primary]** "Very HBS-esque, with a heavy reliance on frameworks that can be identified via geometric shapes or names that are unintuitive."

5. **Doomsday tone without prescription** — **[primary]** "More of a doomsday book, with a million warnings about why your startup will fail but not a strong roadmap."

6. **Insights feel "specific yet vague"** — **[primary]** Hard-to-operationalize for early-stage founders.

7. **Loses the visceral / emotional dimension** — **[primary]** Audiobook reviewer: misses what made the HBS class powerful.

8. **Underweights luck/randomness** — **[inference]** Combined with the deterministic pattern framing, Taleb-style critics would say Eisenmann commits the narrative fallacy. Search did not surface an explicit Taleb-style critique, but the structural risk is there.

9. **Survivorship-bias inversion** — **[inference]** Same patterns probably appear in successful startups; the book lacks a control group of "ventures that did the same thing but succeeded anyway."

## Blind spots others have identified

- **Geographic / network blind spot.** Cases lean heavily on US, especially Boston/SF, especially HBS-alumni-led companies (Quincy Apparel, Baroo, Jibo, Dot & Bo, Triangulate, Fab.com). **[inference]**
- **Sector blind spot.** Heavy on consumer internet, hardware, and marketplace; light on biotech, deep tech, climate, fintech in emerging markets, B2B SaaS scale-up specifics. **[inference]**
- **Funding-model blind spot.** Almost exclusively VC-track. Bootstrapped/indie founders see their lived experience absent. **[primary, Goodreads]**
- **Macro / luck blind spot.** Patterns are framed as founder-controllable; less attention to macro timing, monetary regime, regulatory shocks. **[inference]**
- **Survivor-comparison blind spot.** No symmetric study of the same "patterns" appearing in successful firms. **[inference]**
- **Power / social structure blind spot.** Kitching & Rouse-style critique would say Eisenmann's "founder as primary causal agent" framing under-socializes the entrepreneur — ignoring class, race, capital access, network effects. **[primary, generalized from broader entrepreneurship critique literature]**
- **Emotional / psychological depth blind spot.** Some readers feel the case-study format flattens the founder's lived emotional experience. **[primary, Goodreads]**

## Peer comparisons

### Eisenmann vs. Noam Wasserman (*The Founder's Dilemmas*, 2012)
- **Overlap:** Both HBS, both case-method, both identify "people problems" as leading cause of failure.
- **Divergence:**
  - Wasserman is **quantitative** (n≈10,000 founders, regression-heavy).
  - Eisenmann is **qualitative + survey** (n≈470 founders, pattern-based).
  - Wasserman focuses on **founding-team decisions** (cofounder splits, equity, hiring).
  - Eisenmann is **lifecycle-wide** (early *and* late stage, including investor and partner failures).
- **[inference]** Wasserman is the empiricist; Eisenmann is the synthesizer.

### Eisenmann vs. Steve Blank (*Four Steps to the Epiphany* / Customer Development)
- **Overlap:** Eisenmann cites Blank explicitly; co-authored "Customer Discovery and Validation for Entrepreneurs" (2012, Cespedes/Eisenmann/Blank).
- **Divergence:**
  - Blank is a **practitioner-turned-teacher**; Eisenmann is an **academic-turned-popularizer**.
  - Blank prescribes a *process* (customer development).
  - Eisenmann prescribes *patterns to avoid* (failure patterns).
  - **[primary]** Eisenmann argues many founders "claim to embrace lean...but actually adopt only part of it" — a friendly amendment.
- **[inference]** They are allies; Eisenmann's critique is *within* the lean tent.

### Eisenmann vs. Eric Ries (*The Lean Startup*, 2011)
- **Overlap:** Ries blurbs the book warmly.
- **Divergence:**
  - **[primary, Eisenmann's HBR 2021]** Eisenmann argues "fail fast" / launch-early advice causes the **False Starts** failure pattern: founders skip customer discovery and "turn the 'fail fast' mantra into a self-fulfilling prophecy."
  - Eisenmann thinks Ries-style MVP advice has been *misapplied* and that customer research must precede MVP.
- **[inference]** This is the closest Eisenmann gets to a direct intellectual fight, but it is framed as correction-not-rejection.

### Eisenmann vs. Marc Gruber (*Where to Play*)
- **Overlap:** Both emphasize systematic market opportunity assessment.
- **Divergence:**
  - Gruber's Market Opportunity Navigator is a **forward-looking selection tool**.
  - Eisenmann's diamond-and-square is a **diagnostic of fit** across opportunity, people, execution, financing.
- **[inference]** Gruber is a single-tool author; Eisenmann is a multi-framework synthesizer.

## Platform / two-sided market work reception

- **[secondary]** "Strategies for Two-Sided Markets" (HBR 2006) is one of HBR's most influential platform articles; "Platform Envelopment" introduced a now-standard term.
- **[primary]** Within the academic literature on platforms, Rochet-Tirole (2003) and Armstrong (2006) are the canonical *economic* references; Eisenmann/Parker/Van Alstyne are the canonical *managerial-strategy* references.
- **[secondary]** Hagiu & Wright have refined the definition of "multi-sided platform" partly in response to imprecision in earlier work, including Eisenmann's.
- **[inference]** Eisenmann's platform work has aged remarkably well; if anything, his **practitioner influence here exceeds his startup-failure influence**. Geoff Parker & Marshall Van Alstyne later extended this stream into *Platform Revolution* (2016), which became the popular-book vehicle for the ideas Eisenmann co-originated.
- **[inference]** The startup-failure work may be his more visible second act, but the platform work is his more durable academic legacy.

## How outsiders position him

- **Academic ↔ Practitioner axis:** Squarely **bridge figure**. More rigorous than a McKinsey partner; more accessible than a *Strategic Management Journal* econometrician. **[inference]**
- **Cautious ↔ Bold axis:** **Cautious.** Risk-aware framing, pattern-based prudence, warnings-first. **[secondary, multiple Goodreads]**
- **Insider ↔ Outsider axis:** **Consummate insider** — HBS faculty since 1997, Arthur Rock Center co-chair, taught the first-year required course. Yet positioned as an *honest insider* willing to dwell on failure cases that the HBS-success-story factory usually buries. **[inference]**
- **Process-oriented ↔ Intuition-oriented:** **Process-oriented.** Frameworks, patterns, diamond-and-square. **[secondary]**
- **Theorist ↔ Empiricist:** **Empiricist-leaning synthesizer.** Less data-volume than Wasserman; more data than Ries. **[inference]**
- **Lean-startup stance:** "Reformed apostle." Believes the framework is *right but misapplied*. **[primary, HBR 2021]**
- **Vs. Wasserman:** Wasserman is the *cofounder-dynamics quant*; Eisenmann is the *whole-lifecycle pattern-spotter*. **[inference]**
- **Vs. Blank/Ries:** Eisenmann is the *gentle critic from inside the lean church* — adding "research customers *first*" guardrails. **[inference]**

- WSJ / NYT / FT / Bloomberg / Economist / Fortune reviews not located via standard web search. Likely paywalled or simply not commissioned for this book — itself a signal about how the book was positioned (founder-trade, not literary-business).

The respectful insider reception may itself be a blind spot — peers are too polite to press the methodological case.
