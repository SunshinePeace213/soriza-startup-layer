# Trends / Why-Now — Findings

## Key findings

- **$6 of services for every $1 of software (Sequoia's ratio), with IT services alone at $1.73T vs software at $1.23T in 2025 (Gartner/Forrester).** The addressable gap between tool spend and labour spend is what makes "sell the service" a larger prize than any SaaS category; even a narrow vertical (e.g., US outsourced accounting at $50–80B) rivals entire SaaS markets.

- **Gustaf Alströmer (YC) named four verticals for the 2026 Request for Startups: insurance brokerage, accounting/tax/audit, compliance, and healthcare administration.** His specific framing: outsourced services are "a vendor swap not a reorg" — the budget line already exists, the buyer already accepts an external party doing the work, so replacing the BPO with an AI-native firm skips the change-management fight that replacing internal software creates.

- **Andrew Lee (a16z) declared in "Big Ideas 2026" that fat startups win — the thesis is that successful AI companies bundle software + data + hardware + human ops into full-stack solutions rather than selling a tool.** The practical translation: "done-for-you" positioning (AI bookkeeping not bookkeeping software; AI tax prep not tax software) unlocks outcome pricing and cuts the customer's decision to a familiar vendor-selection frame rather than a make-vs-buy frame.

- **Outcome-based pricing is already displacing seats at measurable scale: Zendesk launched $1.50/automated-resolution pricing in Aug 2024; Intercom Fin charges $0.99/resolution and hit 393% annualised Q1 growth; Sierra (Bret Taylor) hit $100M ARR in 21 months charging per conversation/resolution.** This is not a theoretical model — enterprise buyers are signing outcome-priced contracts today. Implication: a new entrant in any vertical can anchor pricing to value delivered rather than seats or usage tiers.

- **Frontier model token costs fell ~92% on inputs between March 2023 and 2025 (GPT-4 launched at $30/M input tokens; GPT-4o is now ~$2.50/M); mid-tier equivalent quality is available at $0.50–1.00/M via DeepSeek-class models — a 50–100x effective reduction.** This makes the unit economics of running multi-step agentic workflows over thousands of customer tasks viable for the first time; gross margins that were negative in 2023 at service volumes are now workable in 2025–2026.

- **Legal is the proof-of-concept vertical: Harvey went $0 → $190M ARR in ~36 months (Jan 2026 figure), raised at an $11B valuation in March 2026.** It sells work product (contract review, due diligence output) not a software licence. The accounting/tax vertical is structurally primed next: the US lost ~340,000 accountants over five years while 75% of CPAs are nearing retirement, creating a supply shortage that makes buyers actively seek replacement rather than resist it.

- **The verticals that went first share three structural traits (Emergence Capital / Sequoia analysis): high-volume repeatable workflows with clear pass/fail output (a ticket resolved, a return filed, a contract reviewed), existing outsourcing norms so no new trust primitive is needed, and measurable outcome definitions that enable per-unit pricing.** Verticals with ambiguous deliverables (strategy consulting, creative) or heavy licensed-professional liability are going last.

- **Key counter-signal — Klarna reversed its AI-only customer service pivot in May 2025, rehiring humans after CEO Siemiatkowski admitted "cost was too predominant an evaluation factor" and customer satisfaction dropped. IBM survey of 2,000 CEOs (same period) found only 1 in 4 AI projects delivered promised ROI.** The failure mode is not costs — it is quality on edge cases, emotional complexity, and multi-step resolution that pure AI still fumbles. The winning model in 2025–2026 is human-in-the-loop for exceptions (the "fat startup" ops layer), not headcount elimination.

- **The margin compression risk is real and structural: AI-first startups show early gross margins near 25% vs. the 70–80% of classical SaaS; inference costs run 4–9% of revenue and scale linearly with usage (not sub-linearly like software).** Gartner estimates 40%+ of enterprise SaaS spend will shift to usage/outcome models by 2030. Outcome pricing only saves margin if the underlying task cost falls faster than the price customers will pay per resolution — winners will be verticals where AI handles 80–90%+ of volume without human touch.
