# The Six Failure Patterns — *Why Startups Fail* / 不受傷創業 (English)

Tom Eisenmann's six recurring patterns from *Why Startups Fail* (the Chinese edition, 不受傷創業, tells
the same six as stories). The `pressure-test` stage reads this to ask **"which known death pattern does
this idea most resemble?"** — then converts the answer into a falsifiable assumption + a past-behaviour
interview question. It is **not** a kill: it names the risk to test with real people.

**Framing.** Failure ≠ shutting down — Eisenmann's operational definition is *"early investors never
made, and never will make, more money than they put in."* And it's rarely just the idea (horse) or the
founder (jockey): **"it's usually neither"** — most failures trace to a *third party* (team, investors,
partners → pattern 1).

## Early-stage patterns (diagnosed with the Diamond-and-Square)

The **diamond** = the opportunity's four corners (customer value proposition · technology & operations ·
marketing/GTM · profit formula). The **square** = the resources around it (founders · team · investors ·
partners). Early failures are a *misalignment* among these eight.

1. **Good Idea, Bad Bedfellows.** A promising opportunity sunk by the *people* — a mismatched team,
   wrong investors, unreliable partners. *(e.g. Quincy Apparel.)*
   → Test: who are the load-bearing third parties, and is each one actually aligned and reliable?

2. **False Start.** "Ready, fire, aim" — building before doing the basics (customer research, smoke
   tests), so you ship something with no validated need. *(e.g. Triangulate.)*
   → Test: what have you validated *before* building — and did real users reveal the need, or did you
   assume it?

3. **False Positives.** Early enthusiastic adopters mislead you; the mainstream market doesn't follow
   (early adopters ≠ the broader market). *(e.g. Baroo.)*
   → Test: are the people who love this *representative* of the wider market, or a non-generalizable
   fringe? (This is the guard the discovery synthesis coverage/skew check enforces.)

## Late-stage patterns (diagnosed with RAWI + the Six S's)

**RAWI** is a *scaling* gate — *should we step on the gas into hypergrowth now?* — **Ready** (PMF + a path
to profit), **Able** (can access talent/capital), **Willing** (want growth + accept dilution / loss of
control), **Impelled** (a competitive reason to grow now). The **Six S's** of scaling: Staff, Structure,
Shared values, Speed, Scope, Series-X funding.

4. **Speed Trap.** Early fast growth tempts over-aggressive scaling; you exhaust the early market, CAC
   rises, new-customer quality drops, competitors pile in. *(e.g. Fab.com.)*
   → Test: is the early growth from a *repeatable* engine, or are you about to run out of cheap early
   adopters?

5. **Help Wanted.** Either you *can't* secure the resources (talent, capital) to scale, **or** hyper-growth
   outruns the people/capital you have and PMF erodes for new customers.
   → Test: what must be true about hiring/funding for this to scale — and is that realistic for a solo,
   bootstrapped founder?

6. **Cascading Miracles.** A vision so grand it needs *many* low-probability things to ALL succeed —
   changing customer behaviour, enterprise partnerships, incumbent cooperation, regulatory change, large
   capital. Compound 50%-odds bets and you need 1-in-32 luck. *(e.g. Better Place.)*
   → Test: how many independent miracles does this require? Count them — each one multiplies the risk.

## How `pressure-test` uses this

The Eisenmann lens fires **one** objection: *which pattern does this idea most resemble, and what
assumption does that pattern hinge on?* → a falsifiable assumption + a Mom-Test question for the Brief.
The full persona (the `tom-eisenmann-perspective` SKILL.md) carries the deeper machinery (the Catch-22,
the endgame/recovery modules); this file is the fast six-pattern checklist.
