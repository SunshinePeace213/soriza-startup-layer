## Core Recurring Arguments (≥3 occurrences each)

For each, I list at least three primary-source citations. Quotations are verbatim from the source noted in brackets.

### A1. CEO psychology is the hardest skill — and almost nobody talks about it

> "By far the most difficult skill I learned as CEO was the ability to manage my own psychology. Organizational design, process design, metrics, hiring, and firing were all relatively straightforward skills to master compared with keeping my mind in check." [B1 ch. 7, "The Most Difficult CEO Skill"]

- B1 ch. 4 "The Struggle" — recasts the same point via the visceral list (food loses its taste, etc.).
- E6 (March 2011) — original blog post version, same opening sentence.
- B3 essay #3 "The Sad Truth About Developing Executives" — the same framing applied to executives who cannot manage themselves.

### A2. There is always a move — long-term-game survivalism

> "This is not checkers; this is motherfuckin' chess. ... like playing three-dimensional chess on Star Trek, there is always a move." [B1 ch. 4 "The Struggle"]

- B1 ch. 4 ("there is always a move," repeated 3× in close succession)
- B1 ch. 2 — going public with $2M revenue and six weeks of cash, as proof that a move exists at the worst possible moment
- E16 "The Struggle" — original blog version, same passage

### A3. Wartime ≠ peacetime; almost all management literature only teaches peacetime

> "By my calculation, I was a peacetime CEO for three days and wartime CEO for eight years. ... Interestingly, most management books describe peacetime CEO techniques and very few describe wartime." [B1 ch. 7 "Peacetime CEO/Wartime CEO"]

- E7 (Apr 2011) — original blog post.
- B1 ch. 7 — 16-point side-by-side contrast.
- B2 (2019) — extends the framework via Toussaint Louverture as a wartime culture-builder; he refers back to peacetime/wartime as the operating mode that determines which cultural virtue to amplify.
- Interview, Tim Ferriss #392 — reaffirms the dichotomy ten years later.

### A4. Courage is a developed muscle, not a trait — and the courage gradient is asymmetric

> "Every time you make the hard, correct decision you become a bit more courageous and every time you make the easy, wrong decision you become a bit more cowardly. If you are CEO, these choices will lead to a courageous or cowardly company." [B1 ch. 7 "The Fine Line Between Fear and Courage"]

- E8 (Aug 2011) — original blog post, same phrasing.
- B1 ch. 7 — embedded in the book.
- B2 ch. 3 (samurai/bushido) — culture-level restatement: virtues complement each other, and courage without rectitude is dangerous, so culture must enforce both.

### A5. Take care of the people, the products, and the profits — in that order

- B1 ch. 5 is titled this exactly and structured around it.
- B1 ch. 5 "A Good Place to Work" — operationalizes the people-first claim.
- B2 ch. 9 — "people will never forget how it felt to work there, or the kind of people they became as a result" [Walsh Accountants extracted notes of B2 p.~ch.9].

### A6. Culture is not values on the wall — it's what people do when you're not in the room

> "Culture is how your company makes decisions when the leaders are not there. It's the set of assumptions your people use to resolve problems they face every day. It is how we behave when no one is looking." [B2 Introduction, per Walsh Accountants extraction]

> "Culture means designing a way of working that will: distinguish you from competitors; ensure that critical operating values persist; help you identify employees who fit with your mission." [B1 ch. 6 "Programming Your Culture"]

- B1 ch. 6 — "If you fail to do both of those things [10× product, take the market], your culture won't matter one bit. The world is full of bankrupt companies with world-class cultures. Culture does not make a company."
- E21 (Dec 2012) — original blog post.
- B2 (whole book) — built on this premise.
- E32 (Oct 2019, Marker) "Your Mission Statement Is Not Your Company Culture" — title says it all.
- Interview, Fortune (Oct 25, 2019): "the normal way is, you bring in HR consultants, have an offsite, put the values on the wall. That doesn't really do anything other than create a culture of hypocrisy."

### A7. CEOs are made, not born — the role demands "unnatural motions"

> "CEO is an unnatural job. ... when going backward in boxing it's critically important to pick up your back foot first." [B1 ch. 7 "Making Yourself a CEO"]

- E19 (Oct 2012) — original blog post.
- B1 ch. 7 — embedded.
- B3 "The Sad Truth About Developing Executives" — the corollary: *you* can be made via reps, but *your executives* mostly cannot be developed by you, because you don't have the skills or time.

### A8. Bad news has to travel fast — fight Persian-messenger-syndrome culture

- B1 ch. 5 "Why It's Hard to Bring Big Company Execs into Little Companies" — large companies punish bad-news bearers, breeding executives who hide problems.
- B1 ch. 4 "CEOs Should Tell It Like It Is" — Cartheu-the-lineman anecdote; the single biggest improvement Horowitz made as CEO was the day he stopped being too positive.
- B2 ch. 9 (per extracted notes) — three reasons employees don't volunteer bad news, plus prescription: "Encourage bad news ... Focus on issues, not people ... Look for bad news in the regular course of business."

### A9. Hire for strength, not for absence of weakness

> "Valuing lack of weakness rather than strength. The more experience you have, the more you realize that there is something seriously wrong with every employee in your company (including you). Nobody is perfect." [B1 ch. 5 "Hiring Executives"]

- B1 ch. 5 (hiring executives framework).
- E23 "Through the Looking Glass" — applied to sales hiring: courage + competitiveness + hunger + enough intelligence.
- B1 ch. 4 "Lead Bullets" — the Mark Cranney hire (not from central casting, didn't look the part) as proof case.

### A10. Process > talent; or: brilliant people without process generate management debt

- E11 / B1 ch. 5 "Management Debt" — taking expedient short-term decisions creates long-term cost.
- E29 "How to Ruin Your Company with One Bad Process" (Dec 2015) — a single bad budgeting process can destroy a healthy company.
- B1 ch. 6 "Taking the Mystery Out of Scaling a Company" — scaling is the discipline of installing the process before you need it, but not earlier (the Scale Anticipation Fallacy is the matching error).

### A11. Founders are usually right to stay CEO — but only if they actually want it and learn

- E2 "Why We Prefer Founding CEOs" (2010).
- E24 / B3 #1 "Why Founders Fail: The Product CEO Paradox" — they fail when (a) they don't really want it, (b) the board panics, or (c) they cannot transition from intimate product involvement to a structured product process.
- Interview, Lenny's Newsletter (2024) — restates: "certain personality types shouldn't start companies at all."

### A12. The right kind of ambition: caring about the team over yourself

- B1 ch. 6 "The Right Kind of Ambition" — "The surest way to turn your company into the political equivalent of the U.S. Senate is to hire people with the wrong kind of ambition."
- B1 ch. 7 "Follow the Leader" — three traits of leadership: articulate the vision (Jobs), right kind of ambition (Campbell), ability to achieve the vision (Grove). The Campbell attribute is explicitly "right kind of ambition."
- B3 essay "Bill" (tribute to Bill Campbell, 2016) — extended embodiment of the same idea.

## Frameworks He Teaches (procedures, not just slogans)

### F1. How to do layoffs — the six-step procedure
Source: E4 (Sep 2010); B1 ch. 4 "The Right Way to Lay People Off"

1. **Get your head right** — focus on the future, accept the failure, don't perform optimism.
2. **Don't delay** — between deciding and executing, the gap must be as short as possible; leaks destroy trust.
3. **Be clear in your own mind about why** — "The company failed and in order to move forward, we will have to lose some excellent people." Do NOT frame it as performance cleanup.
4. **Train your managers — the golden rule: managers must lay off their own people.** Never outsource to HR, never let a "more confrontational peer" do it. Three things every manager must say: (a) brief explanation, company not personal failure; (b) the decision is non-negotiable; (c) full details on benefits.
5. **Address the entire company (CEO speaks first)** — "The message is for the people who are staying" [Bill Campbell to Horowitz, B1 ch. 4].
6. **Be visible, be present** — don't hide in a bar afterward. Help people carry their boxes.

### F2. How to demote a loyal friend / executive
Source: E14 (Apr 2012); B1 ch. 4 "Demoting a Loyal Friend"

1. **Decide for real.** "If you cannot afford to lose him, you cannot make this change." Don't enter the conversation undecided.
2. **Pick the best post-demotion role for them, not the obvious one.** Reporting under the new boss is usually worse than moving them sideways to a different function where their domain knowledge is still valuable.
3. **Use decisive language.** Replace "I think" with "I have decided." Don't open the negotiation door.
4. **Acknowledge your own gaps.** "If you are a founder/CEO like I was, it probably won't be lost on the employee that you are just as under-skilled for your job as he is for his."
5. **Consider coupling the demotion with a comp increase** to make it unambiguously not about value or punishment.
6. **Goal: honest, clear, effective.** They may not appreciate it in the moment; they'll appreciate it over time.

### F3. How to hire an executive when you've never done the job
Source: B1 ch. 5; partially in *Some More Things* essay #3 (negative space)

**Step 1: Know what you want.** Three traps to avoid:
- **Hiring on look and feel** (default trap — central casting is comfortable but wrong)
- **Looking for someone out of central casting** (Platonic ideal is a fantasy)
- **Valuing lack of weakness rather than strength** (everyone has serious flaws)
- The cure: **act in the role yourself**, even if temporarily. Acting VP of HR, VP of sales, etc. is "really the only way to get the knowledge you need."

**Step 2: Run a process.** Write down the strengths you want and the weaknesses you'll tolerate. Build the criteria using four buckets:
1. World-class at the function?
2. Outstanding operationally?
3. Will they contribute to strategic direction? ("Are they smart enough?")
4. Will they be an effective team member? ("Effective is the key word. ... an executive ... highly effective and profoundly influential while being totally despised. The latter is far better.")

**Step 3: Build the interview team in two layers.** (a) People who can best evaluate criteria; (b) people who'll need to support the executive once hired. (Both lists matter equally.)

**Step 4: Make the call yourself.** Use references; don't delegate the decision. Make a courageous decision rather than a consensus one.

### F4. How to hire salespeople (the rule is the opposite of hiring engineers)
Source: E23 (Apr 2013); B3 #7

> "Great sales people must be courageous, competitive and hungry. They also need enough intelligence to get the job done."

Three differences from engineering hiring:
- Sales interviews shouldn't focus on problem-solving; they should test for courage, hunger, and aggression in unconventional ways.
- Sales talent often comes from *losing* companies, not winning ones — because they had to develop real technique.
- Mistakes are cheaper and recoverable, so hire faster and fire faster than you would for engineers.

His memorable line: "applying engineering hiring logic to sales is like eating poison ivy to get more green vegetables."

### F5. How to give feedback as a new CEO — the dialogue model
Source: E19 (Oct 2012); B1 ch. 7 "Making Yourself a CEO"

Six rules:
1. **Be authentic** — believe what you're saying.
2. **Come from the right place** — they should feel you want them to succeed.
3. **Don't get personal** — focus on prepping them to succeed.
4. **Don't embarrass in public** — protect dignity among peers.
5. **Match the person, not your mood** — customize style.
6. **Direct, not mean** — clarity without cruelty.

Plus the meta-rule: **feedback is dialogue, not monologue.** Senior people often know their function better than you, and you should accept being corrected. And: **make feedback high-frequency** — constant feedback on small things normalizes it and makes bad news culturally acceptable.

### F6. The Freaky Friday technique
Source: E12 (Jan 2012); B1 ch. 8

When two departments (e.g., sales engineering vs. customer support) are at structural war, and both managers are competent and important:

1. Permanently swap their roles. ("Keep their minds, but get new bodies.")
2. Wait one week.
3. From inside the other role, each diagnoses the structural issue immediately and implements process to fix it.

Horowitz's claim: "From that day to the day we sold the company, the sales engineering and support organizations worked better together than any other major groups in the company."

### F7. The Right Way to Set Up Org Design — separate competing metrics, especially CFO vs. GC
Source: E26 (Feb 2014) "Why I Did Not Go to Jail"

Principle: **the General Counsel must report directly to the CEO, NOT to the CFO.** Otherwise the financial-optimization function will overrule the legal-compliance function on a quiet ambiguous question, and the CEO will go to jail.

Specifically: his CFO (from a prior company) eventually went to jail for the same option-grant practice he had declined to adopt — because his GC, Jordan Breslow, who reported directly to him, said: *"I've gone over the law six times and there's no way that this practice is strictly within the bounds of the law."*

### F8. How to manage your own psychology
Source: E6 (Mar 2011); B1 ch. 7

Three techniques:
1. **Make some friends.** Find peers (other CEOs) who've made similar decisions. Advice will be useless but solidarity is critical.
2. **Get it out of your head and onto paper.** Write down the logic of a decision before making it. The act of writing separates you from your psychology.
3. **Focus on the road, not the wall.** Race-car driving analogy — focus on where you want to go, not on what you fear.

Plus: **Don't punk out and don't quit.** When he interviews great CEOs about what made them great, they all say, "I didn't quit."

### F9. How to program culture — the "shocking rule" framework
Source: E21 (Dec 2012); B1 ch. 6; B2 ch. 1

A shocking rule, to embed culture, must:
1. Be **memorable** — forget the rule, forget the culture.
2. Raise the question **"why?"** ("Are you serious?")
3. Have **straightforward cultural impact**.
4. Be **encountered daily**.

His canonical examples:
- **Amazon door desks** — frugality. ("We look for every opportunity to save money so that we can deliver the best products for the lowest cost.")
- **Amazon no PowerPoint** — every meeting opens with everyone silently reading a 6-page memo, loading the data in fast.
- **a16z $10/minute late fee** — respect for entrepreneurs.
- **Facebook "Move fast and break things"** — Zuckerberg notes the rule was powerful because it stated not just what Facebook wanted but what it was willing to give up to get it. Replaced in 2014 with "Move fast with stable infrastructure" — cultures must evolve.
- **Yahoo no-work-from-home** (Marissa Mayer) — successfully restored Yahoo's work culture but couldn't fix the strategy.
- **GM "Dress appropriately"** (Mary Barra) — replaced a 10-page dress code with two words to break a bureaucracy.

### F10. Toussaint Louverture's seven tactics for building a new culture from scratch
Source: B2 ch. 1

Per book notes (extracted secondary):
1. **Keep what works** — don't reset to zero. Louverture preserved slave songs (used as battlefield signals Europeans couldn't decode) and voodoo guerrilla tactics, even though he personally outlawed voodoo.
2. **Create shocking rules.**
3. **Dress for success** — codify visible signals of the new culture.
4. **Incorporate outside leadership** — bring in people who embody what you don't yet have.
5. **Make decisions that demonstrate priorities** — pick visible test cases.
6. **Walk the talk** — leaders enforce on themselves first.
7. **Make ethics explicit** — don't assume people will figure them out. Toussaint famously banned his officers from sleeping with anyone except their wives; this distinguished his army from European colonial armies in the eyes of the populace.

### F11. Slack's four-trait hiring framework (Butterfield's, cited approvingly in B2)
Source: B2 (Walsh Accountants notes)

Stewart Butterfield of Slack screens for all four of:
1. **Smart** — disposed to learning and automation, not raw IQ. ("Tell me something you learned about how to do your last job better." "What's something you've automated?")
2. **Humble** — like Steph Curry; if you're humble, people want you to succeed.
3. **Hardworking** — disciplined, gritty, resourceful.
4. **Collaborative** — leadership everywhere; "success is limited by the worst performer."

### F12. How to tell if your culture is broken
Source: B2 ch. 9 (per Walsh notes)

Three diagnostic signals:
1. **The wrong people are quitting too often** — the ones who fit the culture you want.
2. **You are failing at your top priorities** — e.g., customer service falling.
3. **An employee does something that truly shocks you** — that's the culture revealing itself.

### F13. Founder commencement framework — "Don't follow your passion"
Source: E28 (Columbia commencement, May 2015; reprinted in *Some More Things*)

Three counterintuitive heuristics for new graduates:
1. **Don't follow your passion** — your passion is what you love, but the world doesn't value what you love. Follow what's *both* (a) what you can be great at and (b) what the world genuinely needs.
2. **Think for yourself** — original ideas that contradict consensus are the only ideas that create new value. (The Airbnb example: everyone thought it was a serial-killer idea; Brian Chesky had run the experiment.)
3. **The world is not going to hell** — and your class is not required to save it. Earn first; save the world after you've earned the right.

## Contradictions / Position Changes Detected

These are places where Horowitz's own writings disagree with each other. I preserve them rather than smooth them over.

### C1. Develop your people vs. don't develop your executives

- **Develop your people**: B1 ch. 5 "Why Startups Should Train Their People" and "A Good Place to Work" — strong pro-investment-in-training argument. The Andy Grove influence: managers' leverage comes from training.
- **Don't develop your executives**: B3 essay #3 "The Sad Truth About Developing Executives" (2015) — you can't, you shouldn't try, and trying makes things worse.

The implicit reconciliation (never made explicit by him): development works *below* the executive layer; at the executive layer, hire and replace, don't coach. But this is a real tension between B1 and B3.

### C2. Culture is everything vs. culture doesn't make a company

- B2 (2019) is built around culture as foundational.
- B1 ch. 6 (2014): *"The world is full of bankrupt companies with world-class cultures. Culture does not make a company."*

The reconciliation is that B1 says culture is necessary but not sufficient (you need 10× product + market capture first); B2 takes the necessity claim and makes it the whole argument. Read together, B2 is best understood as the answer to the question "Given you've built a real company, how do you sustain it?" — not "How do you build one?"

### C3. Wartime intolerance vs. peacetime psychological safety

- B1 ch. 7 / E7: wartime CEO *"heightens contradictions ... neither indulges consensus building nor tolerates disagreements ... rarely speaks in a normal tone."*
- B2 (and "Programming Your Culture" E21): a healthy culture is one where bad news travels fast, dissent is rewarded, and "encourage bad news" is a top diagnostic.

The reconciliation is that wartime CEO behavior is reserved for the prime directive (the single bullet in the chamber) while bad-news-friendly behavior governs everything else. But Horowitz himself notes that this is hard to implement and is the kind of thing only peacetime/wartime hybrid CEOs can do.

### C4. Founder advantage vs. founder failure modes

- E2 "Why We Prefer Founding CEOs" (2010): strong defense of founders staying CEO.
- E24 / B3 #1 "Why Founders Fail: The Product CEO Paradox" (2013): catalogues the ways founders fail systematically.

He resolves it explicitly in the second essay ("I am not going to rewrite that post, but I will write this post") — the prior advice still stands as a *default*, but he names the three specific failure modes that override the default. So it's less a contradiction than a layered conditional.

### C5. Promotion fairness vs. the Law of Crappy People

- B1 ch. 6 "The Right Kind of Ambition" and "Titles and Promotions" — promote based on merit, manage politics ruthlessly.
- B1 ch. 6 "Old People" — but big-company executives don't easily fit in, and you should probably hire from inside or from smaller-stage companies.

He's clearly more skeptical of the "import a senior executive from a Fortune 500" pattern than most VCs are — repeated across E20 and B1 ch. 5.

### C6. Apolitical vs. political

- B1 (2014) and B2 (2019) are almost entirely apolitical; he treats CEO craft as a universal discipline.
- E33 "Politics and the Future" (Dec 2023, with Marc Andreessen): announces a16z's first-ever explicit political program, framed as "non-partisan, one-issue voters" but signaling alignment with candidates who support technology against regulation.
- Recent (2024–2026) posts on the a16z author page ("Why Did We Raise $15B?", "$9B to Build the Future") continue this politically engaged stance.

This is the most significant evolution in his public writing. The shift from book-mode "this is timeless craft" to recent-essay-mode "the rules are changing because regulators want to choke the future" is real and worth flagging in any "perspective skill" — he became overtly political in his late writing in a way he was not in *The Hard Thing About Hard Things*.

### C7. "There's always a move" vs. "Sometimes you have to sell"

- B1 ch. 4 "The Struggle" — "There is always a move."
- B1 ch. 8 "Should You Sell Your Company?" — gives a framework for when to sell, conceding that selling is sometimes the correct move. (His criterion: sell when (a) the acquisition price is greater than the present value of the future the company can build alone, accounting for the probability of getting there.)

He's not contradicting himself so much as bounding "there's always a move" — the move can be to sell. But the rhetorical force of "there's always a move" in the Struggle essay is hard to square with the cold sell-or-don't math of the Should-You-Sell essay.

## Notes for downstream agents (other research slices)

- The **rap epigraphs are not decoration.** He uses them to anchor the chapter's mood and to signal which audience he's writing to. A skill that channels him faithfully should be able to draw on a stock of these references rather than ignore them. The Nas, Jay-Z, Kanye, Dr. Dre, DMX, and Drake quotes are the densest in his corpus.
- His biggest single influence is **Andy Grove**, not Marc Andreessen. Any "perspective skill" should weight Grove's *High Output Management* and *Only the Paranoid Survive* very heavily. Bill Campbell is the personal mentor; Grove is the intellectual one.
- His "voice" combines: (a) clinical procedural detail (the six-step layoff), (b) personal exposure ("I was a peacetime CEO for three days and wartime CEO for eight years"), (c) hip-hop epigraph as aesthetic anchor, and (d) refusal of formula ("there's no recipe"). All four are needed.
- His **moral hierarchy**: courage > intelligence; people > products > profits; lonely-difficult-right > popular-easy-wrong; execution > strategy; lead bullets > silver bullets. A perspective skill should default to those orderings.
- He has a strong **anti-MBA, anti-management-consultant** prior. "Management books written by consultants who have never managed a fruit stand" (B1 ch. 7).
- The shift from his pre-2020 writing (operational, apolitical) to post-2023 writing (political, regulatory) is the single biggest evolution in his stance and should not be smoothed away.
