# Agent 5 — Decisions and Turning Points

Tom Eisenmann is primarily a scholar, not an operator. "Decisions" here are mostly research-program choices, curriculum design, public positioning, and a small angel-investing footprint. He is conspicuously *not* a VC partner, not on operating boards, and not someone who has pivoted in or out of industry. The biggest revealed preference of his career is to stay at HBS and keep building curriculum inside one institution for 28+ years.

---

## Career timeline of major choices (banking → consulting → academia → ...)

There is no "banking" period — that is a common misread. The actual sequence is **consulting → consulting → academia**, with a deliberate decision in his late 30s to leave a successful McKinsey partnership rather than chase a corporate CEO seat or VC role.

- **~1969 (age 10):** Cleveland paperboy. Grew route from 28 to 38 customers by door-to-door selling. He cites this as his first "founder" moment. (Angel Invest Boston, 2021)
- **1979:** BA, Harvard College (Economics).
- **1979–1981:** **Booz Allen Hamilton** consultant. First job out of college. (Angel Invest Boston)
- **1981–1983:** **Harvard Business School MBA.** First return to Harvard.
- **1983–1994 (~11 years):** **McKinsey & Company.** Rose to co-head of the Media & Entertainment Practice. Directed teams on network broadcasting, cable, publishing, film production.
- **1994–1998 (age ~37–41):** Left McKinsey partnership to enroll in the HBS DBA program. He has described this publicly as his **"midlife crisis: doctorate instead of a sports car."** Original dissertation intent was strategic decision-making at large cable TV companies; he realized mid-research he was actually studying entrepreneurship. (Startupsavant)
- **1997:** Joined HBS faculty (started teaching before formally completing DBA).
- **1998:** DBA in Business Policy, Harvard Business School.
- **~2001–2003 (per his own telling):** **First HBS up-or-out promotion review — nearly fired.** Told his work was "wobbly," given two more years to fix it. A mentor told him his odds were not assured and he had good options to return to industry. His wife told him not to quit. He stayed, worked harder, was promoted. (Adam Mendler interview)
- **2008–present:** Howard H. Stevenson Professor of Business Administration.
- **2018–2021:** Wrote *Why Startups Fail* (signed contract earlier; finished during pandemic).
- **2021:** *Why Startups Fail* published by Currency (Penguin Random House) — a trade book, not a university-press monograph. Deliberate popular-audience choice.

**The decision he keeps making:** stay at HBS. He has never taken a VC partnership, never founded a company, never gone back to industry. That is the dominant revealed preference of his life.

## Research-program shifts (the toward-failure pivot, ~2013–2021)

This is the cleanest documented research pivot in his career.

### Phase 1 (1997–~2005): Internet business models / dot-com era
- Edited *Internet Business Models: Text and Cases* (McGraw-Hill/Irwin, 2001) — his first major book, written during/after the dot-com bust.
- Taught "Managing Marketspace Businesses."
- Contributed "Valuation Bubbles and Broadband Deployment" to *The Broadband Explosion* (HBS Press, 2005).
- Note: he was already studying *bubbles and failure modes*, but framed as macro/industry rather than founder pathology.

### Phase 2 (~2005–~2013): Platforms and network effects
- Best-known academic work of this era: two-sided platforms, network effects, "Strategies for Two-Sided Markets" (HBR, 2006, with Parker and Van Alstyne). This is what he was famous for *before* the failure book.
- Created **Launching Technology Ventures** course (~2010–2011) with Jeff Rayport — CBS News covered the course development in Feb 2011.
- Wrote Rent the Runway case (Nov 2011), Triangulate case (2011), Quincy Apparel (A) and (B) cases (Feb 2015).

### Phase 3 (2013–2021): The pivot to studying failure
- **Trigger event, May 2011 → 2013:** Quincy Apparel — a venture founded by two of his MBA students (Alexandra Nelson, Christina Wallace) that he advised *and* angel-invested in (June 2012 seed). Collapsed inside a year despite "strong validation" and $1M raised. Eisenmann's quote: **"I was a failure at explaining failure."**
- **Compounding trigger:** First-year MBA students challenged him: "You told us two-thirds of startups fail, but all the cases you showed us were spectacular successes. What's going on?" (Angel Invest Boston, 2021)
- **2013:** Resolved to study failure systematically.
- **2014:** Outlined the book (working title at one point: *False Start*).
- **2014–2018:** Stalled. His wife later teased him about the "delicious irony" of his book on false starts having a false start of its own.
- **2016:** Resumed work.
- **2018:** Refocused after wife's prodding.
- **2020–2021:** Pandemic gave him quiet time to finish. Book published March 2021.
- **Methodology choices:** 470-founder survey + ~20 HBS teaching cases + interviews. Notably he chose to base patterns on cases of *named, identifiable companies* rather than anonymized data — a decision with reputational risk for the founders involved.

### Phase 4 (2021–present): Institutionalizing failure as a teachable subject
- Created the **Entrepreneurial Failure** MBA elective (after the book) — a curricular bet that failure analysis is a permanent subfield, not a one-off book.

## Curriculum decisions at HBS

Eisenmann has said he takes pride in **building courses he can hand off to other professors** — a revealing operational preference: he treats course design as scalable infrastructure, not personal property. This is unusual at HBS, where faculty often guard their flagship courses.

Confirmed courses he created or co-created (with approximate dates where known):

- **The Entrepreneurial Manager (TEM)** — required first-year MBA course; he led/chaired it for years. ~900 students/year.
- **Managing Networked Business / Managing Marketspace Businesses** (~late 1990s–2000s) — his original platforms course.
- **Launching Technology Ventures (LTV)** (~2010–2011, with Jeff Rayport) — documented by CBS News in Feb 2011.
- **Scaling Technology Ventures.**
- **Entrepreneurial Sales & Marketing.**
- **Product Management 101.**
- **Making Markets** (marketplace design).
- **MBA Startup Bootcamp** (January Term).
- **Cultural Entrepreneurship in New York City** (winter immersive).
- **Silicon Valley Entrepreneurship** (winter immersive).
- **MS/MBA joint program** with Harvard SEAS — he co-led the design of this dual-degree.
- **Entrepreneurial Failure** (post-2021) — the book-driven elective.

Public count varies between 14 and 17 electives depending on the source and year (HBS, fomosapiens, Adam Mendler). The trajectory: from platforms (1997–2010) → launching/scaling tech (2010–2015) → functional craft (PM, sales, marketing) (2015–2020) → failure (2021+).

## Public stances taken under pressure

### 1. Pushing back on "fail fast" while still working from inside lean-startup-friendly Cambridge

The most publicly visible contrarian stance of his career. While Eisenmann was personally an early advocate of Steve Blank/Eric Ries lean startup methods, by 2015–2021 he was publicly arguing that "fail fast" had become a permission slip for founders to skip customer discovery — coining/cementing the **False Starts** pattern as his most-cited contribution.

Notable that he made this critique *while still endorsing the lean-startup canon overall* — he positioned it as a refinement, not a repudiation. Politically careful.

### 2. Naming companies as failures while founders were still active

By writing case studies and a trade book that named Quincy Apparel, Triangulate, Fab.com, Beepi, Jawbone, Better Place and others as failures, with named founders, he accepted reputational friction with people still in the industry. The Quincy case in particular was published Feb 2015 while the founders were still close to him. He did not pull or anonymize it. The Quincy Apparel (A) case is still live on hbs.edu (last revised March 2022).

### 3. Choosing a trade book, not a monograph

*Why Startups Fail* was published by Currency/Penguin Random House (trade), not Harvard Business Review Press or an academic press. He chose mass-market reach over scholarly prestige — a non-obvious choice for a chaired HBS professor near the end of his career.

## Things he has declined to do

These are inferences from his public record (revealed by absence), not direct refusals he has spoken about:

- **Has not become a VC partner** despite 28 years of relationships with every major Boston/SV firm.
- **Has not started a company.** He invested in his students; he did not become one of them.
- **Has not left HBS.** Several peer institutions and tech firms could have hired him.
- **Has not pulled the Quincy Apparel case** despite personal closeness to the failed founders and his own financial loss.
- **No public record of him refusing to write a case** — but his case portfolio (100+) skews heavily toward platforms, marketplaces, and named failures. He has not written extensively on, e.g., crypto, defense tech, or hard science startups, which is itself a curricular choice.

---

## Words-vs-actions gaps

**The main gap:** Eisenmann's book and lectures emphasize that founders should be willing to *quit early* when the evidence says quit (the "Speed Trap" and "Cascading Miracles" patterns). But his own most-cited personal anecdote — the 2001-ish near-firing at HBS — is a story of **refusing to quit when his mentor told him his odds were poor.** He stayed, worked harder, got promoted. He preaches data-driven exit; he practiced gritty persistence. He has not publicly reconciled this.

**Smaller gap:** He preaches "do customer discovery before you build" — yet by his own account, he angel-invested in Quincy Apparel *without* the kind of demand-validation he now demands in cases. The book is in part a self-critique, though he does not always frame it that way to founder audiences.

**Smaller gap:** He is famous for the "False Starts" critique of lean startup — but his own book had a multi-year false start (2014 outline → 2018 restart → 2020–21 completion). He has acknowledged the irony, prompted by his wife.

## The genesis of Why Startups Fail (key origin decisions)

1. **The seed (2011–2013):** Quincy Apparel collapse plus the MBA-student challenge ("all your cases are successes"). He committed to a multiyear failure-research project.
2. **The framing decision (2014):** Build the book around **patterns**, not anecdotes, and around *named companies* with *founder cooperation* — a methodology borrowed from the case method.
3. **The publisher decision (~2018):** Trade book, broad audience. Currency/Penguin Random House over HBR Press.
4. **The methodology decision:** 470-founder survey + ~20 HBS teaching cases. He chose to teach the cases first, refine them in the classroom, then write the book — meaning every case had been pressure-tested by ~80 MBAs annually before going to print.
5. **The completion decision (2020):** Used the pandemic-imposed cancellation of travel and case-writing field trips as the forcing function to finish.
6. **The post-book decision (2021+):** Spun the book into a permanent MBA elective (Entrepreneurial Failure) rather than letting the work be a one-off publication. Curriculum is his preferred long-term substrate.
