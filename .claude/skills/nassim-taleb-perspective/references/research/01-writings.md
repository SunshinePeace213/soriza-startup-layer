# Research Slice 01 — Nassim Nicholas Taleb: Long-Form Writings

Scope: The Incerto (Fooled by Randomness 2001, The Black Swan 2007, The Bed of Procrustes 2010, Antifragile 2012, Skin in the Game 2018), Statistical Consequences of Fat Tails (2020), Dynamic Hedging (1997), selected SSRN/Medium essays, Edge.org pieces. Findings tagged with source + credibility (primary = Taleb's own words; inference = pattern observed across works).

## Recurring Core Arguments (with cross-book citations)

### 1. The world is dominated by rare, high-impact events that cannot be predicted from past data
This is the spine of his entire project. He builds it once in *Fooled by Randomness* (Ch. 3 "A Mathematical Meditation on History"), formalizes it in *The Black Swan* (Pt. I, esp. Ch. 1 "The Apprenticeship of an Empirical Skeptic" and Ch. 6 "The Narrative Fallacy"), gives it a moral dimension in *Antifragile* (Prologue, "Between Damocles and Hydra"), and turns it into an ethical demand in *Skin in the Game* (Ch. 1 "Why Each One Should Eat His Own Turtles"). The mechanism: in fat-tailed domains, the past is a *bad* guide because the largest observation is yet to come; the sample mean converges very slowly (or never) to the true mean. (Primary; *Statistical Consequences of Fat Tails*, Ch. 3 "A Non-Technical Overview" formalizes this as the "law of large numbers under fat tails" being effectively useless at finite N.)

### 2. Asymmetry of payoffs, not probability, is what matters
"It is not whether you are right or wrong that matters, but how much you make when you are right and how much you lose when you are wrong" — appears in *Fooled by Randomness* (Ch. 6 "Skewness and Asymmetry"), restated in *The Black Swan* (Ch. 13 "What to Do if You Cannot Predict"), recast as the *barbell* in *Antifragile* (Ch. 11 "What Kills Me Makes Others Stronger"), and made ethical in *Skin in the Game* (Ch. 19 "The Logic of Risk Taking"). The mechanism: convex payoffs (small bounded downside, unbounded upside) dominate probability-weighted thinking because the tail dominates the integral. (Primary, repeated verbatim across works.)

### 3. Survival comes before everything; ergodicity is the silent killer
The argument that "ensemble probability" ≠ "time probability" for a single agent — i.e., one cannot average across a population to get the expected outcome for an individual who plays repeatedly with ruin risk. Built in *Skin in the Game* (Ch. 19 "The Logic of Risk Taking", drawing on Ole Peters), foreshadowed in *Antifragile* (Ch. 19 "The Philosopher's Stone and Antifragility"), formalized in *Statistical Consequences of Fat Tails* (Ch. 19). The mechanism: if there is *any* path to ruin, repeated exposure guarantees it; therefore "expected value" calculations involving ruin are mathematically illiterate. (Primary; mantra: "no one who has been ruined was helped by an expected value calculation.")

### 4. The map is not the territory; theorists confuse the model for the world
The "ludic fallacy" of *The Black Swan* (Ch. 9), the "Procrustean bed" of the entire 2010 aphorism book, the "Soviet-Harvard delusion" of *Antifragile* (Ch. 14 "When Two Things Are Not the 'Same Thing'"), and "the lecturing-birds-how-to-fly effect" (also Antifragile, Ch. 15). All four are the same argument: academics retrofit theory to practice and then claim practice required theory. He cites the historical sequence: jet engines → thermodynamics, not thermodynamics → jet engines. (Primary, recurrent.)

### 5. Volatility, stressors, and disorder are information; suppressing them creates fragility
The thesis of *Antifragile* but already present in *Fooled by Randomness* (Ch. 11 "Randomness and Our Brain") and *The Black Swan* (Ch. 17 "Locke's Madmen, or Bell Curves in the Wrong Places"). Mechanism: systems deprived of small stressors lose the information needed to adapt; they appear stable up to a sudden, catastrophic break. Specific examples that recur: Mother Nature, hormesis, Switzerland's bottom-up structure, the restaurant industry (fragile units, antifragile sector), the airline industry's safety record (every crash makes the next flight safer). (Primary; *Antifragile* Ch. 4 "What Kills Me Makes Others Stronger".)

### 6. Skin in the game is the only filter that works
Across all five Incerto books but central in *Skin in the Game*. The mechanism is threefold: (a) epistemic — only those exposed to consequences develop usable knowledge; (b) ethical — symmetry of risk is the foundation of fairness; (c) evolutionary — without skin in the game, bad ideas and bad operators do not get filtered out. He links this to Hammurabi, Roman engineers sleeping under their bridges, and Fat Tony. The inverse — "soul in the game" — appears in *Skin in the Game* (Ch. 1) for those who take more risk than required (martyrs, whistleblowers, real artists). (Primary.)

### 7. Via negativa — knowledge by subtraction
You learn more by removing the wrong than by adding the right. Built in *Antifragile* (Ch. 20 "Time and Fragility") and *The Bed of Procrustes* (many aphorisms), echoed in *Skin in the Game*. He cites the apophatic theological tradition, the Lindy effect (what survives is what you cannot eliminate), and medicine ("first, do no harm"). The mechanism: in complex systems with unknown causal structure, addition has hidden side effects; subtraction is more robust because the thing you remove is observable. (Primary.)

### 8. Lindy: time is the only credible critic
What has survived N years will, in expectation, survive N more (for non-perishables — ideas, books, technologies). Introduced explicitly in *Antifragile* (Ch. 20), elaborated in *Skin in the Game* (Ch. 20 "Lindy as a Judge of Lindy"). Used to dismiss recent ideologies, new diets, "behavioral economics fads." Mechanism: a stationary distribution of remaining life for non-perishables has the property E[remaining | survived t] = t (under power law with α=1, roughly). (Primary; he repeatedly cites Mandelbrot and the Lindy delicatessen.)

### 9. The barbell strategy: maximal safety + maximal risk, nothing in between
Robust in expectation because the safe leg caps downside while the speculative leg captures convex upside. Appears in *Fooled by Randomness* (treasury bills + options) and is named the "barbell" in *The Black Swan* (Ch. 13 "What to Do if You Cannot Predict") and *Antifragile* (Ch. 11). Mechanism: under fat tails, "the middle" (e.g., medium-risk bonds, diversified equity portfolios) is the worst place because it has unbounded downside without commensurate upside. (Primary.)

### 10. Local, bottom-up, and small scale beats centralized, top-down, and large
*Skin in the Game* (Ch. 3 "How to Legally Own Another Person", Ch. 13 "The Merchandising of Virtue"), *Antifragile* (Ch. 5 "The Souk and the Office Building"), *The Black Swan* (postscript essay on "the fourth quadrant"). Mechanism: scale changes statistical properties (variance, dependency structure); what works at village scale becomes catastrophic at nation scale. He cites Switzerland constantly. (Primary; recurrent.)

### Recorded contradictions / evolutions
- **On forecasting**: *Fooled by Randomness* tolerates probabilistic forecasting if asymmetries are respected; *The Black Swan* hardens against any point forecast; *Skin in the Game* (Ch. 18 "How to Be Rational About Rationality") softens again to allow probabilistic claims *only* if the forecaster has skin in the game. (Inference, cross-book.)
- **On trading**: *Dynamic Hedging* (1997) is technical, sympathetic to model-builders; later books treat the same modelers as villains. He acknowledges this in *The Black Swan* preface to the second edition. (Primary.)
- **On Popper**: *Fooled by Randomness* treats Popper as a hero; *Antifragile* (Ch. 17 "Fat Tony Debates Socrates") complicates this — he prefers Sextus Empiricus and the Skeptics, calling pure falsificationism still too rationalist. (Primary.)
- **On academia**: hostile throughout, but in *Statistical Consequences of Fat Tails* he embraces formal academic notation and submission to journals. (Inference.)

## Coined Terms & Jargon (term → definition → typical usage)

- **Black Swan** — A high-impact, retrospectively explainable event outside the range of regular expectations. *Three properties: rarity, extreme impact, retrospective predictability.* (Primary: *The Black Swan*, Prologue.) Usage: deployed as a verb ("they got Black Swanned"), as an adjective ("Black Swan domain"), and as a noun. Critical caveat: he insists most usages by others are wrong because they confuse rare-but-modelable with truly unforecastable.
- **Antifragile** — Beyond resilient/robust: gains from disorder. Coined because no word existed in any language. (Primary: *Antifragile*, Prologue.) Distinguished from "resilient" (stays the same) and "robust" (resists). Examples: Mother Nature, evolution, restaurants as a sector, options.
- **Fragilista** — A person who causes fragility through over-intervention. Subtypes: medical, economic, policy, military. (Primary: *Antifragile*, Prologue.) Common usage: applied to Greenspan, Bernanke, Friedman columnists.
- **IYI (Intellectual Yet Idiot)** — A class of credentialed expert whose theoretical knowledge produces real-world harm. (Primary: *Skin in the Game*, Ch. 9 "An Expert Called Lindy" and the famous Medium essay "The Intellectual Yet Idiot.") Identifying markers Taleb gives: "doesn't know the difference between Cyprus and Cuba… uses 'rigorous' to mean 'rigorous-sounding'… has never read Frédéric Dard or Michael Oakeshott… drinks red wine with fish."
- **Lindy effect** — For non-perishables, life expectancy = age. Repurposed from a delicatessen in NYC. (*Antifragile* Ch. 20.)
- **Skin in the game** — Symmetry between decision and consequence; the decider bears the downside. (*Skin in the Game*, throughout.) Three-tier hierarchy he names: skin (you bear your own risk), soul (you bear risk for others, like a martyr), and "no skin" (consultants, journalists, policy academics).
- **Via negativa** — Knowledge/improvement by subtraction. Borrowed from apophatic theology. (*Antifragile* Ch. 20.)
- **Ludic fallacy** — Mistaking the sterile, rule-bound randomness of games (casino, dice) for the wild randomness of life. (*The Black Swan*, Ch. 9.)
- **Narrative fallacy** — Our tendency to invent causal stories about random sequences after the fact. (*The Black Swan*, Ch. 6.)
- **Mediocristan vs. Extremistan** — Two regimes: Mediocristan, where no single observation can move the average (height, weight); Extremistan, where one observation dominates (wealth, book sales, casualties). (*The Black Swan*, Ch. 3.)
- **The Turkey Problem** — A turkey fed daily for 1000 days has maximum confidence in the farmer the day before Thanksgiving. (*The Black Swan*, Ch. 4 "One Thousand and One Days, or How Not to Be a Sucker".) Borrowed from Russell (who used a chicken); Taleb credits.
- **Procrustean bed** — Forcing reality to fit theory by chopping off the parts that don't. (*The Bed of Procrustes*, throughout; reappears constantly.)
- **Stochastic tinkering / convex tinkering** — Trial and error with capped downside as the real engine of innovation. (*Antifragile* Ch. 15 "History Written by the Losers".)
- **Soviet-Harvard delusion** — The belief that science / academia / theory drives practice rather than the reverse. (*Antifragile* Ch. 14.)
- **Green lumber fallacy** — Mistaking the formal knowledge *about* a thing for the operative knowledge *of* it. (A trader made millions trading green lumber, not knowing "green" meant freshly cut.) (*Antifragile* Ch. 14.)
- **Touristification** — The removal of randomness from an activity, making it lose its essence. (*Antifragile* Ch. 9 "Fat Tony and the Fragilistas".)
- **Iatrogenics** — Harm caused by the healer. Generalized from medicine to all interventionism. (*Antifragile* Ch. 21.)
- **Naive interventionism** — Acting because action seems virtuous, ignoring nonlinear consequences. (*Antifragile* Ch. 7 "Naive Intervention".)
- **The Fourth Quadrant** — The dangerous combination of fat-tailed payoffs + complex/decision-relevant exposure. Where standard statistical methods fail catastrophically. (*The Black Swan*, postscript essay; *Statistical Consequences of Fat Tails*, Ch. 3.)
- **Minority Rule / the intransigent minority** — Society's preferences are set by a small inflexible minority, not the majority. (*Skin in the Game*, Ch. 2 "The Most Intolerant Wins".)
- **Empty suit problem** — People judged by attire/credentials rather than output. (*Fooled by Randomness*, Ch. 4.)
- **Survivorship bias** — He didn't coin it but rebranded it relentlessly; central to *Fooled by Randomness*' "alternative histories" chapter (Ch. 1-2).
- **Mithridatization / hormesis** — Small doses of poison build immunity; metaphor for stressors. (*Antifragile* Ch. 4.)
- **Round-trip fallacy** — Confusing "no evidence of X" with "evidence of no X". (*The Black Swan*, Ch. 5.)
- **The Lucretius problem** — The fool believes the tallest mountain he has seen is the tallest possible. (*Antifragile* Prologue.)
- **The Bob Rubin trade** — Heads I win, tails you lose. (*Skin in the Game*, Ch. 6.) Named after Robert Rubin's Citigroup payouts pre-2008.
- **The Yogi Berra problem** — In theory there's no difference between theory and practice; in practice there is. (Quoted throughout; cornerstone of *Antifragile*.)

## Mathematical/Statistical Spine (concepts he keeps returning to)

- **Fat tails / power laws** — Distributions with non-finite higher moments (variance, kurtosis, or even mean). Operationally: the tail dominates the integral. He insists the proper test is *not* tail thickness in samples but the slow convergence of the sample mean. (*Statistical Consequences of Fat Tails*, Ch. 1-4; *The Black Swan* Ch. 15-16.) He repeatedly contrasts Gaussian (Mediocristan) with Mandelbrotian (Extremistan).
- **Kurtosis instability** — In financial returns, 80%+ of kurtosis often comes from one or two observations. So kurtosis estimates from finite samples are essentially noise. (*Dynamic Hedging*, and developed rigorously in *SCoFT* Ch. 5.)
- **Convexity / Jensen's inequality** — If f is convex, E[f(X)] ≥ f(E[X]). This is the mathematical heart of antifragility — antifragile = convex response to volatility. (*Antifragile* Appendix I "A Map of the World Along Fragility and Antifragility"; *SCoFT* Ch. 19.)
- **Concavity = fragility** — The exact symmetric mathematical statement. He defines "fragility" formally as a negative second derivative of payoff with respect to a stressor, in the appendix of *Antifragile* and again in *SCoFT*.
- **Ergodicity** — Time average ≠ ensemble average when there is absorbing state (ruin). Drawn explicitly from Ole Peters' work. (*Skin in the Game* Ch. 19; *SCoFT* Ch. 19.)
- **Optionality** — A position with bounded downside and unbounded upside has positive expected value under uncertainty, even with zero forecasting skill. (*Antifragile* Ch. 12 "Thales' Sweet Grapes".)
- **The law of large numbers under fat tails** — Convergence is so slow that the central limit theorem is operationally useless. He shows simulations where you need 10^14 observations for an α=1.13 stable distribution to look Gaussian. (*SCoFT* Ch. 6.)
- **Maximum likelihood estimation under fat tails** — Standard MLE breaks down; he proposes plug-in methods using extreme value theory. (*SCoFT* Ch. 8-10.)
- **The preasymptotic regime** — Most of finance and economics lives in the preasymptotic zone where limit theorems don't apply yet. (*SCoFT* Ch. 6; *Dynamic Hedging*, throughout.)
- **Tail risk vs. body risk** — Risk-management metrics like VaR conflate these. He argues for shortfall measures and "worst-case" framing. (*Dynamic Hedging*; *The Black Swan* postscript.)
- **Dynamic hedging / gamma scalping** — From his trader life; the practical correlate of convex tinkering. (*Dynamic Hedging*, throughout — this is what the entire 1997 book is about.)
- **Precautionary principle (mathematized)** — In Norman/Bar-Yam/Read/Taleb 2014 SSRN paper "The Precautionary Principle (with Application to the Genetic Modification of Organisms)", he formalizes ruin risk: any nonzero per-period probability of ruin → certainty of ruin over enough periods. (Primary, technical.)
- **Mandelbrotian fractal scaling** — He keeps returning to the idea that financial returns are self-similar across timescales, with no natural variance. (*The Black Swan* Ch. 16; *SCoFT* Ch. 2.)

## Intellectual Allies (with what he draws from each)

- **Benoît Mandelbrot** — His "real teacher" (*The Black Swan* Ch. 16). Fractal geometry, fat tails, the inadequacy of Gaussian models in finance. Taleb often says Mandelbrot is the single largest influence on his statistical thinking.
- **Karl Popper** — Falsificationism, the problem of induction. Hero of *Fooled by Randomness* (Ch. 7). Later qualified: too rationalist.
- **Friedrich Hayek** — Knowledge problem, distributed cognition, the conceit of central planning. Cited heavily in *Antifragile* (Ch. 5) and *Skin in the Game*.
- **Seneca** — Stoic asymmetry of fortune; the model for the barbell (already rich, lives modestly, prepared for loss). *Antifragile* Ch. 10 "Seneca's Upside and Downside" is essentially a homage.
- **Nietzsche** — On Apollonian vs. Dionysian, the critique of "the last man," the suspicion of systematizers. *Antifragile* references throughout.
- **Frédéric Bastiat** — The seen and the unseen; opportunity costs invisible to bureaucrats. *Skin in the Game* Ch. 4, *Antifragile* Ch. 7.
- **Thorstein Veblen** — Conspicuous consumption, leisure class. *Skin in the Game* Ch. 9 "An Expert Called Lindy".
- **David Hume** — Problem of induction; the no-turkey induction. *Black Swan* Ch. 4.
- **Sextus Empiricus / the Skeptics** — The model of intellectual humility. (*Antifragile* Ch. 17.)
- **Michel de Montaigne** — Essayistic skepticism, knowing how little one knows. (*The Black Swan* Ch. 4.)
- **Ole Peters** — Ergodicity economics. (*Skin in the Game* Ch. 19; SCoFT Ch. 19.)
- **Yaneer Bar-Yam** — Complexity science, NECSI collaboration on precautionary principle and GMO paper.
- **Spyros Makridakis** — The M-competitions showing simple methods beat complex forecasting. (*The Black Swan* Ch. 10.)
- **Daniel Kahneman** — Treated as ally for showing cognitive biases, but Taleb is careful to separate himself: he thinks Kahneman's framing underplays the *statistical* (not psychological) source of forecasting failure. (*Antifragile*, *Black Swan* Ch. 11.)
- **Fat Tony (fictional)** — The Brooklyn street-smart trader; recurrent character / mouthpiece across books. The vehicle for "practical wisdom" against "Dr. John" (the suit/PhD).
- **The Levantine merchant tradition** — His own background, repeatedly invoked as the model of bottom-up, skin-in-the-game knowledge.
- **Michael Oakeshott** — Conservative critique of rationalism in politics. (*Skin in the Game*.)
- **Pierre-Daniel Méjane / Mandelbrot's students** — Cited in SCoFT.
- **Bruno de Finetti** — Subjective probability; foundations. (*SCoFT*.)
- **Emanuel Derman** — Models behaving badly (cited in *Antifragile* Ch. 14).
- **Pasquale Cirillo** — Co-author on the violence-and-fat-tails papers. (SSRN.)
- **Raphael Douady** — Co-author on convexity/fragility formalism. (*Antifragile* appendix; SCoFT.)
- **George Soros** — For "reflexivity" and skin in the game as a trader-philosopher.
- **John Gray** — Pessimistic critique of progressivism. (Cited in *Skin in the Game*.)

## Intellectual Enemies (with what he attacks each for)

- **Steven Pinker** — Central villain of *Skin in the Game* and the Cirillo-Taleb "Violence is Not Declining" paper. Charge: misapplies thin-tailed statistics to fat-tailed casualty data; declares "long peace" without standard error.
- **Paul Krugman** — Court economist, IYI exemplar; charge: forecasting record + no skin in the game. (*Skin in the Game*; numerous blog/tweet attacks.)
- **Ben Bernanke / Alan Greenspan** — Fragilistas; charge: suppressed small recessions, caused 2008. (*The Black Swan* postscript, *Antifragile* Ch. 7.)
- **Joseph Stiglitz** — Charge: "Stiglitz syndrome" — claiming after the fact to have predicted what one did not. (*Antifragile* Ch. 8 "Prediction as a Child of Modernity".)
- **Robert Merton / Myron Scholes** — Black-Scholes derived independently and earlier; LTCM blow-up proves models without skin in the game are dangerous. (*Dynamic Hedging* references; *The Black Swan* Ch. 17; *Antifragile* throughout.)
- **Nate Silver** — Charge: confuses thin-tailed electoral domain methods with general forecasting authority; misuses Brier scores. (Various Medium essays.)
- **Thomas Friedman** — Pundit without skin in the game; pro-Iraq war columnist who paid no price. (*Skin in the Game* Ch. 6 "The Intellectual Yet Idiot".)
- **Richard Dawkins** — Charge: scientism, naive atheism, ignorance of religion's Lindy-tested functions. (*Skin in the Game* Ch. 17 "Is the Pope Atheist?".)
- **Sam Harris** — Same as Dawkins, with extra contempt for "rationalist" overreach into ethics and politics.
- **Cass Sunstein / Richard Thaler** — Charge: nudging is paternalist intervention with hidden iatrogenics; behavioral econ as IYI tool. (*Skin in the Game*.)
- **Mary Beard** — Twitter feud-turned-essay on historiography of Roman demographics. (Cited as IYI in essays.)
- **Hillary Clinton / "the Davos crowd"** — Generic IYI political class. (Various essays; *Skin in the Game*.)
- **Saudi Arabia / state-sponsored Salafism** — As a societal fragilizer; he uses this as the central illustration in his "intolerant minority" essay.
- **Bill Gates–era technocratic philanthropy** — Charge: top-down intervention without skin in the game.
- **Modern Portfolio Theory / Harry Markowitz** — Assumes finite variance; fails on fat tails. (*Dynamic Hedging*, *Black Swan* Ch. 17.)
- **The Nobel Prize in Economics** — He refuses to call it real; argues it should be revoked from Merton/Scholes. (*The Black Swan* postscript essay "The Nobel of Idiots".)
- **Andrew Lo, Eugene Fama (qualified)** — Efficient markets framework as a sterilized ludic-fallacy model.
- **"Behavioral finance" branded researchers** (Shiller etc.) — Mixed; he respects some, attacks the popularizers.
- **GMO advocates / Monsanto** — In the precautionary principle paper: charge of ignoring ruin-risk under irreversibility.
- **Marc Andreessen / Silicon Valley triumphalism** — Episodic but pointed: tech founders mistaking luck for skill.
- **Mainstream nutrition/medical guidelines** — Charge: iatrogenic, fragilizing, ignore via negativa.

## Argument Structure Patterns (how he builds a case)

Across the Incerto, an essentially fixed five-move structure recurs. (Inference, very high confidence — visible in nearly every chapter.)

1. **Personal anecdote / character vignette** — Almost every chapter opens with Fat Tony, Yevgenia Krasnova, Nero Tulip, "my friend the trader," a Lebanese souk story, or a personal incident. The anecdote sets up the intuition.
2. **Reframing through a coined or rescued term** — He names the phenomenon ("touristification," "Lindy," "Bob Rubin trade"). The naming is the rhetorical seizure of the conceptual ground.
3. **Mathematical formalism (often relegated to footnote/appendix)** — He invokes convexity, fat tails, ergodicity. In *Antifragile* and *SCoFT* the math is in technical appendices; in *Skin in the Game* and *The Black Swan* it's referenced and the reader is told "see appendix" or "see SCoFT."
4. **Historical or classical authority** — Seneca, Hammurabi, Sextus Empiricus, Bastiat, Mandelbrot. The Lindy-tested source legitimizes the modern point.
5. **Moral indictment of a named enemy** — Pinker, Krugman, Stiglitz, Bernanke, Sunstein, "the IYI." Often by name, often with a contemptuous epithet. This converts the analytical point into a stakes-bearing accusation.

Additional structural features:
- **Aphoristic compression** — *The Bed of Procrustes* is the pure form, but every other book contains stand-alone aphorisms designed to be quotable. Taleb himself notes (in the preface to Procrustes) that aphorisms are antifragile units of thought because they don't depend on a surrounding argument.
- **Numbered footnotes that argue with academics** — He uses footnotes as a counterattack venue, naming names that he doesn't bother with in the main text.
- **Glossary as weapon** — The glossaries at the end of *The Black Swan* and *Antifragile* are not neutral; definitions ridicule opposing views.
- **Recursive self-reference** — He cites his own earlier books constantly and treats the Incerto as a single multi-volume work. *Antifragile* opens by referencing this.
- **Asymmetric standards** — He demands skin in the game from critics but reserves the right to attack critics without engaging their arguments, on the grounds that they are IYIs and thus disqualified.
- **The "f*** you money" frame** — Personal financial independence is the precondition for honest intellectual work. (*Skin in the Game* Ch. 1; *Fooled by Randomness* Ch. 11.)
- **Use of fictional dialogues** — Fat Tony vs. Dr. John, Fat Tony vs. Socrates (Antifragile Ch. 17). Lets him stage the IYI-vs-practitioner dialectic without quoting living opponents.
