## Era 1: Early life and education (pre-1983)

Almost no public information about Eisenmann's childhood, family, or undergraduate experience. He is American; speaks/writes in a calm, lecturing register; describes himself as a "case-study guy" who became fascinated by network industries through media work.

- Birth: ~1957 (inferred; unverified)
- 1979 — BA Economics, Harvard College
- 1979–1981 (approx.) — Two-year work gap typical of HBS MBA admits; specific employment unknown
- 1981–1983 — MBA, Harvard Business School

Thought signature emerging: economics-first, allergy to "great founder" narratives, comfort with quantitative analysis of business models.

## Era 2: Pre-academic career — McKinsey years (~1984–1995)

Eleven years at McKinsey & Company. Rose to **co-head of the Media & Entertainment Practice**. Worked at the convergence moment of cable TV, telecom, and the early commercial Internet (1990–1995).

Key formative experiences (inferred from his later research):
- Watching networked media businesses (cable, broadcast, magazines) before "platform" was a vocabulary word
- Seeing real strategic failures inside large incumbents — likely seeded his later distrust of "growth-at-all-costs" tropes
- Working with founders / executives in the pre-bubble Internet era

Why this matters: Eisenmann is unusual among HBS entrepreneurship faculty in having **no operating role at a startup**. His authority comes from consulting pattern-recognition + case-method scholarship, not from having founded a company. This shapes everything downstream — he is the diagnostician, not the operator.

## Era 3: Early HBS career (1995–2005)

- 1995–1998 — DBA program at HBS; dissertation in Business Policy
- 1997 — Joined HBS faculty (likely as assistant professor while finishing dissertation)
- 1998 — DBA awarded
- Late 1990s–early 2000s — Wrote first wave of cases on networked / platform businesses (AOL, Hotmail-era network effects)
- Around 2003–2006 — Builds "Managing Networked Businesses" MBA elective and module notes
- Tenure milestone (unverified year; likely 2003–2005)

Thought signature: **platform economics, network effects, two-sided markets**.

## Era 4: Platform research era (~2006–2012)

The "platforms" decade. Eisenmann's most-cited work comes from this period.

- 2006 — "Strategies for Two-Sided Markets" with Geoffrey Parker and Marshall Van Alstyne (*HBR*, Oct 2006). This becomes one of the foundational pieces of platform-strategy literature; reprinted in HBR's *10 Must Reads on Platforms and Ecosystems*.
- 2006–2008 — "Managing Networked Businesses" course overview, summary, and platform-evolution module notes.
- ~2008 — Begins writing/teaching about "winner-take-all" dynamics in networked markets.
- 2009 — Launches **Launching Technology Ventures (LTV)** MBA elective. This is a pivot: from studying *mature* platforms (eBay, MasterCard, Microsoft) to studying *new* platform startups.
- 2011 — Cases on Dropbox, Aardvark. He's now teaching startups specifically.
- 2011 — "Hypothesis-Driven Entrepreneurship: The Lean Startup" with Eric Ries and Sarah Dillard. **Major intellectual handshake with Steve Blank / Eric Ries lean startup movement.**

Thought turning point #1 (~2009–2011): from platform strategy → startup methodology. Eisenmann starts treating the startup as the unit of analysis instead of the industry/platform.

## Era 5: Lean startup / hypothesis-driven entrepreneurship era (~2012–2017)

- 2011–2015 — Quincy Apparel saga. Two of his ex-students raise money, build, fail. He had invested. Case (A) February 2015. Cofounders' postmortem in case (B).
- 2012–2017 — Continues teaching LTV; refines the "experimentation-machine" model of startups.
- 2015–2017 — Writes background notes on business model analysis, customer discovery, MVPs, pivot decisions.
- ~2017 — Takes on Faculty Co-Chair of HBS Rock Center for Entrepreneurship; helps design MS/MBA Engineering Sciences joint degree.

Thought turning point #2 (~2015 — Quincy Apparel postmortem): triggers his **disenchantment with naive lean-startup orthodoxy**. He starts to believe that "fail fast" and "launch before you're ready" themselves *cause* a class of failures (which becomes "False Starts" in his eventual book).

## Era 6: Failure analysis era (~2017–present)

- ~2018 — Independent study / advisory project with MBA Class of 2018 students researching specific failed startups. This becomes the empirical engine for the book.
- 2018–2020 — Surveys ~470 founders/CEOs of failed ventures; interviews ~100. Develops the **diamond-and-square framework** (diamond = opportunity: CVP, tech/ops, GTM, profit formula; square = resources: founders, team, investors, partners).
- Mar 30, 2021 — *Why Startups Fail: A New Roadmap for Entrepreneurial Success* (Currency/Crown). Six failure patterns: Good Idea Bad Bedfellows; False Starts; False Positives; Speed Traps; Help Wanted; Cascading Miracles.
- May 2021 — HBR feature "Why Start-ups Fail."
- 2021–2023 — Heavy podcast and speaking tour (NFX, Angel Invest Boston, Seedcamp, Pear VC, STVP, Stanford ETL, etc.).
- 2022–2024 — Builds out the MBA elective **"Entrepreneurial Failure"** — the only HBS elective dedicated to startup failure as a subject.
- 2024 — "LinkedIn: Project InVersion" case (April 2024). MS/MBA "Technology Venture Immersion" and "Launch Lab" courses listed as core.
- 2025 — CV revised January 2025; remains Stevenson Professor and Peter O. Crisp Faculty Chair at Harvard Innovation Labs.

Thought turning point #3 (~2017–2021): from "lean startup is the answer" → **"lean startup is one of multiple failure modes."** Eisenmann's most distinctive late-career claim is that prescriptive entrepreneurship advice (fail fast, get big fast, listen to customers) systematically *creates* failure when applied without context. This is the intellectual core of *Why Startups Fail* and what distinguishes him from Ries, Blank, and Andy Rachleff.

**Themes of his 2025–2026 public commentary**:
1. **AI lowers build cost, raises failure rate.** A core repeated point: easier to build means more startups means more bad ideas means higher absolute and percentage failure.
2. **Novelty-trap AI products.** Single-feature wrappers on LLMs lack moats (integration, proprietary data, habit).
3. **Validation still matters.** Despite AI speed, founders still need real customer-pain verification — the diamond's customer-value-proposition corner has not gotten easier.
4. **Investor risk in AI era.** Record competitor counts per AI product idea.
5. **Pedagogy itself is changing.** Active research focus has shifted toward how to teach entrepreneurship in a world where AI scaffolds the experimentation loop.

**What is NOT happening**:
- No second book announced.
- No retirement/emeritus announcement.
- No public X/Twitter feud or sharp position-taking (consistent with his calm-professor persona).
- He has stepped back from teaching the front-line LTV course but remains Unit Head and Rock Center co-chair — strategic / institutional rather than front-of-classroom now.
