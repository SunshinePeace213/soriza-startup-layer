# Premortem — Eisenmann's six failure patterns, run prospectively

The brief is a GO/NO-GO. Before stamping it, run a **premortem**: assume it's 18 months later and this
venture failed — which pattern killed it? The discipline is Tom Eisenmann's (*Why Startups Fail*): no
single-cause stories ("ran out of cash" is the loss of blood, not the wound), and failure has a
*taxonomy*. Read this in Step 6. Source: `/tom-eisenmann-perspective`; the six patterns as a fast
checklist also live there in `references/failure-patterns.md`.

Eisenmann's own rule is that he diagnoses *retrospectively* and refuses to predict a named living
company as doomed. The premortem honors that: you are not predicting THIS venture dies — you are
running the taxonomy as a checklist to surface the *risks* each pattern names, so they are on record
before the founder signs, not discovered in the post-mortem.

## The six patterns (assess each — which apply, which are mitigated, which are live)

**Early-stage (the three that bite before scale):**

1. **Good Idea, Bad Bedfellows** — right opportunity, wrong people. Founder-market fit gap, co-founder
   ambiguity, a missing senior/domain hire, outsourcing a core competency. *Ask:* does the team (incl.
   the solo founder + their stated edge, read from the profile) actually fit this problem?
2. **False Starts** — building before customer discovery; the bias-for-action trap. *Ask:* did real
   discovery precede this concept, or is the brief crystallizing an assumed problem? (If interviews and
   the Discovery Read are upstream, this is *mitigated* — name it as such.)
3. **False Positives** — misreading early-adopter delight as mainstream signal. Friendly/warm-intro
   users love it; the mainstream segment is untested. *Ask:* is the validation skewed to a friendly
   slice? What in the PoC tests a cold (non-network) user?

**Late-stage (named now so they aren't a surprise at scale):**

4. **Speed Trap** — hypergrowth + LTV/CAC squeeze + organizational unraveling (Speed and Scope racing
   ahead of Staff, Structure, Shared values). Usually pre-scale-irrelevant at step 8 — say so, but flag
   if the GTM thesis is "grow at all costs."
5. **Help Wanted** — the senior talent or capital missing at the moment it's needed (the VP hire, the
   regulated-domain expert, the bridge round). *Ask:* which senior gap or capital need would bite at
   scale? Name it now.
6. **Cascading Miracles** — the venture needs several low-probability breakthroughs to *all* land. The
   honest move is to **multiply the probabilities**: four things at 60% each is 0.6⁴ ≈ 13%, not 60%.
   *Ask:* how many independent things must all go right (data access AND distribution AND WTP AND …)?
   Multiply them and state the combined odds, surfaced not hidden.

## How to write the premortem section

For each of the six, one or two lines: does it apply here, is it mitigated, or is it a live risk —
tied to the evidence. When two patterns compound ("False Positives compounded by Help Wanted"), say so;
that is honest, not indecisive. The premortem feeds the Decision: a live, unmitigated early-stage
pattern (especially False Starts or False Positives) pushes toward NO-GO or more discovery, not a GO
with a caveat. Do not launder a live pattern into "we'll handle it."
