## Quoted/paraphrased "under pressure" moments

These are the moments where an interviewer pushed and Eisenmann had to think on his feet.

### Moment 1: Sal Daher pushes him on life-sciences applicability (Angel Invest Boston)
Sal Daher is a hard-tech / biotech angel. He pushes Eisenmann on whether the failure patterns generalize to biotech.

**[P] Eisenmann response (paraphrased from extraction):** "Life sciences is not my strong suit, but I've got a whole bunch of professional reasons to learn more."

**[I] Pattern**: He *concedes the boundary of his expertise immediately* rather than bullshitting. This is the academic register intact under pressure — he names his lane (consumer/B2B tech), defers the rest. Compare to operator-VCs who would extrapolate. This is his most reliable tell.

### Moment 2: NFX hosts press on "engineers" as a failure type
Asked about why founders launch too soon, Eisenmann gets sharp and named-and-shamed a profession:

**[P] "Engineers are particularly vulnerable to this because they want to build."** (NFX)
**[P] "You've wasted a cycle. You've wasted four months in order to save four weeks and that's just a bad trade."** (NFX, on false starts)

**[I] Pattern**: This is one of his rare *animated* moments. He gets crisper, more declarative when the topic touches builder-psychology vs market discovery. The numerical specificity ("four months to save four weeks") is rehearsed — he repeats variants across appearances.

### Moment 3: Jerry Colonna (Reboot) pushes on the emotional cost of failure
Colonna is known for pulling guests into vulnerable territory. With Eisenmann, the curated highlight surfaces this:

**[P] "Failure of the enterprise is not a failure of me."** (Reboot #146, via show notes)

**[I] Pattern**: He generalizes the personal — instead of saying "*my* failure with Quincy didn't make me a failure," he offers a depersonalized maxim. He shares without disclosing. Contrast with operators on Colonna's show who weep on tape. Eisenmann *converts emotion into pedagogy* even when prompted to dwell in it. **This is the defining contradiction**: the academic register holds even when Colonna invites the blunter podcast register.

### Moment 4: Founder responsibility (NFX)
When the host invites him to soften the line, he doubles down:

**[P] "If you're going to blame your co-founder, well, you chose that co-founder."** (NFX)

**[I] Pattern**: This is the bluntest single line in the corpus. Founder-as-decider is non-negotiable for him; he doesn't qualify it the way he qualifies most claims. Watch for this when applying his perspective: the *one* place he refuses the "it depends" hedge is co-founder choice.

### Moment 5: Business of Business — on his own intellectual humility
Asked about the origin of the book:

**[P] "I was a failure at explaining failure. So it set me on a quest to learn everything I could."** (Business of Business)
**[P] "I could point to lots of potential reasons why this venture had failed, but I couldn't pinpoint the root cause."** (Adam Mendler)
**[P] "Was the playbook for entrepreneurial success I was teaching flawed, or maybe missing something?"** (Adam Mendler)

**[I] Pattern**: He volunteers his own past inadequacy as the book's origin. This is rehearsed-but-genuine — the line is too clean to be spontaneous, but the underlying admission is real. He owns that the HBS playbook he'd taught for 20+ years was incomplete.

## Recurring analogies and reference domains

### Forensic / medical (his strongest signature)
**[P] "It's like saying a victim died from loss of blood. Was that due to a gunshot wound?"** (Adam Mendler, on why "ran out of cash" isn't a real diagnosis)

**[I] Pattern**: He repeatedly frames startup post-mortems as *forensic* or *epidemiological* — looking for root cause beneath proximate cause. The medical/autopsy frame is his single most-reused metaphor. It also signals his epistemology: he wants typologies and pattern-matching, not single-shot diagnoses. The book title literally is *Why Startups Fail* — singular question, multiple etiologies.

### Sports (false starts)
**[P/S] Swimming, track, horse racing, auto racing** as analogies for "jumping the gun." (Angel Invest Boston)

**[I] Pattern**: Sports analogies show up specifically around *timing* failures (premature launch, scaling too fast, "ready fire aim"). Notable that he never reaches for team sports — always individual or single-race events. **He doesn't think in team-sport metaphors. He thinks in race metaphors.** That fits his framing of the founder as the decisive agent.

### Engineering / prototyping
**[P/S] Paper prototypes, conceptual models** invoked as pre-engineering validation. (Angel Invest Boston)

**[I] Pattern**: His engineering analogies all argue *against* premature build. The discipline of design-before-build is his most-deployed engineering frame. He uses engineering metaphors to *restrain* engineers.

### Grief / Kübler-Ross
**[P/S]** He applies the five stages of grief to founder psychology post-failure. (Angel Invest Boston)
**[P] "Most founders I find have this period of intense emotion, sometimes depression, and it can take weeks or months to snap out of that."** (Business of Business)

**[I] Pattern**: When the conversation goes psychological, he reaches for *clinical psychology* (Kübler-Ross, "rumination-distraction cycle," "fundamental attribution error"). Never reaches for spirituality, never reaches for Buddhist/meditation framing (despite Colonna's prompts). His psychology is textbook cognitive-behavioral.

### Moby Dick (one-off, attributed to Marc Andreessen)
**[P/S]** Quoting Andreessen: startups chasing big-co partnerships are Ahab chasing the whale. (Pear)

**[I] Pattern**: He *cites* literary/cultural analogies he likes but rarely originates them. He repeats Andreessen and others; his own contributions are clinical.

### What he does NOT reach for
- **Military metaphors**: virtually absent. No "war room," no "trenches."
- **Religious/spiritual**: absent.
- **Founder-as-artist**: absent. He never romanticizes founders.
- **Team-sports**: absent (as noted above).

## Position changes (then → now)

Eisenmann is *constitutionally* cautious about saying "I changed my mind" — he prefers "I learned something new." But several shifts surface across the conversations.

### Shift 1: Lean Startup is not a complete playbook
**Then ([S], pre-2015 HBS teaching)**: Lean Startup methodology + Customer Development was the central playbook taught.
**Now [P]**: He critiques a "crucial flaw in Lean Startup methodology" — specifically that it under-weights *upfront customer research* before MVP and over-weights "ready, fire, aim." (NFX, Substack Entrepreneurship Podcast summary)
**Citation**: He pegs the trigger to Quincy Apparel: validated MVP, raised $1M, launched, failed anyway.

### Shift 2: Founder-quality determinism
**Then [S]**: "Bet on the jockey, not the horse" — investor wisdom he repeated for years.
**Now [P]**: He explicitly counters: success requires alignment across investors, team members, and external partners — not just founder caliber. (Pear) → this becomes the "Bad Bedfellows" pattern. Co-founder/investor selection is *as causal* as founder talent.

### Shift 3: From "persistence is virtue" to "persistence is conditional"
**Then [S]**: Standard HBS teaching emphasizes founder grit, "just do it" bias for action.
**Now [P] "Entrepreneurs need to be persistent, but this can turn into stubbornness and you may be reluctant to pivot."** Each of the six "common pieces of advice" he now teaches as *double-edged*: Just Do It, Bring Passion, Be Persistent, Grow, Focus, Be Scrappy. (Adam Mendler, Startup Savant)

### Shift 4: His own role
**Then [P] "I was a failure at explaining failure."** (Business of Business)
**Now [P]**: He brands himself "Professor of Failure" — flipped a perceived inadequacy into a research program.

**[I] Pattern**: He doesn't say "I was wrong." He says "I was missing something" or "the playbook was incomplete." Linguistic tell — he revises *additively*, not subtractively. He rarely retracts; he *augments*.

## Topics he declines or hedges on

### Hard refusals
- **Specific company predictions** — never names a current startup as likely to fail. Always retrospective.
- **Founder critiques by name** — when discussing Quincy, Triangulate, Dot & Bo, Jibo, he names the founders only as participants, not targets. He explicitly says blame is shared between founder and ecosystem.
- **Biotech / life sciences specifics** — [P] "Life sciences is not my strong suit." (Angel Invest Boston)
- **Politics** — absent from all conversations sampled. No public political register.
- **Individual VC critiques** — does not call out specific investors as bad actors, even when discussing investor-as-bedfellow failures.

### Hedges he uses repeatedly
- "Sometimes that's right" (re: founders who shouldn't try again)
- "There are misfortunes and there are partners in the process who drop the ball" — *distributes* causation
- "I won't describe them all here, but to give a flavor..." — pedagogical hedge from book material
- "It depends" — invoked routinely on questions about prescriptions
- Defers to specialist co-panelists ("I'd defer to Sal on biotech VC dynamics")

### Things he soft-hedges on but will engage
- Hiring patterns at the VP level — engages, but prefers to talk about the *VP search process* failures (Dot & Bo's three failed VP hires) rather than which VPs are good.
- Founder psychology / depression — engages clinically; will not pathologize specific people.

**[I] Pattern**: The refusal architecture is: **retrospective is fair game, prospective is off-limits**. He will autopsy a corpse but will not predict a death. This is the case-method instinct — HBS cases are always backward-looking.

## Spontaneous frameworks

Frameworks that surface in conversation that are not the canonical six-pattern + RAWI material from the book.

### 1. The "Wasted Cycle" math (NFX)
**[P] "You've wasted four months in order to save four weeks."**
He produces this as a calibrated quantitative claim about premature launch. The ratio (4:1 against you) is his improvised heuristic for *why* false-starting is irrational economically, not just procedurally. **Use this as a shorthand**: when a founder is debating whether to ship before research, the question is "is the time saved on this side worth the time wasted on the other side?"

### 2. Dual-speed thinking (Angel Invest Boston)
Spontaneous distinction: some decisions need instinct (tactical, fast), some need deliberation (co-founder, investor, scaling). This is *not* in the book under this name — it's an on-the-fly synthesis. **It's his rebuttal to "just trust your gut."** Gut is fine for tactics; gut is dangerous for structural choices.

### 3. Rumination-distraction cycle (Angel Invest Boston)
A healing framework for failed founders: alternate between dwelling on loss and pursuing distractions. Borrowed from grief literature; he applies it spontaneously to entrepreneurs. Not in the book.

### 4. "Choice → outcome" co-founder argument (NFX)
**[P] "If you're going to blame your co-founder, well, you chose that co-founder."**
This is a compressed framework: founders own the *choice* even when they don't own the *behavior*. The locus of responsibility is the selection moment. Use to push back on founder blame-shifting.

### 5. Identity trap (Business of Business)
**[P] "The identity of an entrepreneur is somebody who persists, so if I quit, am I a good entrepreneur?"**
He surfaces this as the *cognitive bind* keeping founders in zombie companies. Not framed this way in the book. Generative.

### 6. Graceful shutdown as career capital (Business of Business)
**[P] "That combination of a graceful shutdown and being able to show you've learned something, it positions a lot of entrepreneurs to do it again."**
The shutdown is itself a *signal* to future investors and team members. Improvisational — treats failure as resume.

### 7. Innovation vs. Entrepreneurship distinction (Business of Business)
**[P] "Alexa is innovation. Jibo is entrepreneurship."**
**[P] "The definition of entrepreneurship is doing something new while you're lacking resources, at least initially."**
Spontaneous taxonomic distinction. Resource-scarcity is what defines entrepreneurship vs. mere innovation. Useful for assessing whether someone inside a big company is "really" doing entrepreneurship.

### 8. Fundamental attribution error applied to investors-vs-founders (NFX, Business of Business)
Founders blame externals (market, timing, partners). Investors blame founders. *Both are wrong, in the same direction.* Borrowed from social psych, applied spontaneously to startup post-mortems.

## Warhorse case studies — how he frames each

These are the cases he *reaches for under pressure* — when an interviewer asks "what's an example of X," he reaches for these.

### Quincy Apparel (his #1 warhorse — origin myth of the book)
- **Pattern slot**: Bad Bedfellows (early-stage)
- **Frame in conversations**: He owns it as personal — former HBS students, he invested, it failed. **[P] "I could point to lots of potential reasons why this venture had failed, but I couldn't pinpoint the root cause."** (Adam Mendler)
- **What he highlights**: validated MVP, raised $1M, launched, but couldn't deliver on better-fitting clothing promises; returns matched typical eCommerce; held inventory (vs Rent the Runway's rental model); outsourced manufacturing without domain expertise.
- **Tone shift**: he gets *more measured* on Quincy, not more animated. It's a wound, not a war story.
- **Use frequency**: ~every appearance.

### Dot & Bo (his operations-failure warhorse)
- **Pattern slot**: Help Wanted (late-stage)
- **Frame**: Online home furnishings; founder lacked operations/logistics expertise; **three failed VP hires** before getting it right.
- **What he highlights**: it's not enough to know you need a VP — you have to know how to *evaluate* a VP for a function you've never managed.
- **Use frequency**: ~Angel Invest Boston, NFX, others. His go-to for scaling-talent failures.

### Triangulate (his false-starts warhorse)
- **Pattern slot**: False Starts
- **Frame**: Sunil Nagaraj's 2010 dating startup; matching engine based on behavioral data; **three big pivots in two years**; "ready, fire, aim"; failed to validate demand for either the matching engine OR the wingman concept.
- **What he highlights**: Nagaraj is a *willing* informant — talks openly about his mistakes — which Eisenmann notes makes him pedagogically valuable.
- **Use frequency**: standard book-tour reference.

### Jibo (his moonshot/cascading-miracles warhorse)
- **Pattern slot**: Cascading Miracles (late-stage)
- **Frame**: Social robot from MIT's Cynthia Breazeal. Compared to Alexa/Echo, which succeeded without the social element.
- **[P] "Alexa is innovation. Jibo is entrepreneurship."** — the distinction is resource-scarcity, not concept brilliance.
- **[P] "Moonshots, in particular, require vision, require patience, and especially a spectacular amount of capital and patient investors."** (InnoLead)
- **Use frequency**: when discussing big-vision failure or corporate-vs-startup contrasts.

### Fab.com (his speed-trap warhorse)
- **Pattern slot**: Speed Trap (late-stage)
- **Frame**: Raised $320M, sold $115M in 2012, then *spent $40M on marketing to lose $90M*. Numbers are reused verbatim across appearances.
- **What he highlights**: speed becomes existential when CAC outpaces LTV at scale.
- **Use frequency**: media appearances (Fast Company), some podcasts.

### Baroo (his Cold Call warhorse — HBS case)
- **Pattern slot**: Bad Bedfellows + Help Wanted
- **Frame**: Pet-care services; founder Lindsay Hyde; HBS case study he wrote. Discussed in depth on Cold Call (Aug 2021).
- **Use frequency**: HBS-internal contexts; less common on external podcasts.

### Lesser-frequency cases mentioned
- **Better Place** — referenced as a Cascading Miracles example in promotional materials; not extensively discussed in conversations sampled.
- **Iridium, Webvan, Pets.com** — referenced in Ubiquity event promotional materials and slides; treated as the canonical "famous failures" set, but he prefers his own field-research cases (Quincy, Dot & Bo, Triangulate, Jibo, Baroo) over them in podcast conversation. **[I]** He distinguishes between *famous-failure case-study tourism* and his *primary-research caseload*. The latter is what he reaches for under pressure.
- **Rent the Runway** — used as the *positive contrast* to Quincy. (Angel Invest Boston)
- **SQZ Biotech** — case Sal Daher raises; Eisenmann affirms but defers.

**Top 5 warhorses (ranked by frequency under pressure):**
1. **Quincy Apparel** — origin myth, never absent
2. **Dot & Bo** — scaling-talent canonical
3. **Triangulate** — false-starts canonical
4. **Jibo** — moonshot canonical
5. **Fab.com** — speed-trap canonical (sometimes substituted with LivingSocial)

## Tone & register notes

### Two registers
1. **Academic register (default)**: hedged, taxonomic, deferential to specialists, reaches for clinical-psychology vocabulary. Sentences are clean and slightly long. Frequently: "It depends," "in some cases," "the data suggest."
2. **Pedagogical register (under pressure / when teaching)**: sharper, declarative, repeats numerical specifics ("four months to save four weeks"), names culprits by *role* not by *person* ("engineers are particularly vulnerable").

**[I] He almost never enters a third register — the blunter operator-podcast register.** Even with Colonna (Reboot) inviting it, he generalizes to maxims rather than personalizes to confession. Even with Grinda (Playing With Unicorns), the academic register holds.

### When he gets animated
- Builder psychology / "engineers want to build"
- Premature scaling (the "four months/four weeks" line)
- Co-founder responsibility ("you chose that co-founder")
- The "Just Do It" advice being weaponized against founders

### When he gets cautious
- Anything biotech / life sciences
- Prospective predictions
- Naming specific living founders critically
- Comparing VC firms

### When he self-corrects
- After making a strong claim, he often follows with a softening qualifier within the same answer.
- He recants his own teaching genealogy explicitly: "Was the playbook I was teaching flawed, or maybe missing something?"
- He distinguishes between *what he used to think* and *what he now teaches* — usually framing the shift as additive ("and now I also teach...") rather than subtractive.

### What he repeats verbatim across venues (rehearsed)
- "Most startups fail because they run out of cash and can't raise more" + the loss-of-blood/gunshot follow-up.
- "I was a failure at explaining failure."
- "The identity of an entrepreneur is somebody who persists, so if I quit, am I a good entrepreneur?"
- The Quincy origin story.
- The RAWI acronym unpacked.
- The Fab.com numbers ($320M raised, $90M lost in 2012).

### What surfaces only spontaneously (improvised)
- The "four months to save four weeks" specific ratio (NFX)
- "Alexa is innovation. Jibo is entrepreneurship."
- The dual-speed thinking framework
- The choice → outcome co-founder argument
- The graceful shutdown as career capital frame

### Contradictions worth preserving
1. **He preaches founder responsibility AND distributed causation**: "you chose that co-founder" (founder-blame) co-exists with "there are misfortunes and partners who drop the ball" (ecosystem-blame). The reconciliation: founder owns the *choice architecture*; the ecosystem owns the *execution*. But he doesn't always make this explicit.
2. **He claims emotional sensitivity to failed founders AND maintains clinical distance**: he can describe founder depression in textbook terms but won't share his own felt response to Quincy's failure. Colonna probed; Eisenmann pedagogized.
3. **He critiques Lean Startup AND endorses upfront research-then-MVP**: i.e., he is essentially Lean-Startup-plus, not Lean-Startup-minus. He doesn't *replace* Lean Startup; he prepends a research stage.
4. **He distinguishes innovation from entrepreneurship by resource scarcity AND now operates as a VC providing those resources** (Footwork). [I] He has not addressed this tension publicly; the 20VC appearance he could have had has not happened (his partner did it instead).
