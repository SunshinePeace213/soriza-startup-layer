# Charlie Munger — Timeline (1924–2023)

> **Research cutoff is permanent: November 28, 2023.** Charlie Munger is deceased. There will be no new primary Munger material — no new speeches, no new Daily Journal Q&As, no new annual-letter sidekick appearances. This skill is built against a closed corpus. Anything dated after Nov 28, 2023 is *about* Munger, not *by* him (e.g., Buffett's 2023 annual letter eulogy published Feb 2024).

Sources cross-checked against Wikipedia, the Daily Journal's own 1924–2023 tribute, Janet Lowe's *Damn Right!* (2000), the Acquired podcast (Oct 30, 2023), Berkshire SEC filings (2011 Wesco merger), and obituaries in CNBC, CBS News, Bloomberg, and the Washington Times (Nov 28, 2023). Items marked **[unverified]** could not be pinned to two independent sources.

## Chronological table

| Date | Event | Why it matters for how he thinks |
|---|---|---|
| **Jan 1, 1924** | Born Charles Thomas Munger in Omaha, NE, to Alfred Case Munger (lawyer) and Florence "Toody" Russell Munger | Midwestern Protestant-lawyer formation: thrift, duty, contractual integrity, "deserved trust." Being born on New Year's Day means his age tracks the calendar — easy mental anchoring. |
| **~1930s** | Worked at Buffett & Son grocery (Warren's grandfather Ernest's store) in Omaha as a teenager | Same town, same store, but he and Warren never overlapped as kids — a 25-year delayed handshake. Source of the "Omaha is a small place" running joke. |
| **1941** | Graduated Central High School, Omaha | Same school Buffett would later attend. Public-school Midwest baseline before the elite credential. |
| **1941–1943** | Attended University of Michigan; studied mathematics | Math-first temperament → probabilistic, quantitative cast of mind that resurfaces in his "Lollapalooza effects" and base-rate reasoning. |
| **Early 1943** | Dropped out after turning 19 to join the U.S. Army Air Corps | First "soldier on" moment. Drops a prestigious credential to do the urgent thing. |
| **1943–1946** | Army Air Corps 2nd Lt.; sent to Caltech for meteorology training; later posted to Nome, Alaska | Caltech exposure plants the seed for hard-science respect; Alaska gives him weeks alone with books → autodidact habit. |
| **1945** | Married Nancy Huggins (first wife) while at Caltech | Wartime marriage; three children to follow (Wendy, Molly, Teddy). |
| **1946** | Admitted to Harvard Law School with no undergraduate degree, after Roscoe Pound (former HLS dean, family friend) intervened with the dean of admissions | Lesson absorbed for life: networks and reputation override formal gates. Also: his father had been HLS, so legacy mattered. |
| **1948** | Graduated Harvard Law *magna cum laude* (one of 12 in a class of 335) | Confirms the raw horsepower. The credential plus the math undergraduate exposure give him a hybrid lawyer-quant identity. |
| **1948–1949** | Moved to Los Angeles; joined Wright & Garrett (later Musick, Peeler & Garrett) | LA, not New York — deliberate. He builds his life away from the financial center. |
| **1953** | Divorced Nancy Huggins; left with little; lived in a "dingy apartment" near his daughters | Financial bottom #1. Drives home: do not get trapped in the wrong long-term contract. |
| **1955** | Son Teddy died of leukemia at age 9 (then 100% fatal) | **Formative tragedy.** This is where the Stoic core comes from — Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius. "Envy, resentment, revenge, and self-pity are disastrous modes of thought." He learned to walk around grief, not through it. |
| **1956** | Married Nancy Barry Borthwick; merged families into 8 children (her 2 + his 3 + 3 together) | Second stable platform. The "two Nancys" pattern is sometimes confused; this is the 54-year marriage that defined his adult life. |
| **1959** | Met Warren Buffett at an Omaha dinner organized by Dr. Edwin Davis and his sons; Munger was 35, Buffett 28 | **Formative event.** 64-year intellectual partnership begins on day one. Buffett: "I'm not going to find another guy like this." Sets up everything that follows. |
| **1962** | (a) Co-founded **Munger, Tolles & Olson** (still one of the top US law firms). (b) Co-founded **Wheeler, Munger & Co.** investment partnership with Jack Wheeler (seat on the Pacific Coast Stock Exchange) | Builds two career tracks in parallel — the hedge that he could quit law cleanly. |
| **1965** | Effectively left full-time law practice for investing | Crystallizes the identity shift: not a lawyer who invests, but an investor with legal training. |
| **1962–1969** | Wheeler Munger generates ~37% gross / ~24% net annualized | First wealth. Concentrated, leveraged, "go big when the world offers." |
| **1972** | **See's Candies** acquired by Blue Chip Stamps (which Munger and Buffett controlled) for ~$25M | Munger talks Buffett into "paying up for quality." Inflection point from Graham deep-value to Munger-Buffett quality-compounding. Arguably the second-most-formative *idea* event after meeting Buffett. |
| **1973–1974** | Wheeler Munger loses ~32% in 1973 and ~31% in 1974 — roughly 53% peak-to-trough drawdown | **Formative event.** Equanimity-or-die. He doesn't sell at the bottom, but the experience of watching limited partners suffer convinces him never again to run other people's money in a marketable-security vehicle. |
| **1975 (winds down 1975–76)** | Closes Wheeler Munger after returning capital; ~19.8% net CAGR over the 14-year run vs. ~5% for the Dow | Chooses permanent capital over fees. Pivots into Blue Chip Stamps / Berkshire as his platform. |
| **1976** | Increasing his role at Blue Chip Stamps alongside Buffett | Builds the bridge into Berkshire. |
| **1978** | Appointed Vice Chairman of Berkshire Hathaway | The title that travels with him until death. |
| **1980** | **Botched cataract surgery destroys his left eye**; epithelial-downgrowth complications; eye eventually removed due to pain; sympathetic-ophthalmia risk briefly threatens the other eye; he starts Braille lessons | **Formative event.** "Soldier on." Took personal responsibility (he, not the doctor, chose the older procedure). Trains his lifelong stance: do not blame, do not self-pity, learn to read with one eye. |
| **1984** | Becomes Chairman, CEO, and President of **Wesco Financial** | His own sandbox to run an annual meeting, build a track record, and develop the Q&A muscle that later becomes the Daily Journal format. |
| **Jun 13, 1986** | "**Prescriptions for Guaranteed Misery**" — commencement speech at the Harvard School (now Harvard-Westlake) in LA, his son's graduation | First widely-circulated public articulation of *inversion* ("invert, always invert" — Jacobi). The Munger voice arrives in writing. |
| **1994** | "**A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom**" — USC Marshall School of Business | The "latticework of mental models" doctrine made canonical. Probably his single most influential speech. |
| **Jun 1, 1995** | "**The Psychology of Human Misjudgment**" — Harvard University (note: Harvard *University* this time, not Harvard School) | The 25 cognitive tendencies. Later revised and expanded for *Poor Charlie's Almanack* (2005). |
| **2000** | Janet Lowe's *Damn Right!* published (John Wiley); first full Munger biography, with his cooperation | Locks in the family-history record we now depend on. |
| **2003** | "**Academic Economics: Strengths and Faults**" at UC Santa Barbara | Begins his late-career critique of single-discipline thinking and economists' physics-envy. |
| **2005** | *Poor Charlie's Almanack* first published, edited by Peter D. Kaufman | The Munger canon as a book object. |
| **Sep 2008** | **BYD investment**: Berkshire (via MidAmerican Energy) buys 225M BYD shares (~9.89%) at HK$8 for ~$230M, on Munger's strong recommendation | His single best percentage-return idea at Berkshire. Validates the "lifelong learning + go to China" stance late in life. |
| **2008–2009** | Daily Journal makes opportunistic equity purchases (WFC, BAC, USB, POSCO) at GFC lows | Cash → equities at the bottom. Lived demonstration of his own advice about courage when "the world offers." |
| **Feb 6, 2010** | Wife Nancy Barry Munger dies, age 86 | Late-life loss. The Stoic apparatus that was forged in 1955 is now tested again. |
| **Jun 2011** | **Wesco Financial merged into Berkshire Hathaway** (merger agreement Feb 4, 2011; shareholder vote Jun 24, 2011) | End of his independent corporate stage; consolidation under Berkshire. |
| **2014** | **Daily Journal annual meeting** evolves into a multi-hour public Q&A format with Munger | His personal version of Berkshire's "Woodstock for capitalists" — uncoached, hours of off-the-cuff reasoning. The single richest video corpus we have of Munger thinking out loud. |
| **May 2017** | USC Gould School of Law commencement address (a second, distinct USC speech; the famous one is **May 13, 2007**) | Late-life restatement of the "seamless web of deserved trust" thesis. |
| **2021** | **Munger Hall / UCSB controversy**: $200M donation tied to a 1.68M-sq-ft, 4,500-bed dorm with ~94% windowless single rooms; consulting architect resigns; 14,000-signature petition. UCSB officially abandons the original design in **August 2023**. | Demonstrates both his late-life intellectual stubbornness *and* its costs. Useful for the skill's "honest limits" module — Munger is not infallible. |
| **Feb 2022 (announced Mar 28, 2022)** | **Steps down as Daily Journal chairman** at age 98 (donates his stock), remains a director | Late-stage power-handoff. His own model of when to release a title. |
| **Feb 2022 → Feb 2023** | **Alibaba reversal**: bought into BABA 2021, peaked Q4 2021, halved Q1 2022; at the **Daily Journal 2023 annual meeting** he calls Alibaba "one of the worst mistakes I ever made" — "I got charmed by the idea of their position in the Chinese internet; I didn't stop to realize they're still a goddamn retailer" | Last public admission of a major error. Crucial datum for honest modelling of his thinking: he updates publicly, including against himself, at 99. |
| **Apr 2023** | Recorded the **Acquired podcast** episode with Ben Gilbert and David Rosenthal at his LA home (released Oct 30, 2023) | His only dedicated long-form podcast interview. *De facto* final summative interview. |
| **Oct 2023** | *Poor Charlie's Almanack* republished by **Stripe Press** with John Collison foreword; free online edition | Posthumous-by-six-weeks canonization for the next generation. |
| **Nov 28, 2023** | **Dies at a Santa Barbara hospital, age 99** — 34 days short of his 100th birthday | Closes the corpus. |
| *Feb 2024* | *Buffett's 2023 Berkshire annual letter (the Munger eulogy) published* | About Munger, not by him. Outside the cutoff. |

## The Four Formative Events

If the skill needs a compressed origin story — the four data points that, taken together, generate most of Munger's adult voice — these are it.

**1. The death of Teddy Munger (1955).** Charlie was 31, freshly divorced, working as a junior LA lawyer, and his nine-year-old son was dying of leukemia in an era when childhood leukemia killed 100% of the children it touched. The biographers describe him walking the streets of Pasadena weeping after hospital visits. Two things crystallized: (a) the Stoic operating system he would describe for the rest of his life — Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius, the refusal of self-pity as a *strategic* refusal because self-pity changes nothing and costs energy you can't spare; and (b) the line he repeated at almost every Daily Journal meeting — "Envy, resentment, revenge, and self-pity are disastrous modes of thought." That is not a koan; it's a memorized survival manual.

**2. Meeting Warren Buffett (1959).** Dr. Eddie Davis and his sons arranged the dinner at the Omaha Club, expecting maybe a polite hour. Buffett later described Munger rolling on the floor laughing at his own jokes — "exactly the same thing I did" — and deciding inside five minutes that this was a once-in-a-life intellectual counterparty. The partnership runs uninterrupted from 1959 to 2023, 64 years, almost certainly the longest documented business intellectual partnership in modern American capitalism. Almost everything the skill knows as "Munger" is actually Munger *as expressed within the gravitational field of Buffett* — the annual letters, the Berkshire meetings, the See's Candies pivot from cigar-butts to compounders. Any perspective skill that ignores Buffett is mis-modelling Munger.

**3. The 1973–74 drawdown at Wheeler Munger.** Two consecutive years of ~32% and ~31% losses, ~53% peak-to-trough. He did not sell at the bottom — the positions recovered hugely by 1975 — but watching his limited partners suffer convinced him that running other people's money in marketable securities was a form of unnecessary suffering for everyone involved. He wound the partnership down voluntarily *after* the recovery. The lesson he carried forward: prefer permanent capital (Berkshire, Wesco, Daily Journal) over fee-based capital; size positions so you can survive the worst two years you can imagine; equanimity is not a virtue, it's a survival prerequisite.

**4. Losing his left eye (1980).** A botched cataract operation, his choice of the older surgical technique, complications, an eye that had to be removed because the pain was unbearable, a brief terror that sympathetic ophthalmia would take the other eye, and Braille lessons started in his fifties as a contingency. He refused to sue. He refused to blame the surgeon. He learned to read with one eye and kept reading several hours a day for the next forty-three years. This is the source of the operational stance the skill should reproduce: *take personal responsibility for outcomes you contributed to, even partially; do not waste a single cycle on grievance; the world does not owe you symmetry.*

These four — Teddy, Warren, the drawdown, the eye — are the load-bearing wall. Almost every Munger quote the user is likely to ask about traces back to at least one of them.

## Notes on what is **not** in the record

- His exact financial state in 1953–1955 is described in *Damn Right!* but specific net-worth figures are **[unverified]** in independent sources.
- Whether Roscoe Pound personally phoned Harvard or merely wrote is **[unverified]** — both versions appear.
- The 1972 See's Candies purchase price is reported variably as $25M and $30M across sources; the SEC-filed Blue Chip Stamps record gives ~$25M.
- The exact date he started Braille lessons is **[unverified]**; biographers place it in 1980–81.
- Acquired podcast recording date is sometimes reported as April 2023 and sometimes as a series of sessions through summer 2023 — the released episode dropped Oct 30, 2023.
