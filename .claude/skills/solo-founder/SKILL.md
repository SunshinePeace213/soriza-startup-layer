# Solo Founder — the one-person operating model

A solo founder is not a person who does everything — they are the **vision-holder and orchestrator** who
decides ruthlessly what *only they* can do and routes everything else to AI and contractors. The default
this pushes against: *"solo = do it all yourself,"* which leads straight to burnout and to being busy on
the wrong work.

## When this applies

- **Inline-read by stages:** `solution-design` (is this scope a one-person job?), `build-poc` (scope the
  build to what a solo founder can actually run).
- **Standalone:** "should I build this or delegate/automate it?", "what should only I do?", "am I
  overextending?", "do I need a co-founder?"

**Out of scope.** This is about *how a solo founder allocates and protects their effort* — not for
writing code, debugging, or unrelated technical tasks. If the request is an implementation task, just
do it; don't route it through the keep-vs-delegate matrix.

## The model: orchestrator, not lone hero

You are the central decision-maker who orchestrates a network of AI tools and on-demand contractors —
not someone literally alone. The whole discipline is **clarity on what only you can do versus what can
be systematized.** Successful one-person businesses are defined by that line, not by heroics.

## The keep-vs-delegate/automate matrix (the core tool)

Route every piece of work into one of three lanes:

| Lane | What goes here |
|---|---|
| **Only you** | vision; the irreplaceable judgment calls; **customer conversations** (especially early discovery); steering the agents; the founder-attention decisions |
| **Automate (AI as cofounder)** | research, drafting, code generation, analysis, repetitive ops — the tireless-partner work |
| **Delegate (contractor, on demand)** | specialist gaps outside your core (design, niche engineering, content) — bought as needed, **not** as headcount |

**Rule:** protect what only you can do; eliminate, automate, or delegate the rest. Accept **"good
enough"** wherever excellence doesn't move the business — perfection across all functions is impossible
solo, so spend your excellence budget where it matters.

## The solo failure modes (self-checks)

- **Decision fatigue** — no partner to validate with. → Use structured devil's-advocates (the
  `disconfirm` panel), pre-commit to kill-criteria *before* data, lean on advisors.
- **Isolation** — consequential calls made alone. → Peer/founder groups, advisors, masterminds.
- **The skill-gap (usually GTM/sales/distribution)** — name your weakest area honestly. For this
  archetype it's almost always *distribution* → plug it deliberately (own a channel, hire a contractor,
  apply the `forward-deployed-founder` doctrine). Don't pretend the gap isn't there.
- **Bus-factor** — you *are* the company. → Document; keep systems able to run for short stretches
  without you.

## Ruthless focus — one project

Resist launching multiple things at once; the solo constraint *is* the forcing function for
prioritization. Break work into achievable chunks, and protect **founder-attention** (decisions,
customer conversations, steering agents) over raw build hours — build hours parallelize across agents;
your attention does not.

## Solo vs co-founder (the call)

- **Go solo:** full control + speed + no equity split or co-founder conflict; forces lean AI/automation.
- **Bring someone in:** complementary skills (especially the GTM gap) and emotional resilience through
  the hard stretches.
- **The honest answer:** it depends on your skills and the business. Many of the strongest one-person
  businesses are digital/SaaS/services where a focused founder with good tools outperforms a
  poorly-matched team. **Plug a *temporary* gap with tools/contractors first; reserve a co-founder for a
  *durable, complementary* gap you can't close any other way.**
