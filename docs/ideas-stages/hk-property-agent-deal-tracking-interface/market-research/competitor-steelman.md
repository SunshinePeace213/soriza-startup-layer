# Competitor Steelman — hk-property-agent-deal-tracking-interface

*Adversarial by design. This is the incumbent strategist's case for why the founder loses. It is not balanced — fairness is the main conversation's job.*

The founder's bet rests on three claimed differentiators: (1) **per-agent custom workflow views** ("each agent renders their pipeline their own way"), (2) **outcome / commission-percentage pricing** ("you only pay when you earn"), and (3) **HK-local, Cantonese, relationship-driven focus** that US-centric CRMs ignore. Every one of these is reachable by an incumbent who already owns the agent's attention, the agent's listings, or the agent's chat thread. Here is how.

## Per-tier threat

- **Direct — the big-agency tech stacks (Centaline, Midland) and the listing portals (28Hse, Squarefoot/Spacious).** The founder is targeting independents, but the gravitational mass of the HK market is the brokerages: Centaline runs ~2,000 offices and ~40,000 staff; Midland runs 400+ branches and ~7,000 staff. They already build agent-facing tooling and have integrated WhatsApp Business with their lead engine (Centaline's Sanuker chatbot tracks communication history, collects customer info, and routes leads to the right agent). The portals (28Hse, Squarefoot) already sell **annual agent packages** and own the listing-supply funnel every independent depends on. They don't need to "enter" the deal-tracking market — they sit upstream of it. Any of them can bundle a pipeline view into the dashboard the agent already logs into daily, at zero marginal acquisition cost, and the independent's "I'm underserved" pain evaporates the day 28Hse ships a deal tracker as a package perk.

- **Indirect — the spreadsheet and the $24 Notion/Airtable template.** The founder's entire wedge is "generic CRMs force one Kanban; agents want their own view." But the agent's *current* tool — Excel/Google Sheets — is already the most configurable per-agent view on earth, costs nothing, and the agent already built it exactly their way. And the moment they want something prettier, there is a mature, English-and-localizable supply of customizable real-estate CRM templates: Notion's *AgentFlow* (properties, clients, deals, tasks, showings, **and commissions** in one template), free Notion real-estate CRM templates, Airtable's customizable-pipeline CRM, and a $24 niche bundle with a listing tracker, offer-comparison dashboard, and transaction timeline. "Good enough, infinitely customizable, near-free, and I keep my data" is exactly the value the founder is charging a commission percentage for. The indirect option doesn't just compete — it occupies the founder's claimed differentiator outright.

- **Potential acquirer — the listing portals (28Hse / Squarefoot-Spacious) or a regional proptech roll-up.** If the founder somehow proves independents will pay, the cheapest way for a portal to neutralize it is to acquire or clone it and fold it into the agent package they already bill annually. The portal owns distribution to *every* independent in HK; the founder owns a word-of-mouth slice of one local network. In an acquisition negotiation the founder has no leverage — the buyer can rebuild the feature for less than the asking price because they already have the agents, the listings, and the billing relationship. The space gets bought (or copied) out from under the founder the instant it's validated.

- **Adjacent — WhatsApp Business / Meta itself.** Every HK agent runs their deal flow through WhatsApp. The free WhatsApp Business app already ships **labels** (classify chats by status — "new customer," "pending," "resolved," "pending payment"), a **catalog** (list properties with photos and prices), quick replies, and broadcast lists — a crude but free per-agent pipeline living inside the one app the agent never closes. Meta has every incentive and the platform position to deepen this. The founder is building a deal tracker *next to* WhatsApp; Meta can build it *inside* WhatsApp, where the conversation, the client, and the listing already are, with zero install and zero switching cost.

## The one that kills you

**28Hse (and its mirror, Squarefoot/Spacious) — the HK listing portal.** Not Centaline, not Notion, not Meta. The portal is the most dangerous because it sits on the one thing the independent agent cannot live without — **listing supply and buyer eyeballs** — and it already has a billing relationship and an agent dashboard.

**Why the portal's approach is better for the customer.** The independent HK agent's actual job-to-be-done is *win the deal*, and deals start with a listing that gets seen. The agent already pays 28Hse/Squarefoot annually to post and be found. Deal tracking is a downstream chore, not the agent's top spend. A portal that bolts a per-agent deal view onto the dashboard the agent *already opens to manage listings* gives the agent one login, one bill, and tracking that's pre-populated from their own listings and inbound inquiries — no double entry. The founder's standalone tracker forces the agent to re-key listing and client data the portal already holds. For the customer, "it's already in the tool I pay for and it auto-fills from my listings" beats "a new app I have to feed by hand," every time.

**Why customers choose them.** Distribution and trust. The portal is already in the agent's annual budget and is the brand the agent associates with getting business. A solo founder reaching agents through "Ringo's local network" is fighting for attention against the platform every HK agent checks daily. The portal can offer the tracker free-with-package; the founder needs the agent to adopt a new tool *and* accept a variable commission-linked bill.

**Why each claimed differentiator fails:**

1. **"Per-agent custom workflow views."** This is not defensible — it's a configuration feature, not a moat. Configurable views are table stakes that Airtable, Notion, and the $24 templates already deliver, and a portal adding saved-view / custom-field options is a sprint, not a strategy. Worse, the agent's *spreadsheet already does this for free.* A differentiator that a $24 template and an Excel file both already satisfy is not a differentiator; it's the price of entry. The portal doesn't even have to match the flexibility — it wins on data pre-fill (auto-populated from listings the agent already posts), which the founder structurally cannot match because the founder doesn't own the listings.

2. **"Outcome / commission-percentage pricing — you only pay when you earn."** This is the most fragile claim, on two fronts. (a) **The agent doesn't want it.** A commission-linked bill means handing a third party the agent's most sensitive number — actual commission earned, deal by deal — to a startup, in a market where commission is privately negotiated and the EAA framework already governs written commission disclosure. Agents disclose commission to *principals* under regulation; they will resist piping it to a software vendor's billing engine. The hypothesis itself flags this risk ("agents fear variable cost or prefer fixed-fee"). (b) **It's trivially undercut.** The portal's answer is "free, included in your package" — there is no outcome to price against zero. Outcome pricing only aligns incentives if the buyer believes the tool *caused* the close; for a deal tracker, that attribution is unprovable, so the agent reads the commission cut as a tax on deals they'd have closed anyway. The Intercom Fin "$0.99/resolution" analogy doesn't transplant: a resolution is a discrete event the software demonstrably performs; a property close is not caused by your spreadsheet.

3. **"HK-local, Cantonese, relationship-driven focus."** The incumbent owns this more than the founder does. 28Hse is HK-built, HK-staffed, Cantonese-native, founded 2005; Centaline and Midland are the relationship-and-brand fabric of HK property. Localization is the incumbent's home turf, not the founder's wedge. The founder's "US-centric CRMs ignore HK" argument is true of Salesforce/HubSpot — and entirely irrelevant, because the agent isn't choosing between the founder and Salesforce; they're choosing between the founder and a free spreadsheet, a $24 template, WhatsApp labels, and the HK portal they already pay. None of those are US-centric. The founder's localization edge is an edge against a competitor the agent was never going to buy.

## Survival bar

For the founder to survive this, **all** of the following must be demonstrably true — stated as conditions to clear, not reassurances:

1. **Prove the job is tracking, not listings.** Customer discovery must show independents rank deal-leakage pain *above* listing-supply and lead-gen pain — i.e., that they'd adopt a tracker that does *not* solve listings. If the top pain is "I need more buyers," the portal wins by definition.

2. **Prove agents will expose commission to a vendor.** Get explicit willingness from ≥5 agents to connect real commission data to a billing engine, *after* the EAA-disclosure and "why would I tell a startup my numbers" objections are surfaced. If they flinch, the entire outcome-pricing thesis — the stated alignment differentiator — collapses, and the product is just another seat-priced CRM with no moat.

3. **Beat the spreadsheet on a dimension the spreadsheet can't follow.** Custom views won't do it — Excel already wins there. Survival requires a wedge the configurable-template/spreadsheet stack *structurally* cannot copy: e.g., automated WhatsApp-thread-to-deal-state capture that kills the double-entry the portal also can't easily do. Absent a non-copyable mechanism, the indirect tier erases the product.

4. **Have a defensible answer for the day 28Hse ships a deal tracker in its agent package.** If the only answer is "but mine is more customizable," there is no business. The founder needs a reason the portal cannot or will not bundle this — and right now there isn't one.

If even one of these fails, the incumbent wins. The burden is entirely on the founder to clear all four.
