## Core recurring arguments (with frequency notes)

A claim is listed here only if it surfaces in **≥3 distinct writings or interviews**. Frequency in parentheses; underlying citations follow.

### 1. "Failure is not the horse OR the jockey — it's the *fit* between idea, people, resources, and timing." (≥6)
   Eisenmann's entire 2021 project is a frontal pushback on the canonical VC heuristic ("we bet on the jockey, not the horse"). His thesis: VC folklore collapses six different failure mechanisms into a binary that hides real causes.
   - HBR 2021 article subtitle ("It's Not Always the Horse or the Jockey").
   - *Why Startups Fail*, opening chapter.
   - Repeated in NFX podcast and Angel Invest Boston interview.
   - Underscore VC summary cites him: "A lot of failures can be traced to not having the industry expertise when you need it."
   - **Confidence: primary**.

### 2. "Most startups fail because they run out of cash — but that's a symptom, not a cause." (≥4)
   He uses this as a rhetorical setup ("at the simplest level…but that's not very helpful") to motivate root-cause analysis. Failure analysis was *understudied* and needed a typology.
   - Adam Mendler interview (direct quote).
   - HBR 2021 article.
   - *WSF* introduction.
   - **Confidence: primary**.

### 3. "Lean Startup is correct but routinely under-applied; founders cherry-pick 'fail fast' and 'launch early' while skipping customer discovery." (≥5)
   Eisenmann describes himself as "an apostle of the lean start-up approach" who concluded its practices were "falling short of their promise." The miss is upstream: founders skip Steve Blank's customer-discovery interviews.
   - HBR 2021 article.
   - *WSF* False Start chapter.
   - HBS Background Note 812-095 (Eisenmann/Ries/Dillard 2011/2013).
   - Adam Mendler & NFX interviews.
   - HBS WK piece "Teaching a Lean Startup Strategy."
   - **Confidence: primary**.

### 4. "A bias for action is an asset that becomes a liability when it cuts short research." (≥4)
   Phrased almost identically across sources: action-bias is one of the entrepreneur's few real advantages — but it is the *cause* of False Starts.
   - "Move fast when you can, but take your time when you need to." (Underscore VC quoting *WSF*.)
   - "You've wasted four months in order to save four weeks." (NFX podcast.)
   - Mendler interview.
   - HBR 2021 article.
   - **Confidence: primary**.

### 5. "Early-adopter delight is dangerous evidence of mainstream product-market fit." (≥5)
   The False Positives pattern — central pedagogical claim about Dropbox vs. Fab.com.
   - "Product-market fit can be lost over time. If mainstream customers don't keep foaming at the mouth like your early adopters, you're going to have trouble." (Underscore VC.)
   - Produx Labs interview ("their needs are different than the needs of the mainstream users").
   - *WSF*, NFX, HBR article.
   - **Confidence: primary**.

### 6. "Startups should ask RAWI — Ready? Able? Willing? Impelled? — before stepping on the gas." (≥4)
   Decision-gating heuristic for whether to scale.
   - HBR 2021 article: "If the answer to any of the four questions … is 'no,' then the entrepreneur should think hard before stepping on the gas."
   - *WSF* late-stage chapters.
   - Confirmed in summaries (Bookey, Brave Sea, Underscore VC).
   - **Confidence: primary on definition; primary on framing**.

### 7. "Founder ego cuts both ways — ambivalence and obstinacy are both lethal." (≥3)
   - Blog post "Head Games" (2013).
   - *WSF* failing chapter (founder psychology, Kübler-Ross stages).
   - Reboot podcast with Jerry Colonna.
   - **Confidence: primary**.

### 8. "Two-sided platforms obey a different strategic logic than single-sided businesses." (≥5, in platform corpus)
   Network effects across distinct user groups create winner-take-all dynamics; subsidy-side vs. money-side pricing matters more than cost-plus economics.
   - "Strategies for Two-Sided Markets" (2006).
   - "Opening Platforms" (2009).
   - "Platform Envelopment" (2011).
   - *Managing Networked Businesses* course.
   - HBR/HBS case studies (Facebook, Apple, Microsoft, eBay).
   - **Confidence: primary**.

### 9. "For bet-the-company decisions, gut instinct is unreliable — emotions distort." (≥3)
   - Mendler interview (direct quote: "you can't trust your gut: it'll be wracked by strong emotions").
   - *WSF* failing chapter on shutdown timing.
   - NFX podcast on "pulling the plug."
   - **Confidence: primary**.

### 10. "Half of failed founders come back and try again." (≥3)
   Failure as a recoverable career event, not terminal. Underwrites his case for treating failure as a teachable skill.
   - NFX podcast (direct quote).
   - *WSF* final chapter.
   - Reboot interview.
   - **Confidence: primary**.

## Signature frameworks and coined terms

### The Six Failure Patterns
Three early-stage, three late-stage. The book is structured around them.

**Early-stage (Part I):**
1. **Good Idea, Bad Bedfellows** — Right opportunity, wrong people (founders, team, investors, partners). Anchor case: *Quincy Apparel* (Eisenmann's own angel investment; co-founders lacked founder-market fit, fashion-manufacturing expertise, and CEO clarity).
2. **False Starts** — Skipping customer discovery; building before validating. Anchor case: *Triangulate* (online dating, engineering-led).
3. **False Positives** — Misreading early-adopter enthusiasm as mainstream signal. Anchor cases: *Fab.com* (failure), *Dropbox* (counter-example — Houston deliberately designed for mainstream).

**Late-stage (Part II):**
4. **Speed Trap** — Hypergrowth + LTV/CAC squeeze + organizational unraveling. "The primary killer of late-stage startups." Anchor: *Fab.com* (again, scaled into the trap), *Dot & Bo*.
5. **Help Wanted** — Missing senior talent or capital at the moment they're needed. Anchor: *Dot & Bo* operations VP gap.
6. **Cascading Miracles** — Ventures that require multiple low-probability breakthroughs to *all* land. Anchor: *Better Place* (EV charging swaps), *Iridium*, *Jibo*, *Segway*. Counter-example: SpaceX/Tesla (where the miracles actually cascaded *successfully*).

### The RAWI Test (for whether to scale)
**R**eady — Do you have product-market fit?
**A**ble — Do you have the resources (capital, people, systems) for hypergrowth?
**W**illing — Are founders + board emotionally bought in to losing money for years?
**I**mpelled — Is there a *competitive* reason to grow now (winner-take-all dynamics, capital window)?

Any "no" → don't step on the gas. Functions as Eisenmann's antidote to "grow at all costs."

### Diamond-and-Square Framework (early-stage opportunity assessment)
- **Diamond** = the opportunity, four corners: **Customer Value Proposition**, **Technology & Operations**, **Marketing (Go-to-Market)**, **Profit Formula**. Adapted from Christensen/Johnson business-model canvas.
- **Square** = the resources, four corners: **Founders**, **Team (other early hires)**, **Investors**, **Strategic Partners**.
- Promising startup = "dynamic alignment of all 8 pieces."
- Tagline framing: "How can an aspiring entrepreneur know whether she has actually identified an attractive opportunity?"

### The Six-S Framework (late-stage scaling diagnostic)
Inner triangle (organizational core): **Staff, Structure, Shared Values**.
Outer circle (strategic levers): **Speed, Scope, Series X (next round / CEO transition)**.
Maps directly onto the late-stage failure patterns.

### Four ways to handle entrepreneurial risk
From his Jan 2013 blog post and *WSF*:
1. **Resolve** risk (lean tests / customer discovery)
2. **Defer** risk (staged investing)
3. **Shift** risk (strategic partnerships)
4. **Ignore** risk (compelling storytelling — used when the prior three aren't available; explicitly *not* always wrong, e.g., Musk-style ventures).

### Platform strategy concepts (with co-authors Parker & Van Alstyne)
- **Two-sided market / two-sided network** — distinct user groups, cross-side network effects.
- **Subsidy side vs. money side** — one side pays, the other is courted.
- **Same-side vs. cross-side network effects** — direction matters for pricing.
- **Multi-homing costs** — whether users can be on multiple platforms cheaply; determines winner-take-all probability.
- **Winner-take-all dynamics** — strong cross-side effects + high multi-homing costs + low differentiation = one winner.
- **Platform envelopment** — entry path where a platform in market A enters market B by bundling functionality, leveraging shared user relationships. Not Schumpeterian — does not require new technology.
- **Three envelopment types** — platforms that are (a) complements, (b) weak substitutes, (c) functionally unrelated.
- **Platform openness** — choices at four roles: end-users, complementors, providers, sponsors. Each can be independently opened or closed.

### Other coined / signature terms
- **"Founder-market fit"** — popularized (though not solely his); core to Bad Bedfellows.
- **"Graceful shutdown"** — Eisenmann's preferred frame for ending a failed company (creditors paid, employees helped to next role, customers transitioned).
- **"LTV/CAC squeeze"** — diagnostic for Speed Trap.
- **"Threat-rigidity response"** — borrowed from Staw/Sandelands/Dutton; he uses it in his 2016 Cyber Space Odyssey post to explain his own 2001 forecasting failure.
- **"Hypothesis-driven entrepreneurship"** — title of the HBS background note; his preferred label for what Ries called Lean Startup.

## Argument style / methodology

**Reasoning direction:** Case studies → patterns → prescriptions. Strongly inductive. He explicitly contrasts himself with theorists-from-first-principles. From his own description: *"read everything any academic or practitioner had written, interviewed scores of failed founders and investors, did a big survey, eventually wrote 20 HBS teaching cases"* (NFX podcast).

**Triangulation principle:** Each pattern in *WSF* is built from: (a) an anchor case he taught, (b) multiple confirming cases, (c) at least one counterexample (e.g., Dropbox for False Positives, SpaceX for Cascading Miracles). The counterexamples are not decorative — they define the boundary condition.

**Sample-selection humility:** He repeatedly acknowledges survivor bias in startup research and admits he cannot run controlled experiments. He compensates by interviewing failed and successful founders symmetrically, and by drawing on his own losing investments (Quincy Apparel — the founders were his former students, and the failure was the trigger for the book).

**Rhetorical structure of his pieces:**
1. State the folk wisdom (e.g., "horse or jockey," "fail fast," "bet on the founder").
2. Show where it's load-bearing-correct.
3. Show where it breaks — usually by case study with surprising root cause.
4. Offer a typology that disambiguates.
5. Provide an actionable checklist (RAWI, Diamond-and-Square, Six-S).

**Handling uncertainty:** His default move is to refuse single-cause explanations. *WSF* repeatedly insists failure has multiple proximate causes — "complex, interconnected factors rather than single mistakes." Where data is thin, he triangulates qualitative interviews with quantitative survey data (his "big survey") and explicitly acknowledges what cannot be cleanly identified.

**Tone:** Empirical, pedagogical, mildly contrarian, never polemical. Compare with Thiel (provocative) or Blank (prescriptive) — Eisenmann is the academic synthesizer who validates *and* gently corrects the practitioner gurus.

## Distinctive stances

### 1. Failure analysis as an *understudied* sub-discipline
He argues entrepreneurship research has obsessed over success — Sahlman/Stevenson on opportunity, Christensen on disruption, Wasserman on equity — while leaving failure to memoir and blog posts. His book aims to fill that gap with HBS-style rigor.
- **Confidence: primary (book intro, multiple interviews).**

### 2. Pushback on "Fail Fast" mythology
Founders take "fail fast" as a license to skip upfront research. They pivot endlessly without ever validating the original problem. Result: lots of cheap failures that share the same root cause — a flawed problem-solution pair.
- **Confidence: primary.**

### 3. Lean Startup is **partially** misapplied — not wrong
Unlike critics who reject Lean Startup, Eisenmann endorses Blank's full pipeline (customer discovery → customer validation → customer creation → company building) and complains that founders skip the first step. This is a *defense of Blank against the Eric-Ries-popularized abbreviation*.
- **Confidence: primary (HBS BN 812-095 co-authored with Ries himself; HBS WK; HBR 2021).**

### 4. Sometimes founders should NOT pivot
Counter to the cliché that all great companies pivoted, Eisenmann argues persistence-vs-pivot is the wrong binary. The real question: *was the original hypothesis falsified by an MVP, or merely under-resourced?* Pivoting away from a hypothesis that was never actually tested wastes the pivot.
- **Confidence: primary (HBR 2021, Blog "Head Games" 2013, HBS BN 812-095).**

### 5. Some great companies require Cascading Miracles
Eisenmann does not say "don't attempt Cascading Miracles." He says: *be honest about the conjunctive probability*. SpaceX, Tesla, Moderna are the same risk class as Better Place and Theranos. The pattern is the same — only the outcome differs.
- **Confidence: primary.**

### 6. Graceful shutdown as a professional virtue
Where startup culture treats shutdown as a single catastrophic moment, Eisenmann frames it as an extended responsibility: pay creditors, help employees, transition customers. This positions founders to come back. The chapter "Failing" in *WSF* is unusual in entrepreneurship literature.
- **Confidence: primary.**

### 7. Status-quo bias in his own forecasting
In the 2016 *Cyber Space Odyssey* post, he openly admits his unpublished 2001 book *Speed Trap* missed Google, the iPod, Kindle, social networking, and the LinkedIn-vs-Monster reversal. He attributes this to *threat-rigidity response* in a post-bubble environment. **This is rare epistemic honesty in entrepreneurship writing**, and it's a contradiction-evolution worth flagging (see below).
- **Confidence: primary.**

### 8. Process-based product management
On his blog he repeatedly amplifies the "active process" view (Sam Clemens / InsightSquared): PM should be deliberately designed, not organically grown. Higher release frequency + irreducible customer visits + minimal specifications. Quietly contrarian against the anti-process romantic strain in startup land.
- **Confidence: primary (blog 2016).**

### 9. Platforms ≠ products: needs different strategic apparatus
The 2006/2009/2011 platform trilogy with Parker & Van Alstyne argues that two-sided market dynamics demand entirely different pricing, openness, and competitive strategies than single-sided businesses. The implication: many startup playbooks (price-to-cost, vertical integration, differentiation) are dangerous when applied to a two-sided platform.
- **Confidence: primary.**

## Contradictions and evolutions

### A. 2001 confident growth-strategy theorist → 2021 failure-analysis humility
*Speed Trap* (2001, unpublished) was a confident manuscript on internet growth strategies; Eisenmann shelved it for tenure reasons and later admitted it was riddled with status-quo bias and missed every major platform development of the 2000s. The 2021 book is structurally the opposite — it is *about* the conditions under which growth strategies destroy value (the Speed Trap pattern got promoted from book title to failure-pattern label). **Evolution, not contradiction**, but a striking arc.

### B. Lean Startup advocate → Lean Startup critic-defender
- 2011/2013 HBS BN co-authored with Eric Ries — straight advocacy.
- 2021 *WSF* + HBR — argues "founders embrace Lean Startup but skip customer discovery," producing more failures, not fewer. He defends Blank-the-theorist against Ries-the-popularizer's abbreviation.
- This is a public, deliberate shift over ~10 years.

### C. Founder-as-Hero (early career) → Founder-as-System-Component (2021)
Earlier writing emphasizes founder talent, vision, persistence. *WSF* explicitly attacks the "horse or jockey" binary — the founder is one of eight pieces (four diamond + four square). Decentered.

### D. "Bias for action is the entrepreneur's edge" (consistent) vs. "Bias for action causes False Starts" (2021)
Held simultaneously. The resolution: action-bias is correct *after* customer discovery is done. He retains both halves rather than abandoning the first.

### E. Platform openness — early enthusiasm tempered
The 2006 HBR article reads more bullishly on openness than the 2009 "Opening Platforms" working paper, which adds the appropriability tradeoff and warns that opening too far destroys rents. 2011 "Platform Envelopment" adds yet another defensive lens. The arc: openness is good for adoption but bad for capture, and the optimum is contingent.
