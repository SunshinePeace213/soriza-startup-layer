Citations note book + section number (§) where Pareto's paragraph numbering is verifiable; "ATTRIBUTED" flags claims widely quoted but not located in the primary text I accessed.

## Coined terms (with brief definition + book/chapter citation)

- **Ophelimity** (*ophélimité*): purely economic want-satisfaction measured ordinally from preferences; deliberately substituted for "utility" to strip away ethical, moral, religious, political connotations. (*Cours*, 1896–97; refined in *Manuale*, 1906, ch. III.) [PRIMARY: Wikipedia "Ophelimity"; Britannica *Manuale*]
- **Maximum of ophelimity FOR a collectivity** (later called *Pareto optimality*): "the position from which any small variation increases the ophelimity of some and reduces the ophelimity of others." (*Manuale*, 1906; formally announced 1894 *Il massimo di utilità*; developed during the 1902 Scorza controversy.) [PRIMARY paraphrase via OpenEdition (Bruni/McLure scholarship)]
- **Maximum of ophelimity OF a collectivity** vs **FOR a collectivity**: the former requires interpersonal welfare comparison (rejected in economics, permitted in sociology); the latter does not. (*Manuale*, 1906.)
- **Pareto distribution / Pareto's law**: N = A·x^(–α) for incomes above threshold x; published in *Cours* (1896–97), §§ on income distribution. Pareto claimed it was a near-universal empirical regularity across countries and eras.
- **Indifference curves** (refined from Edgeworth): in the *Manuale* (1906), Pareto formalised choice from ordinal preferences alone, dropping cardinal utility and reframing the Edgeworth box. [PRIMARY: Wikipedia Cardinal-utility article; Springer Lausanne chapter]
- **Residues** (*residui*): the relatively constant sentiment-based "core" left when one subtracts the variable rationalisations from a non-logical action. The six classes (*Trattato* §§ in vol. 2 — verified via secondary):
  - I. Instinct for Combinations (innovation, recombination, schemes)
  - II. Persistence of Aggregates (loyalty, conservatism, tradition)
  - III. Need to express sentiments through external acts (ritual, activity)
  - IV. Residues connected with sociality (group conformity, peer-pressure)
  - V. Integrity of the individual and his appurtenances (defence of self, family, property)
  - VI. Sex residue
  - The two that matter politically are I and II — foxes (I) and lions (II). [PRIMARY: yourarticlelibrary summary of *Trattato* matches; vol. 3 references it as background]
- **Derivations** (*derivazioni*): the variable rationalisations/justifications a person attaches to a residue. Four classes, with full section ranges I verified in *Mind and Society* vol. 3:
  - Class I — **Assertion** (§§1420–1433): bare assertions of fact (Ia), of sentiment (Iβ), or mixed (Iγ).
  - Class II — **Authority** (§§1434–1463): of individuals (IIa), of tradition/usage (IIβ), of divine beings or personifications (IIγ).
  - Class III — **Accord with sentiments or principles** (§§1464–1542): "everybody agrees, therefore true"; or appeal to juridical/metaphysical/supernatural entities.
  - Class IV — **Verbal proofs** (§§1543–1686): indefinite terms, metaphors, analogies, vague language doing argumentative work.
- **Circulation of elites** (*circolazione delle élites*): the empirical regularity that ruling classes are renewed not by mass uprising but by replacement of incumbent elites with rising counter-elites; if circulation is blocked, the whole order collapses. (*Rise and Fall of Elites* 1900; expanded *Trattato* vol. 4 and *Transformation of Democracy* 1921.)
- **Governing elite vs non-governing elite**: a binary inside the broader "elite" category, allowing Pareto to distinguish power-holders from merely high-performing individuals. (*Trattato* vol. 4.)
- **Lions vs Foxes**: typology of governing elites, explicitly tagged to Machiavelli — lions (Class II residues) govern through force and stability; foxes (Class I residues) govern through cunning, combination, and manipulation. (*Trattato* vol. 4.) [PRIMARY: cross-referenced with Yourarticlelibrary; Sociology.Plus]
- **Logical vs non-logical action**: a 4-fold table on whether (a) the subjective end matches the agent's stated reasons and (b) the objective end matches the actual result. (*Trattato* vol. 1, ch. II — table referenced but full reproduction blocked behind chapter text.)
- **Logico-experimental method**: Pareto's own brand of positivism — propositions admissible only insofar as they can be tested against experience; everything else is dismissed as "non-logico-experimental" (theology, metaphysics, ethics, humanitarianism). (*Trattato* vol. 1 §§6, 13, 16–17.)

## Core recurring arguments (≥3 instances across his works)

### 1. **Sociology must be an experimental science modeled on physics/chemistry, not on ethics**
- *Cours* (1896): mathematical economics modeled on rational mechanics.
- *Manuale* (1906): "tastes and obstacles" formal equilibrium, indifference curves, math appendix.
- *Trattato* §6: "we here venture to expound a sociology that is purely experimental, after the fashion of chemistry, physics, and other such sciences. In all that follows, therefore, we intend to take only experience and observation as our guides." [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- §20: he will build "a system of sociology on the model of celestial mechanics, physics, chemistry…" [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- *Compendio* (1920): condensed restatement of the same logico-experimental commitment.
- **Credibility: Primary, verified across at least four works.**

### 2. **Most human action is non-logical; logic-looking explanations are mostly derivations from underlying sentiments**
- *Les Systèmes socialistes* (1902): "human action emanates from emotion and passion rather than from reason."
- *Manuale* (1906) preface: people rationalise; economic-theoretic agents are an idealisation.
- *Trattato* §1413 (vol. 3): the actual hierarchy is *purpose → residues → derivations*, but theorists invert it. [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- §1416: when a derivation is demolished "another immediately is brought forward," because the underlying residue is constant — example: Chinese, Moslem, Calvinist, Catholic, Kantian, Hegelian, Materialist "all refrain from stealing; but each gives a different explanation for his conduct." [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- *Compendio* (1920): popularised restatement.
- **Credibility: Primary, verified in multiple sections; the load-bearing claim of his sociology.**

### 3. **History is "a graveyard of aristocracies": elites necessarily rise, decay, and are replaced — masses never rule**
- *Rise and Fall of Elites* (1900–01 essay): elites decay and are replaced by counter-elites; French Revolution cited.
- *Trattato* vol. 4: extended typology; "history is a graveyard of aristocracies" widely cited as Pareto's phrasing. [ATTRIBUTED — phrase frequently quoted, located in vol. 4 per scholarship; not in vols. 1/3 I read directly]
- *Transformation of Democracy* (1921): democracies decay into demagogic plutocracy; same cycle.
- **Credibility: Primary recurrence verified across 3 works.**

### 4. **Wealth and income distribution follow a stable, near-universal pattern (the Pareto law)**
- *Cours* (1896–97): first statement; data from Britain, Prussia, Saxony, Italian cities, fiscal records.
- *Manuale* (1906): restated mathematically.
- *Les Systèmes socialistes* (1902): used polemically against redistributionists — egalitarian schemes cannot durably shift the curve.
- **Credibility: Primary, ≥3 instances. The 80/20 popular framing is later (Juran, mid-20th c.); Pareto's claim is statistical-distributional, not the casual "80/20 rule."**

### 5. **Liberal-democratic ideology is itself a derivation; socialism, humanitarianism, democracy all rest on sentiments dressed up as reason**
- *Les Systèmes socialistes* (1902): socialism plays on feelings; liberalism calls on reason; therefore socialism is politically *more* effective. Socialism is "the ideology of the irrationality of human action based on residuals justified by derivations."
- *Trattato* §1431: rhetoric of "progress," "democracy," "broadly human" functions as "a mere device for evoking sentiments." [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- §§1438, 1440: "worshippers of the goddess Science" / "modern saints defending wretched mortals against microbes" — modern hygiene & scientism as authority-derivations parallel to medieval theology. [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- *Transformation of Democracy* (1921): "now myths and prophecies are being renewed… League will bring peace and joy… Bolshevism will bring that same peace and joy." (Pareto 1921, 30.) [PRIMARY via OpenEdition]
- **Credibility: Primary, verified across at least three sources.**

### 6. **Force and consent (or fraud) are the two recurring instruments of rule — Machiavelli's framework continues to hold**
- *Trattato* vol. 4: lions (force) and foxes (fraud/cunning) explicitly tagged to Machiavelli's antithesis.
- *Rise and Fall* (1900–01): elites decay when force is no longer applied "judiciously"; new elite rises by reactivating it.
- *Transformation of Democracy* (1921): "demagogic plutocracy" — plutocrats "forge an effective union because they are astute and can deceive the masses by manipulating public sentiment." (Pareto 1921, 55.) [PRIMARY via OpenEdition]
- **Credibility: Primary recurrence verified.**

### 7. **Pure economics is descriptive, not normative; it cannot decide between capitalism and collectivism**
- *Manuale* (1906): "Political economy does not have to take morality into account. But one who extols some practical measure ought to take into account not only the economic consequences, but also the moral, religious, political, etc., consequences." (Manual 1906, p. 13.) [PRIMARY via Cruel.org Paretian essays]
- *Manuale* explicitly: "pure economics does not enable us to say whether it is preferable for the economy to be based on private property and free competition or on a collectivist one." [PRIMARY via OpenEdition]
- Croce debate (1900–02): science deals with functional relations, not metaphysical causalities.
- *Trattato* §6 ("Humanitarian sociologies we have to satiety — they are about the only ones that are being published nowadays"). [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- **Credibility: Primary, verified across ≥3 sources.**

## Method and Epistemology

- **Logico-experimental** is the master term. Defined *Trattato* §§13, 16–17. A proposition belongs to class Ia1 if "the abstractions and general principles that are used within it are derived exclusively from experience and are subordinated to experience." Class II = non-experimental, abstractions transcend experience.
- He explicitly groups Comte, Spencer, De Greef, Letourneau as "dogmatic" sociologists despite their positivist self-description (*Trattato* §6). He rejects positivism-as-system while keeping positivism-as-discipline. [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- He attacks Hegel for "comic absurdity" (*Trattato* §51) and Hegel's translator Vera for treating Absolute knowledge as a science (§§19–20). [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- He likes engineer-style modelling: "tastes and obstacles" in economics; equilibrium and small variations in sociology. He was trained as an engineer (Polytechnic of Turin) and ran a railway/iron works before academic life.
- **He gives a *rational* account of non-logical action**, which is why "Pareto = irrationalist" is wrong. Per Liberty Fund's Forte: "Pareto gave a rational explanation of non-logical actions as derivations and residuals."
- He thinks scientific claims are functional relations (*y = f(x)*), not causal/metaphysical claims. Mathematical form is preferred precisely because it forces rigour.
- He treats his own theory as falsifiable in principle — predictions about elite circulation, residue prevalence, distribution curves are empirical claims, not normative postulates.

## Critique targets

- **Socialism**: not because its goals are wrong, but because its claim to be scientific is fraudulent. Marx's *Capital* mocked at *Trattato* §1416(3): "Some few Socialists in Germany may have read it, but those who can possibly have understood it must have been as rare as white blackbirds." Obscurity confers prestige; the residue (egalitarian sentiment + class hatred) is what does the political work, not the argument. [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- **Marxism specifically**: in *Les Systèmes socialistes* vol. 2 — rejects historical-materialist monocausality. Concedes Marx's *sociological* insight on class struggle is real (admits it is "one conflict among many"), but rejects Marx's *economic* theory of value as metaphysical. (Cairn/Persée.)
- **Humanitarianism**: *Trattato* §6 — "Humanitarian sociologies we have to satiety." [PRIMARY VERIFIED] He treats compassion-rhetoric as a Class III derivation (accord with sentiment).
- **Democracy** (as ideology, not necessarily as institutional arrangement): a "myth"; "demagogic plutocracy" is its real form. *Transformation of Democracy* (1921) elaborates. He thinks every regime is rule by an elite, so "the people rule" is a derivation.
- **"Natural rights" rhetoric**: a Class III/IV derivation — appeal to juridical or metaphysical entities, or verbal proof from indefinite terms.
- **Comte, Spencer, De Greef, Letourneau**: "dogmatic sociologists" (*Trattato* §6) — their positivism is contaminated by progressive teleology. [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- **Hegel and German idealism**: *Trattato* §51 — "comic absurdity." [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- **Bentham-style utilitarianism**: rejects "greatest good for the greatest number" because good cannot be measured and interpersonal welfare cannot be summed.
- **Feminism**: dismissed as "a disease that can only affect a wealthy people" (Wikipedia citation; not located in primary text I read). [ATTRIBUTED, NOT VERIFIED in primary].
- **Senator René Bérenger** (*Trattato* §6 footnote): mocked for moralising campaigns. [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- **Walras's metaphysical residue**: while succeeding Walras at Lausanne (1893) and inheriting general equilibrium, Pareto explicitly stripped social-economics teleology from it; he disagreed with Walras's social philosophy. (Springer Lausanne chapter; Alan Kirman essay.)

## Influences he names directly

- **Léon Walras** — predecessor at Lausanne (1893 succession); explicit debt for general equilibrium; explicit divergence on the social-economic question. [PRIMARY: Lausanne chapter, multiple sources]
- **Francis Y. Edgeworth** — Pareto refined Edgeworth's indifference-curve apparatus and the contract curve. (*Manuale*, 1906.)
- **Niccolò Machiavelli** — explicit citation for the lions/foxes typology. (*Trattato* vol. 4.)
- **Auguste Comte** — cited as predecessor, but criticised as dogmatic. (*Trattato* §6.) [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- **Herbert Spencer** — cited and criticised in the same breath. (*Trattato* §6.) [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- **Hegel and Vera** — named to be attacked. (*Trattato* §§19–20, 51.) [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- **Marx** — named at least once in *Trattato* (§1416(3), the "white blackbirds" passage); critiqued more extensively in *Les Systèmes socialistes* vol. 2. [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- **Publilius Syrus** — cited for maxims illustrating derivation types. (*Trattato* §1420.) [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- **Bossuet** — cited as the archetype of dogmatic universal history he refuses to imitate. (*Trattato* §6.) [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- **St. Augustine** — cited at *Trattato* §1438 as parallel to "modern worshippers of Science." [PRIMARY VERIFIED]
- **Georges Sorel** — Pareto corresponded with him; mentioned in the *Trattato*'s editorial apparatus; they shared the analysis of myth as social-cohesion mechanism. (Sorel-Pareto correspondence referenced in Sorel biographies.)
- **Gaetano Mosca** — rivalry over priority of the elite-theory idea, "ill-tempered dispute" (Topoi 2021 article). Pareto's elite is identified via psychological/residue analysis; Mosca's *classe politica* via organisational dominance. They didn't cite each other generously.
- **Adam Smith, Hume, Burke, Joseph de Maistre, Gustave de Molinari** — listed in the Wikipedia bibliography of influences; I did not verify direct citation. [ATTRIBUTED, NOT VERIFIED]

## Contradictions worth preserving (do NOT smooth away)

1. **Liberal economist → disillusioned sociologist.** In *Cours* and early articles he sounds like an orthodox laissez-faire liberal, fighting Italian protectionism. By *Trattato* he treats liberalism itself as a derivation — a faith in reason that misreads how societies actually work. (Liberty Fund 2018 Forte essay; Britannica.)
2. **Economics rejects interpersonal welfare comparison; sociology requires it.** In the *Manuale* he refuses to compare ophelimities; in the *Trattato* he routinely speaks of "social utility" and total residue distribution as if comparable. He flagged this himself — "for the society" vs "of the society" — but the methodological tension remains. (OpenEdition Bruni/McLure scholarship.)
3. **Anti-metaphysical, but produces a near-universal historical law.** Pareto's law of income distribution and his elite-circulation cycle are very strong empirical generalisations that look quasi-metaphysical despite his constant disavowal of metaphysics.
4. **Hostile to socialism, but argues socialism is more politically effective than liberalism.** *Les Systèmes socialistes*: socialism plays on feelings, feelings drive action, therefore socialism wins. This is closer to "socialism is the realist option for a non-logical species" than to "socialism is silly."
5. **Appropriated by Mussolini's regime, but his actual position is classical-liberal "leave me alone."** Pareto was made a senator under Mussolini in 1923 (just months before death). Whether he endorsed fascism is contested; the Forte/Liberty Fund reading is that his liberty was "the liberty of being left alone," not authoritarian. The Jacobin (2026) reading treats him as a precursor of reactionary realism. PRESERVE both readings.
6. **Calls himself an experimentalist but his sociology is almost entirely historical/illustrative.** The *Trattato*'s 2,000+ pages are full of quotations from Latin moralists, news clippings, theology — not experiments. He treats *historical observation* as experimental evidence; critics including Pitirim Sorokin have argued this stretches the term.
7. **Mosca priority dispute.** Pareto and Mosca developed elite theory in parallel, in Italy, in dialogue with the same intellectual scene — and ended in mutual recrimination over who had the idea first. Their methods (psychological residues vs organisational dominance) are different enough that the dispute is partly substantive, but the bitterness exceeds the substance.

## Self-conception

Most defensible reading from the primary text: Pareto saw himself as **an engineer of social facts** — a scientist of "what is" who treats moral and political doctrines as natural phenomena to be observed and classified, the way a chemist classifies compounds. He was contemptuous of writers who confused "what ought to be" with "what is," whether socialist, liberal, Christian, or humanitarian. He saw himself as continuing Machiavelli (force/fraud realism), Walras (mathematical form), and the experimental tradition (Galileo-Newton lineage) — and as exposing Hegel, Comte, Spencer, Marx, and Bossuet as different flavours of the same dogmatic vice.

He was not a systematic political philosopher and would have rejected the label. He produced a typology and a method, and was content to leave the political consequences ambiguous — which is exactly why both reactionaries and welfare-economics liberals have claimed him.
