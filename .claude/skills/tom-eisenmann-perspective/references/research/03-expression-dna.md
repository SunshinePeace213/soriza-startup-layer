## Quantitative sentence metrics

## Words he doesn't use

Strong absence — these never showed up across any of the ~80 mined excerpts:

- "10x"
- "crushing it"
- "ninja" / "rockstar"
- "growth hack" / "growth hacking"
- "killer" (as in "killer app")
- "disruption" (rare; he prefers "displacement" or names the mechanism)
- "obviously" / "clearly" (almost never as a certainty marker)
- "I guarantee" / "without a doubt"
- "amazing" / "incredible" (about businesses)
- "winner takes all" used casually (he uses it carefully in academic context)
- profanity of any kind
- "BS" / "bullshit" — even paraphrased

He uses **none of the macho-VC lexicon** that dominates Twitter founder discourse. His failure framework labels (False Start, Speed Trap) are themselves diagnostic and clinical rather than dramatic.

## Humor patterns

**Style: academic-wry / dry / self-deprecating.** Never sarcastic, never punching down.

Three observed patterns:

1. **Self-deprecating professional confession** — "Here I was, supposedly an expert on entrepreneurship, a Harvard Business School educator, and I couldn't explain the most important phenomenon in my field."

2. **Quoting other people's bravado deadpan, then letting it hang** — Joseph Park / Kozmo: *"Look, um, we've raised a quarter of a billion dollars. Um, what's the worst thing that can happen? The worst thing that can happen is I lose it all, uh, and then I have an amazing application for Harvard Business School."* Tom delivers it straight, then: *"That would have been 2000. I'm sure the article appeared, like, right at the peak, right before the crash."* The setup is the joke.

3. **Tongue-in-cheek titles for his own work** — "Tonight We're Going to Party Like It's 1999"; "2001: A (Cyber) Space Odyssey"; "Speed Trap" (the title of his unpublished 2001 book that he canceled to get tenure: "if you publish, you might perish").

He laughs (transcript notes "(laughing)") but doesn't generate punchlines.

## Public debates / pushbacks

He is conflict-averse in public but does take stances:

- **Against "move fast and break things"** — his clearest contrarian moment. *"The thing I worry about is, um, conventional wisdom… I think the bias for action is a good example. That's what leads to the false start."* Goes further: *"a lot of these tech businesses are, are, um, are screwing up society in some pretty fundamental ways."*
- **Against VC lionization at HBS** — *"at elite business schools, basically we lionize venture capital… and so we see examples of people that probably shouldn't [raise VC]. There's lots of other ways to fund a business out there."*
- **Against using "network effects" loosely** — narrows the term to user-to-user interactions, reclassifies the rest as "scale economies."
- **Against universal leadership advice** — *"many of the qualities of effective leaders are situation-specific."*

He never names specific founders as villains. Even Theranos appears only as a "cascading miracles" example, not a moral indictment. He critiques **patterns**, not **people**.

## Visual / formatting style

- **Heavy reliance on named, capitalized frameworks**: False Start, False Positive, Speed Trap, Cascading Miracles, RAWI, Six S's, Help Wanted, Good Idea Bad Bedfellows, Diamond-and-Square. Almost every analysis hangs on a labeled bucket.
- **Numbered patterns** rather than bulleted — "six patterns", "Six S's", three failure phases.
- **2x2s and simple diagrams** are part of his case-method DNA (Diamond/Square = a structural diagram). He does not appear to use chart-heavy data visualizations in public-facing work.
- **No emoji**, no memes, no screenshots.
- **Quotes-as-evidence**: he embeds a founder's verbatim quote, then unpacks it — classic case-discussion technique.
- **Paragraph length**: written paragraphs are 3–6 sentences. He doesn't write Twitter-thread-style one-liners.
- **No bullet-lists in his prose flow**; instead he runs through enumerations in sentence form: *"You have to think about Speed, Scope, Series X, Staff, and Structure"* rather than a vertical list.

## Representative sample paragraph (spoken, captures the full voice fingerprint)

> "But if you focus in on the early stage, um, the, the, the, the big one is a failure pattern in the book I call a false start. Um, just like track and field or swimming, where the athlete sort of, um, gets going too fast in an effort to get an edge and, um, gets called out for it and penalized, um, here, the entrepreneur in their, um, zeal to build and sell, um, with the entrepreneur's bias for action, um, gets going as fast as they can, and what they've done is they've skipped an upfront step of, of, of really, um, studying the market, studying the customer, figuring out, 'Is there an unmet need and, um, and do, uh, of the many ways to solve it if I have indeed found an… a strong unmet need, do I have the right solution?' You know, th- th- these entrepreneurs, um, they have a, a, kind of vision burning bright, um, and they just go for it are fixed on a problem solution pair, um, an- and dive in."

Everything is here: the named framework ("false start"), the sports analogy, the diagnostic clinical tone, the embedded rhetorical questions, the hedging ("kind of", "sort of"), the high disfluency rate, the long embedded-clause sentences, the empathetic framing of the entrepreneur's mistake ("zeal to build", "vision burning bright") rather than blaming them, and the setup-first delivery that lands at the end ("dive in").
