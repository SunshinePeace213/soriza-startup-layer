## 1. Eisenmann's exact definition of startup failure

**The general definition:** failure is "an outcome that falls short of expectations." Eisenmann immediately qualifies that this is useless on its own — you must ask **which expectations and whose expectations**. (Founders, investors, employees, and society can each have different expectations, and an outcome that disappoints one may satisfy another.)

**The operational definition he uses for the book's analysis:** a startup has failed when **its early investors did not — or never will — make money**; i.e., the venture never returns capital to those who funded it. He explicitly notes this is an imperfect proxy because it ignores societal impact and whether the outcome matched the *founder's* personal ambitions. He scopes the study further to ventures that **initially showed promise** and then failed **due more to an entrepreneur's avoidable mistakes than to pure misfortune** (so a startup killed only by Covid, or one that never had a shot, is outside his core analysis).

**Failure ≠ merely shutting down.** Running out of cash is the *symptom, not the cause* — his signature analogy: blaming "ran out of cash" is "like saying a victim died from loss of blood. Was that due to a gunshot wound?" Shutting down is an event; *failure* is the underlying pattern of mistakes that made the shutdown inevitable. A venture can shut down and still not be a "failure" in the disappointment sense if it met expectations (e.g., a graceful pivot or modest acquihire that returned capital).

## 2. "Horse or jockey?" — and his actual conclusion

The VC folk wisdom: when forced to choose, back the **jockey** (the founder/team) over the **horse** (the opportunity/bet) — an able founder can fix a mediocre market.

**Eisenmann's conclusion: it is NOT always the horse or the jockey** — that binary is the wrong frame. (His HBR article's literal subtitle is *"It's Not Always the Horse or the Jockey."*) His data show failure is usually **neither cleanly** — it is the **misalignment among a whole constellation of players and opportunity elements**: founders, *other* team members, investors, and strategic partners, set against the four opportunity elements. A great jockey on a great horse still loses if the *bad bedfellows* (employees, partners, investors the founder selected) drag the venture down. So the single-cause "bet the jockey" maxim hides at least six distinct failure mechanisms. This is the central move of the book: **refuse single-cause stories**; diagnose *which alignment broke*.

## 3. The RAWI test — Ready, Able, Willing, Impelled

**Important correction to the brief's framing:** In the book, RAWI is **not** a "should I found a startup?" go/no-go gate. It is a **late-stage SCALING decision gate** — *"should we step on the gas and pursue hypergrowth NOW?"* It sits in Part II (Scaling) and gates the Speed Trap / Cascading Miracles risks. ("RAWI" is named after the Arabic *rāwī*, a reciter of epic poems — because these late-stage failures are "epic.") If **any** of the four answers is "no," the founder should think hard before accelerating.

- **Ready?** Does the venture have **product-market fit** — real customer need *and* a clear path to profitability — and can it *sustain* PMF as it expands? Is the target market big enough to keep growing, and is the margin high enough to survive the price/cost squeeze as rivals enter and cheaper-to-acquire customers run out?
- **Able?** Can it actually **access the human and capital resources** hypergrowth demands — hire and *train* large numbers of new workers, coordinate them, and raise the capital?
- **Willing?** Do the founders *genuinely want* to go fast — get rich, chase the mission, take the risk, make the work/family tradeoffs — and will they accept the **equity dilution** and **loss of board control** (risk of being fired) that big outside capital brings? Does fast growth advance the *original* vision?
- **Impelled?** Is there a **competitive/strategic reason** to grow now? Favorable market structure — **network effects, high switching costs, scale economies, first-mover lock-in** — that would let rivals run a land grab if you *don't* move? ("Will you wake a sleeping giant / invite clones?" cuts both ways: a real winner-take-all race *impels* speed.)

The hardest, most-ignored of the four is usually **Impelled** — founders feel impelled by their *cap table and funding clock*, not by genuine competitive dynamics, and conflate the two.

## 4. The Diamond-and-Square framework

The core **early-stage opportunity-assessment** diagnostic. A promising startup requires the **dynamic alignment of all eight pieces** — four in the *diamond* (the opportunity / strategy) and four in the *square* (the resources / people). Strength in any one piece does not save you; the failure is almost always a **misalignment** between two or three. "Dynamic" matters: as the venture matures the opportunity evolves and so must the resources — alignment that held at seed can break at Series A.

**The DIAMOND — the opportunity (4 corners):**
1. **Customer Value Proposition (CVP)** — the unmet need and why your solution is better.
2. **Technology & Operations** — what you build and how you deliver/operate it.
3. **Marketing / Go-to-Market** — how you reach, acquire, and retain customers.
4. **Profit Formula / Cash-Flow Formula** — unit economics: revenue, cost structure, margins, the path to making money.

**The SQUARE — the resources (4 corners):**
1. **Founders** — founder-market fit, complementary skills, decision rights, relationships.
2. **Team** (the rest of the team / early employees) — the specialists and managers around the founders.
3. **Investors** — value-add (or value-subtract) of outside capital and the board.
4. **Strategic Partners** — suppliers, channel, platform, and corporate partners whose priorities must align with the venture's.

Lineage (per the skill's own notes and Eisenmann's acknowledgments): the **diamond** corners are adapted from the Christensen/Johnson **business-model** tradition; the **square** corners come from the **Sahlman/Stevenson "people–deal–opportunity"** HBS tradition. *Bad Bedfellows* failures are diagnosed as breaks in the **square**; *False Starts/False Positives* as breaks in the **diamond** (especially CVP ↔ Marketing).

Note on a common error: several secondary summaries mislabel the diamond as "Idea / Execution / Potential / Market." That is a **garbled** rendering — the authoritative corners are **CVP, Technology & Operations, Marketing/GTM, Profit Formula** (diamond) and **Founders, Team, Investors, Partners** (square).

Sources: https://www.nfx.com/post/hidden-patterns-startup-failure ; https://blog.sdgstrategy.com/2021/06/10/the-diamond-and-square-framework/ ; https://www.vefund.io/en/diamond-and-square-framework-assessing-startups-and-understanding-startup-failure/

## 5. The "Catch-22" of entrepreneurship (第22號軍令)

The user's "第22號軍令 / Order No. 22" is indeed a **Catch-22** reference, and it names a real, central concept in the book — **the entrepreneur's risk-vs-resources bind**:

> You can't get resources without reducing risk; you can't reduce risk until you get started; and to get started you need resources.

This is the foundational paradox of early-stage entrepreneurship: investors (and talent, partners) want de-risked evidence before committing, but you need their resources to *produce* that evidence. Eisenmann frames the entire **launch** discipline as managing this bind — and the early-stage failure patterns are what happen when founders mishandle it (e.g., burning scarce resources on an unvalidated build = False Start).

**The four tactics to manage the bind** (you can either *reduce the risk* or *reduce the resource requirement*):
1. **Resolve the risk** — kill the uncertainty cheaply and rigorously *before* you commit big resources. This is what **lean testing / customer discovery** is *for*. (Preferred move.)
2. **Defer the risk** — stage the commitment so you only take on each risk when you must (staged financing: seed → A → B; milestone-gated spend).
3. **Shift the risk** — move it to a party better able to bear it (strategic partners, customers who pre-pay, suppliers who carry inventory).
4. **Get people to ignore the risk** — narrative / "reality-distortion-field" persuasion that gets stakeholders to commit despite the risk. (Eisenmann treats this as real but the most dangerous and least honest lever — it's how *Cascading Miracles* ventures attract capital they shouldn't.)

## 6. The SIX failure patterns (verified grouping + canonical examples)

### EARLY-STAGE (Part I — "Launching") — breaks in the Diamond-and-Square

**(1) Good Idea, Bad Bedfellows** — *right opportunity, wrong people.* A genuinely attractive opportunity, but the founder fails to mobilize and align the **square** (resources): co-founder conflict or missing founder-market fit, weak team, low-value-add investors, or strategic partners whose priorities diverge. The failure is in the *people/resource* corners, not the idea.
- **Canonical example: Quincy Apparel** (Alexandra Nelson & Christina Wallace, HBS grads, ~$1M raised, dead in ~1 year). Two first-time co-CEOs with no apparel-industry experience, an ambiguous 50/50 leadership split, and outsourced manufacturing without domain expertise. (This is the venture Eisenmann had angel-invested in — the wound that launched the whole research program.)

**(2) False Starts** — *building before validating.* Founders, eager to build and ship, **skip customer discovery** — the upfront interviews/research to confirm the problem and the solution — and waste time and capital on a first product that misses the mark. The "fail fast / launch before you're ready" maxim, *misapplied* to skip the work. Multiple costly pivots follow.
- **Canonical example: Triangulate** (Sunil Nagaraj) — an online-dating startup that built its matching engine *before* validating demand for either the matching or "wingman" concept; ~3 pivots in ~2 years.

**(3) False Positives** — *misreading early-adopter delight as mainstream signal.* Early adopters love the product for reasons the **mainstream won't share**; the founder reads this as confirmed PMF and scales into a market that doesn't actually want it. Early adopters ≠ mainstream customers.
- **Canonical example: Baroo** (Lindsay Hyde) — a "high-touch" pet-concierge service. Its first building (new construction in South Boston, plus a transient film-crew population with no existing pet-care relationships) produced a *non-representative* early-adopter signal; the team assumed those users represented the broad market. **Note:** Baroo is the canonical *False Positive* example, **but** its story also illustrates the late-stage cascade — the false positive set it on an unsustainable scaling course, so secondary sources sometimes (mistakenly) file Baroo under *Speed Trap*. In the book's primary mapping it is **False Positives**; the *Speed Trap* anchor is **Fab.com**.
- **Counter-example that defines the boundary: Dropbox** — Drew Houston *deliberately* designed for mainstream users from day one, so its early-adopter signal happened to align with the mainstream.

### LATE-STAGE (Part II — "Scaling") — breaks in the scaling system (see Six S's, §9)

**(4) Speed Traps** — *hypergrowth that outruns the economics and the org.* Growing too fast triggers an **LTV/CAC squeeze** (later customers cost more to acquire and are worth less), while the organization — operations, tech, team, partner relationships — unravels under the strain. Growth itself becomes the killer.
- **Canonical example: Fab.com** — flash-sale design e-commerce; raised ~$336M, ~$1B valuation; early adopters loved the distinctive curated taste; grew too fast and cratered when it pushed into the mainstream and the unit economics collapsed.

**(5) Help Wanted** — *the right resource is missing at the moment it's needed.* The venture still has PMF, but at a critical scaling moment it suddenly lacks **senior management talent OR growth capital** (or both) — e.g., it can't recruit the VPs needed to run a bigger org, or investor sentiment shifts and the next round evaporates. A resource shortfall, not a strategy error.
- **Canonical example: Dot & Bo** (online home-furnishings retailer; Chapter 8). Strong demand, but repeated failed senior hires (three failed VPs) and a capital crunch at the wrong moment.

**(6) Cascading Miracles** — *the venture needs many low-probability things to ALL go right.* Bold, ambitious plans requiring some combination of: radical changes in customer behavior; partnerships with powerful incumbents; technological breakthroughs; regulatory relaxation; and vast capital — each one "do or die." Because the overall probability is the **product** of the individual probabilities, if any factor goes to zero the whole expression goes to zero. (Eisenmann credits the framing's spirit to John Malone/TCI.)
- **Canonical examples: Jibo** (the $900 social robot — crushed when Amazon shipped the $200 Echo/Alexa) and **Better Place** (Shai Agassi's EV battery-swap charging network; ~$900M+ raised, 2007–2013). Other-named: Iridium, Segway.
- **Eisenmann's honesty rule:** survivors do not disprove the math. **Tesla, SpaceX, Moderna** sit in the *same risk class* as Better Place, Theranos, Iridium — same pattern, different outcome. The correct response isn't "never attempt it" — it's **multiply the probabilities honestly and finance for the runway/patience the math demands.**

## 7. Managing a failing venture — the endgame (引擎空轉, "engine idling")

Part III, Stage 1–2: deciding **whether/when to pull the plug**, then winding down with integrity. Eisenmann's core warning: founders **delay too long** because the intense emotions of a struggling venture (or the avoidance of them) distort judgment — "the longer a founder hopes in vain that new investors or an attractive acquisition will save the day, the longer he is burning through capital," and the longer employees waste their time on a lost cause instead of moving to their next act. The "engine idling" is exactly this: burning fuel going nowhere while hope substitutes for evidence. **For bet-the-company decisions, distrust your gut** — emotion is highest precisely when the call is most existential.

**The endgame options, roughly in the sequence a founder should run them when failure signals appear:**

1. **Pivot to a new business model.** If the *diamond* can be re-aimed at a validated problem (not the one you assumed), pivot — but pivot on **evidence**, before the cash and team are gone. A late, desperate pivot rarely works; a timely one can.
2. **Bridge round from EXISTING investors.** The first call when things get dire is to your *current* investors — they know the company and have incentive to protect their stake. Ask whether they'll bridge you to a milestone. Honest framing matters; do not raise a bridge you can't justify with a credible path.
3. **Raise from NEW investors.** Harder and slower in a downturn; new investors price the distress. Realistically a long shot once decline is visible — don't let the *hope* of it keep the engine idling.
4. **Sell the company / acquihire.** Talk to **strategic buyers** and potential **acquihirers** *before* you're out of cash — a sale or acquihire that returns *some* capital and lands employees softly is far better than a zero. Negotiating leverage evaporates as the bank balance does, so start early.
5. **Lay off employees.** If you must downsize to extend runway or wind down, do it **cleanly and humanely**: comply with notice law (e.g., the U.S. **WARN Act** — typically ~60 days' notice for larger layoffs/closures), be honest, give references, help people land their next role.
6. **Orderly shutdown / dissolution.** When there's no viable path, shut down *gracefully* rather than letting it collapse. Eisenmann's stakeholder obligations: **pay vendors/creditors properly; return remaining capital to investors whenever possible; take care of customers (transition them, honor commitments); and support employees into their next roles.** Creditor priority in a wind-down generally runs: **secured creditors → employee obligations → taxes → unsecured creditors.** A clean wind-down preserves **relationships, reputation, and integrity** — which is what lets the founder come back.

The throughline: **shutdown is an extended ethical responsibility, not a single moment** — and doing it well is the bridge to recovery.

## 8. Founder recovery & resilience (重整旗鼓, "regroup")

Part III, Stage 3 — the aftermath. Eisenmann uses a **clinical-psychology** frame and a **three-phase** recovery model.

**Grief is real and first.** A founder must cope with the **grief, depression, anger, and guilt** that accompany any major personal setback — he maps it onto the **Kübler-Ross** stages (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance). He also names a **rumination ↔ distraction** dynamic: founders oscillate between obsessing over the failure and avoiding it; the goal is to **balance distraction with reflection** rather than drown in either.

**Three phases:**
1. **Recovery** — heal from the emotional battering of the shutdown. Don't make big career decisions while grieving.
2. **Reflection** — move *past* blaming others or external bad luck (resisting the **fundamental attribution error** of crediting your failure to circumstances). Through honest introspection, understand **what went wrong, what role *you* played, and what you'd do differently** — and learn your own strengths/weaknesses as entrepreneur, manager, leader.
3. **Reentry** — use those lessons to decide whether to start another venture or take a different path.

**Resilience facts & advice:**
- **~Half of failed founders come back** and try again. The ones who recover best are those who managed the shutdown gracefully and can articulate, credibly, what happened, what they did wrong, what they learned, and what they'll do differently.
- **Reputation is preserved by behavior at the end**, not by the outcome — graceful wind-down and stakeholder care protect the ability to raise, hire, and partner next time. Failure is a **recoverable career event**, not a scarlet letter.
- **Be intentional:** mine the lessons deliberately; focus on tactical, controllable improvements for next time rather than self-flagellation or blame-shifting.

## 9. Load-bearing extras beyond the six patterns

**The Six S's — the late-stage scaling diagnostic.** Part II's structured checklist for *whether/how* a startup can scale without a Speed Trap or Help Wanted failure. The six management challenges of hypergrowth:
1. **Speed** — how fast can you build the brand, tap network effects/scale economies, and saturate the market?
2. **Scope** — expansion decisions: new geographies, new products, vertical integration, growth-by-acquisition — and whether you can manage them.
3. **Series X** (funding) — fast growth fights profitability; you're perpetually capital-hungry. Can you attract the right *growth-stage* investors?
4. **Staff** — early startups run on generalists; scaling needs *specialists*. Can you transition the team and the CEO role?
5. **Structure** — early chaos must give way to process and org design without killing speed.
6. **Shared Values** — lock down culture / "true north" so decisions, hiring, and communication stay coherent at scale.
RAWI gates *whether* to scale; the Six S's diagnose *what will break* if you do. Source: https://blog.sdgstrategy.com/2021/06/14/the-six-s-framework/

**The "fail fast" critique — and the lean-startup relationship.** Eisenmann is **inside the lean-startup tent**, not outside it (he co-authored the 2011 HBS note "Hypothesis-Driven Entrepreneurship" with Eric Ries). His critique is an **internal correction**: "fail fast" and "launch before you're ready" have been **misapplied as permission to skip customer discovery** — the upfront interview phase Steve Blank insists must come *before* you build. Skipping it produces **False Starts**. His position: do **rigorous customer discovery first**, *then* iterate fast. So the six patterns are not anti-lean; they catalog the ways lean is **half-applied** (founders cherry-pick "build/launch fast" and drop "validate first"). Source: https://sayyes.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/HBR-Why-Start-ups-Fail-Tom-Eisenmann.pdf ; https://www.adammendler.com/blog/harvard-business-school-tom-eisenmann-author-why-startups-fail/

**Conventional-wisdom allergy.** Every chapter takes a piece of gospel founder advice — "Just do it," "Bring passion," "Be persistent," "Grow at all costs," "Be scrappy," "Fail fast," "Listen to your customers" — and shows it's each *partially right and systematically misapplied*: correct only within a boundary condition the founder usually can't locate. Persistence is virtue *and* stubbornness-against-the-data; bias-for-action is edge *and* the cause of False Starts. Source: https://hbr.org/2021/05/why-start-ups-fail

**Pivots.** Treated as a normal, healthy mechanism *when evidence-driven and timely* (Triangulate pivoted repeatedly but too late/too expensively because it built first). A pivot is a re-aiming of the diamond at a validated problem — not a Hail-Mary after the cash is gone.

**Research base / methodological caveat.** Built from **~470 founder surveys, ~100 interviews, and ~20 HBS cases**, plus two decades teaching. The honest hole: he **selected on the dependent variable** — studied only failures, no control group — so the patterns are diagnostic of *how a given corpse died*, not proven *sufficient causes* absent in successes. Source: https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/623123/why-startups-fail-by-tom-eisenmann/ ; https://hbr.org/2021/05/why-start-ups-fail

**AI-era thesis (post-book, preliminary).** Eisenmann has argued through 2025 that AI lowering build cost will *raise* the absolute failure rate (more thin LLM-wrapper products without proprietary data, deep integration, or habitual use), not lower it. Source: https://stvp.stanford.edu/podcasts/tom-eisenmann/
