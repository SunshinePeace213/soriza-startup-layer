# Outreach Methods — the leverage ladder

Named for *The Mom Test* Ch. 6 ("Finding conversations"). The pipeline already says **warm-first** —
but it only operationalizes "warm = people who already publicly complained" (the warm list ★). This
reference adds the rest of the book's menu, and the part the pipeline lacks entirely: **ways to
*manufacture* warmth** so prospects come to *you* and take you seriously.

**How to use this:** the design worker does **not** dump every method into the run-pack. It picks the
**2–3 highest-leverage methods that fit *this* segment + *this* founder's situation** and writes those
into the run-pack's *Outreach strategy* block. The full menu lives here; the pack stays focused (the book
is emphatically anti-busywork — "go build your dang company already").

Methods are ordered **best-leverage first**. Climb as high as the situation allows; cold is the floor,
not the goal.

---

## The two tiers (a hard execution boundary)

Every method below is also tagged **Tier A** or **Tier B**, because *who executes it* is a real safety
boundary in this pipeline:

- **Tier A — channels the FOUNDER builds.** Multi-day/week, founder-driven: warm-intro asks, meetups,
  teaching, blogging, landing pages, the clever-host hack. **Cowork never runs these** — they are not
  per-prospect emails. They produce warm leads over time.
- **Tier B — per-prospect DRAFTS.** Warm-reply DMs to public complainers (the **founder** posts these from
  their own account, platform rules) + cold emails (**Cowork** sends, **gated per batch**, on the founder's
  approval). All V.F.W.P.A.-framed. Per-prospect, not channel-building.

Keep the two visibly separate in the run-pack so a headless Cowork run never mistakes "organize a meetup"
for something to email.

---

## Tier 1 — Bring them to you (flag-planting)

> They find *you*. That flips the power dynamic: they're not suspicious you're wasting their time, they
> take you more seriously, and they *want* to help. Highest leverage in the book.

### Organize a meetup — *Tier A*
Host a "[role] happy hour" / roundtable. You're credible simply because you sent the invite. The book:
*"the most unfair trick I know for rapid customer learning… it also instantly bootstraps your industry
credibility."*
- ✅ Run an "HR ops happy hour" → spend the evening hearing HR people's real problems, as the host.
- ❌ Attend someone else's event as an anonymous attendee and hope to corner people.

### Speaking & teaching — *Tier A*
Workshops, talks, online videos, free office hours / consulting. You refine your message, fill a room
with people who take you seriously, and learn which parts resonate *before building*.
- ✅ Teach "how we cut month-end close from 4 days to 1" → the attendees who lean in are your leads.
- ❌ Give a polished product pitch from the stage (that's selling, not learning).

### Industry blogging — *Tier A*
Write the post, ask readers to get in touch. Even with **zero audience** it warms cold email: people
check your domain, see the blog, and decide you're worth a reply.
- ✅ "I'm researching how finance teams version their spreadsheets — if that's your pain, reply."
- ❌ A launch-announcement blog with a "sign up" CTA and no invitation to talk.

### Get clever — the host hack — *Tier A*
Invent a reason to convene the people you need. The book's example: a founder who needed top-university
decision-makers set up a semi-monthly "knowledge-exchange" call between 3 elite departments and let
others dial in — **absorbing their credibility and getting direct access to leads.**
- ✅ Host a cross-company "knowledge exchange" on the exact problem you're probing.
- ❌ Cold-email the same decision-makers asking for "30 minutes to pick your brain."

### Landing pages — *Tier A* ⭐
Joel Gascoigne's Buffer test: a value-prop page that collects emails. **The book's real lesson is the
opposite of the folklore** — it wasn't the conversion rate that mattered, it was *the conversations from
emailing every single person who signed up and saying hello.* Paul Graham's generic launch is the same
idea: ship, see who likes it most, reach out to those users.
- ✅ Landing page → email every signup → start a Mom-Test conversation. The page is a **lead source for
  conversations.**
- ❌ "847 signups and a 12% conversion → validated demand." **Signups are not demand.**
  > **⚠️ Vanity-metric guardrail.** A landing page's number is a *vanity metric*, never validation
  > (cf. the `lean-startup` skill, and the synthesis honesty rule). It tells you who to *talk to*, not
  > that you're right. Only behaviour in the conversations that follow counts. Do not let a signup count
  > enter `customer-discovery.md` as evidence — it has no ledger grade.

## Tier 2 — Warm intros (the goal)

> A mutual contact vouches for you. Conversations are *infinitely* easier. Kevin Bacon's 7 degrees
> applies: you can reach anyone if you ask a couple of times.

### 7-degrees-of-bacon — *Tier A*
Everyone knows someone; just ask out loud. The book's story: the founder who stood on a chair in a
coworking space and yelled "does anyone know someone at McKinsey?" → 3 chats, a diary full of intros.
- ✅ "I need to talk to 3 wedding planners — who do you know?" (consumer intros are even easier).

### Industry advisors — *Tier A*
~0.5% equity, a monthly meeting each → a steady weekly stream of credible intros without burdening any
one person. Good advisors look like good flagship customers — you can poach them from early conversations.

### Universities / professors — *Tier A*
A goldmine for intros: professors get grant funding from senior industry people who are self-selected to
be excited about new projects. Easy to reach — they post their emails.

### Investors — *Tier A*
Top-tier investors do cold intros to practically any industry, and can land higher-tier advisors than you
could alone. Ask: who's already bought into the idea, and who can they connect you to?

### Cash in old favours — *Tier A*
Reply to every "sounds great, let me know how I can help" email and call in the intro now. You'll get
ignored a lot — who cares; you need a few conversations, not a low failure rate. (Use sparingly; it can
burn bridges.)

## Tier 3 — Go to them

> You make the approach, so you're on the back foot — but it still works, especially early.

### Immerse yourself where they are — *Tier A*
Live in their world. The book's author hit the conference circuit, gave free talks everywhere, did
favours for organizers, and soon had all the conversations he could handle.

### Seize serendipity — *Tier A*
Keep your big-3 questions loaded so a chance encounter with a dream customer becomes a conversation, not
a "let's grab coffee sometime" (exactly like everyone else). Keep it casual.

### Find a good excuse — *Tier A*
If it's a topic you both care about, invent an excuse to talk and your idea never needs to come up. The
canonical one: *"I'm doing PhD research on the problems around X — could I ask you a couple of questions?"*

## Tier 4 — Cold (the goal is to stop needing this)

> Cold calls/email are a gamble and put you on the back foot. The point of cold outreach is to **find
> clever ways to generate warm intros so you stop doing it.** When you must, frame it well (below).

### Cold email / DMs — *Tier B* (Cowork-sendable, gated)
The warm list ★ (public complainers) and cold prospects both get **V.F.W.P.A.-framed** drafts.

---

## Framing the ask — "Very Few Wizards Properly Ask" (V.F.W.P.A.)

Every Tier-B draft (and every meeting opener) gets these five beats. Order can flex; combine into 1–2
sentences if a beat is obvious.

| Beat | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Vision** | the problem/world you're improving — **not your idea** | "I'm trying to make desk rental less painful for new businesses." |
| **Framing** | what stage you're at; that you have nothing to sell | "We're just starting and don't have anything to sell." |
| **Weakness** | the specific thing you don't understand (invites help, signals you're not a time-waster) | "I've only seen the tenant side — the landlord economics are a fog to me." |
| **Pedestal** | why *they specifically* can help | "You've rented desks for years and could cut through it fast." |
| **Ask** | for help, low-cost | "Got time for a chat in the next couple of weeks?" |

**Bad openers to avoid** (they set off alarm bells):
- ❌ "Thanks for agreeing to this **interview**…" → sounds boring and formal.
- ❌ "Can I get your **opinion** on what we're doing?" → signals neediness / fishing for approval.
- ❌ "Do you have time for a quick **coffee/chat**?" → no expectation set → reads as a time-waster.

**The advisory flip (mindset, not a line):** go in looking for *advisors*, not customers. It kills the
needy vibe, keeps you in control (you're evaluating them), and means you always know why you're there.
Don't announce it; just orient that way.

---

## Two cross-cutting rules from Ch. 6

- **Start in person, not on a call.** Phone/Skype loses body language, the casual-friend dynamic, and the
  future warm intros that in-person meetings generate. Calls become scripted interviews — the exact
  formality we're avoiding. Move to calls only as a deliberate volume trade-off, never the default.
- **How many?** A focused segment saturates in **3–5 conversations**; keep going until you stop hearing
  new things. If you're **>10 in and the answers are still all over the map, your segment is too fuzzy** —
  tighten it (route back to `/sharpen-hypothesis`), don't just collect more interviews.

## Choosing for the run-pack (worker guidance)

Pick **2–3** methods, climbing as high up the ladder as the situation allows:
- **Reachable public complainers already mined** (kill-scan demand-scan)? → warm list ★ (Tier B) is your
  fastest start; add one Tier-1 flag-planting play to compound it.
- **Founder has a network / advisors / investors near this space?** → lead with warm intros (Tier 2).
- **Niche with active public venues but few named complainers?** → immersion + a meetup or landing page.
- **Founder already writes/teaches/speaks?** → blogging/teaching is nearly free leverage — use it.
- **No warmth available at all?** → V.F.W.P.A. cold (Tier B) as the floor, while you build one Tier-A
  channel in parallel.

Name, per chosen method, *why it fits this segment* and whether it's **Tier A (founder runs)** or
**Tier B (Cowork-sendable, gated)**. Never list all twelve — focus is the point.
