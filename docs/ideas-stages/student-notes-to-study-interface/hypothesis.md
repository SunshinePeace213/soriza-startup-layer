# Hypothesis: Student Note-to-Study-Interface

**Status:** Provisional, to-be-tested

---

## Core Hypothesis (Testable Sentence)

High-school or university students preparing for high-stakes standardized exams (DSE, GRE, CPA, bar) who currently store notes in Notion, Obsidian, or Google Drive spend **2–4 hours weekly during exam prep season** manually reorganizing the same notes across multiple formats (flashcards, concept maps, revision calendars) to match their preferred learning mode. This manual reorganization overhead competes with higher-value study time. A single app that ingests their existing notes and renders the same underlying data in multiple student-selectable study interfaces (spaced-repetition deck, concept map, revision calendar, Pomodoro timer view) — without requiring manual reformatting — would recover those 2–4 hours per week and shift time from reorganization to deepening understanding.

---

## Four Dimensions (Provisional)

### WHO
**Named role + context + segment:**
- **Role:** High-school or university student
- **Context:** Actively preparing for a high-stakes, standardized exam (DSE, GRE, CPA, bar exam)
- **Segment:** Already using digital note-taking tools (Notion, Obsidian, Google Drive, Apple Notes)
- **Geography:** Hong Kong, Greater China, English-language markets (aligned with founder's trilingual reach and location)

### HOW OFTEN
**Frequency:**
- Weekly, during exam prep season (typically 4–12 weeks before the exam date)
- Most acute during the final 4–6 weeks when students are consolidating and drilling

### HOW SEVERE
**Cost / pain magnitude:**
- **Time cost:** 2–4 hours per week spent on manual reorganization (copy-paste, reformatting, creating flashcards, splitting notes across apps)
- **Magnitude:** High — exam timelines are fixed, time is non-recoverable, and every hour spent reorganizing is an hour not spent on active recall, problem-solving, or conceptual deepening
- **Opportunity cost:** Direct displacement of high-ROI study activities in a time-constrained, high-stakes window

### STATUS QUO
**What they do about it now:**
- **Option A (Active adaptation):** Manually reorganize by copying content between tools (Notion → Anki, slide images → Google Docs), create study guides and flashcards by hand, or split notes into multiple documents (one per study method)
- **Option B (Sub-optimal coping):** Study from a single format (e.g., static PDFs or linearly-scanned notes) and forgo multi-modal learning benefits, accepting lower retention
- **Option C (Outsourcing):** Use generic flashcard apps (Quizlet, Anki) that require re-entry of content or hire tutors/study groups to reformat materials

---

## Rationale for Advancement

All four dimensions are **specific and testable:**
- WHO names a role, context, segment, and existing behavior (not "students" generically)
- HOW OFTEN is quantified as a frequency (weekly, during a defined season)
- HOW SEVERE specifies a magnitude (2–4 hrs/week) with justification (fixed exam timeline)
- STATUS QUO names the actual current processes (manual copy-paste, flashcard creation, multi-app fragmentation)

The core hypothesis is **falsifiable** — it can be disproven by evidence:
- Interview students in the segment: ask if they spend 2–4 hrs/week reorganizing notes → if not, hypothesis fails
- Ask whether the proposed solution (multi-format rendering) would eliminate that time spend → if they say no or that they'd spend it on other overhead, hypothesis fails
- Pilot the tool with students and measure actual time on reorganization vs. study → if time savings don't materialize, hypothesis fails
- Measure study outcomes (exam scores, self-reported retention) with vs. without the tool → if no improvement, hypothesis is challenged

**Note:** This is a sharpening of an idea-stage hypothesis, not a validated claim. All dimensions remain provisional pending customer discovery and testing.
