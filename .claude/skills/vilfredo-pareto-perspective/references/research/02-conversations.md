# Agent 2: Pareto's "Conversations" — Letters, Polemics, Disputes

**Sourcing convention:**
- **(HE SAID)** = direct quote from Pareto's pen (letter, article, treatise footnote, lecture text)
- **(OTHERS SAID)** = contemporary or scholarly characterization
- **(INFERENCE)** = my reading of the pattern, flagged as such

Pareto's "conversation register" is found in five places: (1) his 33-year correspondence with Maffeo Pantaleoni, (2) the open-letter exchange with Croce in *Giornale degli Economisti* (1900–01), (3) his *cronache* in that journal (1893–97) and his late articles in *Gerarchia* and *La Nación* (1922–23), (4) the prefaces, footnotes, and parenthetical asides of *Les Systèmes socialistes* (1902) and the *Trattato* (1916), and (5) scattered letters to Placci, Sensini, Murray, and others. The register is unmistakable: clinical, mordant, sarcastic, contemptuous of "humanitarian" cant, increasingly tired.

---

## The Pareto–Pantaleoni Correspondence

Pantaleoni (1857–1924) was the marginalist economist who pulled Pareto into academic economics and stayed his closest friend until death. The published *Lettere a Maffeo Pantaleoni 1890–1923* (ed. De Rosa, 1962, 3 vols.) is the single richest source of "private Pareto." Bobbio called it the closest thing we have to a Pareto diary.

**Tone across the correspondence (INFERENCE from scholarly summaries):** Pareto wrote to Pantaleoni in shirtsleeves — flatly, often crudely, with running jokes about Italian politicians as "scoundrels" and "asses," and a settled habit of treating his own discoveries as obvious and his opponents as either dishonest or unintelligent.

### Early-period quotes (1890s, still a militant liberal):

- **(HE SAID, 25 Dec 1891, to Pantaleoni):** "With all the respect I have for Walras, I believe that if he had fought protection from his Chair at Lausanne he would have been more useful than by giving a more or less rigorous demonstration that market equilibrium can be achieved by increasing price with demand." — A young Pareto telling his best friend that his own predecessor at Lausanne wasted his life on technicalities. The note of impatience with pure theory in the face of political evil is already there.

- **(HE SAID, 1892, to Pantaleoni):** "I believe, with Spencer, that human society progresses by distancing itself from the military type, to draw near to the industrial." — The optimistic Spencerian Pareto, who would not survive the decade.

### Mid-period quotes (1900s, the turn):

- **(HE SAID, 1900, to Pantaleoni, paraphrased in Mingardi):** "There was once a time when my desire was to straighten the limbs of the halt; now I laugh at their infirmity." — The single most compressed statement of his evolution: from reformer to clinician. The verb is *rido* — "I laugh." This is the turn.

- **(HE SAID, c. 1907, from correspondence cited in Mingardi):** "The greater what is today called liberty grew, the smaller became what in other times was meant by liberty, namely, the scope for individual action."

### Late-period quotes (1913–1923, the disenchanted sociologist):

- **(HE SAID, 1913):** His youthful efforts "in defence of economic freedom" were "all wasted time" — written when he still "did not understand the profound difference that exists between the operating [*operare*] and the knowing [*sapere*]."

- **(HE SAID, November 1920, to Pantaleoni):** "What meagre results we have achieved! What scant effects did have our work!" — Two old liberals comparing the ledger of their lifetimes.

- **(HE SAID, on the sociology project, vol. III of the correspondence):** "I am going ahead with my Manual of Sociology. I am pleased with it, but I fear that the public will not understand me and will not want to understand me. My approach is too new..." — The mix of confidence and isolation is characteristic.

- **(HE SAID, on academic ceremony):** "It seems to me ridiculous to go and whip out heraldic and even professorial titles if one is going to publish two big volumes incorporating twenty years' studies." — Pareto refusing pomp, in his usual dismissive register.

**(INFERENCE) The Pantaleoni letters are where you see that Pareto was not a cold man — he was a warm man who had concluded that warmth was useless in politics.** With Pantaleoni he is affectionate, gossipy, and unguarded about contempt. Everyone else got the clinical mask.

## The Pareto–Croce Debate (1900–1902, *Giornale degli Economisti*)

This was an open-letter polemic, not a private exchange — both knew they were performing for the same readership. It was reprinted in *International Economic Papers* as the canonical text on the methodology of economics in the marginalist generation.

### What was at stake (epistemologically):

Croce charged that Pareto's mathematical economics smuggled in a hidden metaphysics: that human choice could be modeled like the trajectory of a billiard ball. Croce drew a sharp line — economics studies "the practical activities of men" as "mental facts" involving free will, and a "mechanical point of view" of these is "a contradiction in terms." Mathematizing them, said Croce, is a category error dressed in physics envy.

### Pareto's reply (in tone and substance):

- **(HE SAID, 1900, replying to Croce):** "It is useless to waste time on an *a priori* discussion of the efficacy of a method. One knows the good craftsman by his work." — Pure Pareto: methodology is settled by results, not by philosophy. Argue with the output.

- **(HE SAID, restated in later writings):** "Any science, including economics, deals with functional relations, not with metaphysical causalities." — Functions, not causes. This is the move that lets him bracket free will: he doesn't need to deny it, he just doesn't need it.

- **(OTHERS SAID, Nik Prassas on the exchange):** Pareto's rejoinders were "dismissive and impatient." He mocked those "trying to wriggle out of it" while producing nothing better. He was combative, not conciliatory.

- **(INFERENCE) Pareto's posture toward Croce was: I will not be lectured on metaphysics by a man who thinks Hegel is science.** The contempt was reciprocal. Croce thought Pareto a vulgar positivist; Pareto thought Croce a verbose idealist priest. They were both partly right.

### What it cost Pareto:

The Croce charge stuck enough that Pareto, in the *Trattato* (1916), reorganized his whole apparatus around the distinction between **logical** and **non-logical** actions, with the latter rationalized post-hoc through **derivations**. (HE SAID, Becchio's reading): this was *not* a retreat into "irrationality" — Pareto's "non-logical actions" are still given *rational explanations*, just at a meta-level. He absorbed Croce's hit without conceding the methodological point.

## Newspaper Polemics and Political Pamphlets

### The *Cronache* in *Giornale degli Economisti* (1893–1897)

Pareto wrote regular chronicles of Italian public policy. The persona is a radical-liberal scourge of the Crispi-era ruling class.

- **(HE SAID, on the Italian bourgeoisie, 1888 article):** The Italian ruling class remains indifferent to liberty's violation "provided the number of State-salaried offices placed annually at the disposition of their sons does not diminish." — A line Pareto kept making for thirty years: the bourgeoisie sells liberty for sinecures.

- **(HE SAID, *Cours*, 1896):** Other liberal economists who avoid analyzing political plunder are "amateur entomologists, who restrict themselves to only catching the most attractive butterflies." — Note the cruelty of the metaphor: not just wrong, but trivial.

### *Les Systèmes socialistes* (1902, Lausanne lectures)

Pareto's two-volume demolition of socialism is, by his standards, restrained on the Marxists and savage on their bourgeois enablers.

- **(HE SAID, *Systèmes*):** "Liberal utopians overlooked what, in their master's teachings, concerns plundering." — Bastiat's followers softened the master to avoid offending the powerful.

- **(HE SAID, *Systèmes*):** "Economic freedom cannot promise any privilege to its devotees, nor entice followers with the lure of illicit gains." — The single best-compressed Pareto explanation of *why liberalism loses*: it cannot bribe.

- **(OTHERS SAID, H. Stuart Hughes):** *Les Systèmes socialistes* "caused Lenin many a sleepless night." Whether literally true or apocryphal, this is the work that Pareto used to dismantle Marxist economics line by line — and where he simultaneously expressed something like grudging respect for socialists' moral seriousness compared to the protectionist crooks running Italy.

- **(HE SAID, on socialist leaders):** They are "an aristocracy of brigands" threatening to despoil the country — but he also said elsewhere their motives were *purer* than the rulers'. The contradiction is the point: Pareto's quarrel was with naïve enthusiasm of any flavor.

### "The Eclipse of Freedom" / "Liberty" essays (1903–1907):

- **(HE SAID, 1903):** "Liberal doctrine is optimistic, as it presupposed men can cease pillaging each other... facts shew at an advantage that, at the very least, the times are not ripe for its being made a reality."

- **(HE SAID, 1904, on hypocrisy of progressives):** "Free thinkers" demanding the punishment of professors who challenge divorce — Pareto's running joke that censorship is just as enthusiastic on the left when it suits.

- **(HE SAID, on democracy, 1900):** "It is an illusion to believe that now stands, in front of the ruling class, the people; what stands in front of it is a new... aristocracy." — His core thesis: oligarchy is not optional, only its label.

### Asides on his own youth:

- **(HE SAID, 1907):** "I was approximately sixteen when I chanced to read two authors of a completely opposite nature, Bossuet and Bastiat. I heartily disliked the first, whereas the second fully pleased my sentiments."

## Footnotes and Asides — His Real Voice in the Treatise

The *Trattato di sociologia generale* (1916) is famous among scholars for being two books: the main text (dense, systematic, in numbered paragraphs) and the footnote-and-aside apparatus (acid, mordant, full of quoted absurdities held up for ridicule). The footnotes are where Pareto's tone is least filtered.

Sample voice (HE SAID, *Trattato* / *Mind and Society*):

- "There is not a reactionary, however extreme, who dares speak ill of the god People."
- "All ruling classes have at all times identified their own interests with the 'interests of the country.'"
- "The statesman of greatest service... is the man without prejudice who knows how to profit by the prejudices of others."
- "When it is useful to them, men can believe a theory of which they know nothing more than its name."
- "Men follow their sentiments and their self-interest, but it pleases them to imagine that they follow reason."
- "Whoever becomes a lamb will find a wolf to eat him."
- "History is the graveyard of aristocracies."
- "A sign which almost invariably presages the decadence of an aristocracy is the intrusion of humanitarian feelings."
- "The strong man strikes only when absolutely necessary, and then nothing stops him."
- "The class struggle... is not confined only to two classes... it occurs between an infinite number of groups with different interests, and above all between the elites contending for power."

**(INFERENCE) The voice is consistent: he treats virtue-talk the way an entomologist treats a beetle.** The cruelty is the methodology. He is not insulting — he is *classifying*, with a barely-suppressed grin.

### The Dreyfus aside (HE SAID, from *Trattato*):

> "After the victory of the Dreyfussards, however, I was amazed to see them employing against their opponents the same villainous methods that they themselves had denounced..."

A defining moment. Pareto had been a Dreyfusard. Watching the victors of his cause turn into persecutors broke whatever was left of his liberal sentimentality. His turn to sociology is partly a turn from "this side is right" to "every side does the same thing."

### On feminism (HE SAID, *Trattato*):

> "Feminism is a disease that can only affect a wealthy people, or the wealthy part of a poor people."

Note: not "feminism is wrong" — "feminism is a *symptom of surplus*." Even his prejudices are filtered through his sociology.

## The Fascism Question (1922–1923)

This is the most-litigated chapter of Pareto's life. He died **19 August 1923**, ten months after the March on Rome, before the Matteotti murder (June 1924), before the dictatorship was consolidated. What he wrote and said in those ten months is therefore the entire record.

### The senate appointment:

- Mussolini nominated Pareto Senator of the Kingdom in 1923.
- **(OTHERS SAID, multiple sources):** Pareto never validated the appointment. Some sources say he "refused to become a ratified member"; others say the appointment lapsed because Pareto had taken Fiuman citizenship to obtain a divorce; others say he "never responded" to the invitation.
- **(HE SAID, paraphrased — possibly verbatim — in Mingardi):** "At my age and in my state of health, I can work by staying in Céligny: I could not come to Rome. And so, what is the point of being a senator?" — Note the sarcasm. He is treating a great honor as a tiresome travel imposition.

### The *Gerarchia* articles:

Mussolini's own journal *Gerarchia* (which Mussolini directed) published two Pareto articles in 1923: **"Legalità"** (April) and **"Libertà"** (July). He also published in *La Nación* of Buenos Aires.

- **(HE SAID, "Legalità", April 1923):** Preceding governments had been "incapable of using force to ensure the effectiveness of law"; this allowed Fascism to resort to violence "in its first time"; but this resort to violence must be "merely transitory" and "should end with the violent conflict that originated it." — Conditional license, with a leash.

- **(HE SAID, "Libertà", July 1923 / *Pochi punti di un futuro ordinamento costituzionale*, 1923):** "A broad freedom of the press is indispensable.... Care should be taken lest one yields to the temptation of strongly curbing it." — Pareto, *in Mussolini's own house journal*, telling Mussolini: don't shut down the press. Mingardi observes this stands in "direct contrast" to Fascist ambitions and practice.

### Private remarks:

- **(HE SAID, letter to Carlo Placci, 1 July 1923):** "It is very right that, as you say, 'we cannot live eternally in a regime of exception.'" — Quoting Placci back to Placci. Affirms that the emergency phase must end.

- **(OTHERS SAID, scholarly consensus, e.g. Mingardi, Femia):** Pareto was critical of Fascism in private letters; his public support was conditional on the regime *normalizing* — i.e. on it returning to legality and economic liberty. His "initial sympathy" has been "overestimated and probably had been manipulated by the regime." Mussolini's people made him a Senator and recruited him for the Geneva Disarmament delegation precisely because his prestige laundered the regime.

### The political-philosophy substrate (HE SAID, *Trasformazione della democrazia*, 1921):

- "Il termine democrazia è indeterminato, come molti altri termini del linguaggio volgare." (The word democracy is undefined, like many vulgar terms.)
- He describes the bourgeoisie as cowards: "si china il capo, si capitola" — "they bow their heads, they surrender." They pay the strikers to placate them; they abandon strike-breakers; they have lost the medieval nobility's courage.
- The diagnosis: "plutocrazia demagogica" — a *demagogic plutocracy* in which wealthy speculators and the masses ally to plunder the productive middle.

**(INFERENCE) Pareto's 1923 position was: the old liberal parliamentary system is dead; demagogic plutocracy is destroying Italy; Fascism, *if it stays transitional*, may break the deadlock. If it doesn't, it becomes the next sclerotic elite that will need replacing.** He died before the test ran. Calling him a fascist requires ignoring his own conditions.

## Positions He Changed Over Time

The single largest documented evolution in Pareto's life:

1. **1865–1890 (Bastiat-Spencer phase):** Free-trade liberal, anti-clerical, progressive in the 19th-century sense. Reads Bastiat at 16 and is "pleased."

2. **1890–1900 (militant economist phase):** Writes the *Cours*, contests Italian protectionism in print, runs for parliament (loses), believes liberal advocacy can win. (HE SAID, 1892: "human society progresses by distancing itself from the military type... industrial.")

3. **1900–1910 (the turn):** Dreyfusards become persecutors. Liberalism loses the cultural war. Pareto starts laughing instead of campaigning ("I laugh at their infirmity"). Begins *Les Systèmes socialistes* and the framework that becomes sociology.

4. **1910–1923 (Olympian phase):** Writes the *Trattato*. (HE SAID, 1913): defense of economic freedom was "all wasted time" — *not* because liberty is wrong, but because *operare* and *sapere* are different jobs. He retains classical-liberal conviction; he abandons activism.

5. **1922–1923 (final months):** Conditional, sarcastic, qualified engagement with Fascism. Tells Mussolini's own journal to preserve press freedom. Refuses (or fails to validate) the Senate seat.

### Other position-shifts:

- **On Pareto-optimality** — he kept refining it (the income-distribution law from 1896 onward) and absorbed early criticism; the constancy of α was modified as data accumulated.
- **On metaphysics in economics** — Croce forced him to develop logical/non-logical action distinction in response.

## Refusals — Questions or Modes of Argument He Rejected

What Pareto would not engage with:

1. **A priori method-arguments.** "One knows the good craftsman by his work." If you want to argue methodology, produce a better economics. He treated philosophy-of-science discussions as displacement activity for people who couldn't do the science.

2. **Sincerity claims in politics.** He treated *all* ideological language as a *derivation* — a post-hoc justification. He refused to argue with someone's stated reasons; he asked instead what residues those reasons were masking. This drove Croce and the Marxists mad.

3. **"The People" as a moral category.** "There is not a reactionary, however extreme, who dares speak ill of the god People." — Note "god." For Pareto, *populus* was a sacred term doing theological work, and he refused to genuflect.

4. **Progress narratives.** After 1900 he would not accept that history was going anywhere in particular. Elites circulate. Aristocracies rise and decay. There is no terminus.

5. **Honors.** Pareto called himself a pacifist who "disdained honors" — he refused decorations from the Italian state, kept his distance from Lausanne ceremony, was buried "without fanfare in a small cemetery in Céligny."

6. **(INFERENCE) Sentimental engagement with his own past.** When asked about his liberal activism he says "wasted time" — not "noble defeat," not "the work continues." He could be cold about his own younger self in a way that suggests he treated the prior Pareto as just another specimen.

---

## Final note on register

Pareto's voice is unmistakable across the corpus: **cold-clinical when describing humans collectively, warm-impatient with Pantaleoni, sarcastic-dismissive with rivals, mordant in footnotes, conditional-suspicious in his last political writings.** He is funny in a way that is never quite jokes — the humor is the diagnosis. The closest English analogue is Gibbon. The Italian comparison would be Machiavelli with worse temper and better math.
